Chapter XI. On Justification

I. Of the First and Principal Part of Communion with Christ

The primary and principal head of our communion with Christ is seated in our justification before God through Him.

Ames saith: The communion of blessings flowing from union with Christ is that whereby the faithful are made partakers of all those things which they need for living well and blessedly unto God, Ephesians 1:3, “Who hath blessed us with all spiritual blessings.” Romans 8:32,“He that spared not His own Son.” Therefore, this communion bringeth a translation and change of state to the faithful, from the state of sin and death to the state of righteousness and eternal life, 1 John 3:14, “We know that we have passed from death unto life.” This change of state is double: relative and absolute or real. The relative change of state is that which consisteth in the reputation [or reckoning] of God, Romans 4:5,“But to him that worketh not, but believeth on Him that justifieth the ungodly, his faith is counted for righteousness.” 2 Corinthians 5:19,“To wit, that God was in Christ, reconciling the world unto Himself, not imputing their trespasses unto them.”

II. Of the Term “Justification”

Justification signifieth the judicial act of God, whereby He absolveth the guilty man and reputeth [or reckoneth] him righteous.

Gomarus saith: To justify is a word not born in Latium, but in the Church, for expressing the Hebrew word Tsiddek or Hitsdik, and the Greek dikaioō; whose signification in the sacred letters is only threefold in kind. The first is of the judge, to judge righteous, in mind, Psalm 143:2; Romans 3:19-20, or in speech, Deuteronomy 25:1, absolutely, Romans 4:2; 8:33, or relatively to accusation and contrary to condemnation; the second is of the indicator, to indicate righteous, Luke 16:15, with Matthew 23:28; Revelation 22:11. The third, finally, is of the vindicator, to vindicate or liberate, Romans 6:7. The first is proper; the other two are derived from it by metaphor.

Walaeus saith: The word justification, frequently used in this matter by the Scriptures, doth not signify any infusion of righteousness or holiness, as the Romanists feign; but:

  1. To free and absolve the accused and brought into judgment.
  2. To esteem and pronounce as righteous.
  3. To attribute the praise and reward of righteousness; all which three are conjoined in this act and flow from the same fountain.

Therefore, it is a judicial word, taken by similitude from a guilty person accused before a judge and absolved by his sentence. Yet we do not deny that God also sanctifieth him whom He justifieth, that thereafter he may live according to righteousness and bring forth works of gratitude; but we assert that these are distinct benefits; and although they are always together, yet they are not the same; nor are we justified before God because of the holiness which is in us, as we shall demonstrate afterward. The arguments which are brought for the true opinion are diverse and invincible:

  1. From the use of the Hebrew word Hitsdic, Proverbs 17:15, “He that justifieth the wicked, and he that condemneth the just, even they both are abomination to the LORD.” Isaiah 5:23, “Woe unto them that justify the wicked for reward.” Ezekiel 16:51, “Thou hast justified thy sisters in all thine abominations which thou hast done.”
  2. From the Greek word dikaioō or dikaiousthai in the New Testament, Luke 7:29, “And all the people that heard Him, and the publicans, justified God, being baptized with the baptism of John.” Matthew 12:37, “For by thy words thou shalt be justified, and by thy words thou shalt be condemned.” Romans 3:4, “That Thou mightest be justified in Thy sayings, and mightest overcome when Thou art judged.”
  3. From legal justification, Romans 2:13, “For not the hearers of the law are just before God, but the doers of the law shall be justified”; where the word justification cannot be taken for the infusion of righteousness, because the doers of the law are thereby just in themselves, in that they perform the law; but they are said to be justified before God because they are esteemed just before God and receive the reward of righteousness. As a like place is extant, Romans 8:4, “That the righteousness of the law might be fulfilled in us,” that is, the reward of righteousness.
  4. From diverse places of Scripture where it treateth of justification by faith, where manifestly it is put for to absolve, and is opposed to condemnation, as Job 9:2; Psalm 143:2, “Enter not into judgment with Thy servant: for in Thy sight shall no man living be justified.” Romans 8:33, “Who shall lay any thing to the charge of God’s elect? It is God that justifieth.” 1 Corinthians 4:4, “For I know nothing by myself; yet am I not hereby justified: but He that judgeth me is the Lord.”
  5. From the addition of the particle “before Him,” Romans 2:13; Psalm 143:2, or “in His judgment.” For what would it signify that righteousness is infused before God? But to be justified before God most commonly signifieth to be absolved from guilt before the tribunal of God.
  6. From equivalent phrases whereby to justify is explained; as Luke 18:13, the publican prayeth that God would be merciful to him a sinner; which Christ, verse 14, interpreteth, “I tell you, this man went down to his house justified rather than the other”; therefore, to be justified and to have God merciful are the same. So Romans 4, for the same and equivalents are put to justify, verse 2, to impute righteousness by grace, verse 4, to forgive and cover sins, verse 7, and not to impute sins, verse 8. Likewise, 2 Corinthians 5:19, for the same he putteth to reconcile with God, and not to impute sins, and to make us the righteousness of God in Christ. Likewise, Ephesians 1:6, to make us accepted in the Beloved.
  7. Because this benefit of justification is distinguished throughout in Scripture from sanctification, as in the Epistle to the Romans, where in the first five chapters it treateth of our justification, in the three following of sanctification, and 1 Corinthians 1:30, Christ is said distinctly to be made unto us justification and sanctification. As also 1 Corinthians 6:11, we are said to be justified and sanctified in Him.

III. Particular and Universal Justification

The justification of the person is one particular, of the cause; another universal, of the person.

Gomarus saith: The justification of God from its object is either particular or universal. The former is that whereby He judgeth a particular righteousness, an act, Deuteronomy 24:13; Psalm 106:31; James 2:21, with Genesis 22:12, or a controversial matter, 2 Chronicles 6:23; the latter, whereby He judgeth the person, with universal righteousness, righteous, Psalm 143:2; Galatians 2:6.

IV. Legal and Evangelical Justification

The justification of the person is one legal, another evangelical; and after we all have fallen from that, the faithful are preserved by this.

Junius saith: Therefore, as there is a double promise of righteousness and salvation: one in the covenant of works, whereby we acknowledge that God is good, but to those who satisfy the law; the other in the covenant of grace, whose condition is found outside us in Christ; so there is a double application of righteousness, which is called justification. The first is legal, which is rendered plainly null, not from the fault of the law, whose promises are neither uncertain nor fallacious, but because of the weakness of the flesh, Romans 8:3; Galatians 3:17. The other is evangelical, whereby God reputeth the man terrified with the sense of sin, Matthew 11:28, and fleeing unto grace, only by philanthropia, Titus 3:4, his sins being remitted, righteous, free from condemnation, and heir of eternal life, because of the imputation of the righteousness of Christ apprehended by true faith. More briefly, Oecumenius, in Romans 3, “It is absolution and liberation from sins, from which the law itself neither would nor could absolve.”

V. The Definition of Evangelical Justification

And the evangelical is the gracious sentence of God, whereby He absolveth men guilty in themselves of eternal curse, for Christ’s sake, in whom they believe, and pronounceth them righteous.

Trelcatius saith: Therefore, justification is properly the gratuitous judicial action of God, whereby He judgeth the elect, obnoxious in themselves to the accusation and curse of the law, through faith, for Christ’s sake, by the imputation of His righteousness, righteous, unto the praise of the glory of His grace and their salvation, Romans 3:24-25.

Ames saith: Justification is the gracious sentence of God, whereby for Christ apprehended by faith He absolveth the faithful from sin and death, and reputeth him righteous unto life, Romans 3:22,24, “Even the righteousness of God which is by faith of Jesus Christ unto all and upon all them that believe, that they might be justified freely by His grace through the redemption that is in Christ Jesus.”

VI. Of Active and Passive Justification

It is considered either actively, in God; or passively, terminated and received by faith in man.

Trelcatius saith: Justification is taken either actively with respect to God, who justifieth; or passively, with respect to man, who is justified.

Maccovius saith: Active justification signifieth the absolution of God, whereby He absolveth the guilty man from guilt, because of the satisfaction of Christ, and reputeth him righteous, because of His righteousness imputed. That this differeth from passive justification appeareth first hence, that it is done by one indivisible act; but passive justification, which consisteth in the application of the righteousness of Christ, is not so, as when as often as we sin, we apply the righteousness of Christ unto ourselves. Hence also in the Lord’s Prayer we are commanded daily to pray that God would forgive us our sins. Second, that active precedeth faith, passive followeth, as that which is through faith; for through faith we receive the remission of sins, and a lot among the saints, Acts 26:18. And that active precedeth faith is proven:

  1. Because every object is prior to its act, since this dependeth from that.
  2. Because through the act of believing, as Paraeus teacheth in De Justificatione, Book 1, Chapter 10, page 257, the remission of sins is not effected, but received.
  3. Because through the satisfaction of Christ we not only attain justification, but also faith itself, and repentance, that is, the circumcision of the heart; for God blesseth us with all spiritual blessings in Christ, Ephesians 1:3, even before faith and repentance the satisfaction of Christ is applied unto us, as that for which we attain effectual grace to believe in Christ and to do repentance. This same teacheth Daniel Tostanus, once the most learned theologian of the Academy of Heidelberg, in an epistle (the exemplar of which Lucius, professor of Basel, published) to Vorstius, whose words are these: “Thou confoundest, O Vorstius, the acquisition of justification and the very deletion of sin, which was made by the blood of Christ, with its application. Therefore, thou feignest to thyself a contradiction where there is no contradiction; for all the elect are justified in Christ, if thou respect His merit, even before they are born; and so before we believe, we were justified and redeemed in Christ; but afterward He giveth faith unto His elect, whereby they seek His righteousness in Christ alone.” Thus far Tostanus. Passive justification is that whereby one is absolved from guilt and is esteemed righteous, or rather the reception of absolution from guilt and of the imputation of the righteousness of Christ. See Keckermann in Systema Theologicum and Trelcatius in Loci Communes.

Ames saith: The sentence of justification was:

  1. As it were conceived in the mind of God, through the decree of justifying, Galatians 3:8, “The Scripture, foreseeing that God would justify the heathen through faith.”
  2. It was pronounced in Christ our Head, now risen from the dead, 2 Corinthians 5:19, “To wit, that God was in Christ, reconciling the world unto Himself, not imputing their trespasses unto them.”
  3. It is virtually pronounced from that first relation which ariseth from ingenerate faith, Romans 8:1, “There is therefore now no condemnation to them which are in Christ Jesus.”
  4. It is expressly pronounced through the Spirit of God testifying to our spirits our reconciliation with God, Romans 5:5, “And hope maketh not ashamed; because the love of God is shed abroad in our hearts by the Holy Ghost which is given unto us.” In this testimony of the Spirit, justification itself doth not so properly consist as the actual perception of it previously granted, through the act of faith as it were reflex.

Spanheim saith: We esteem the form of active justification to be both the plenary remission of sins and the imputation of the righteousness of Christ, nor do we think that these phrases are either to be equalized or confounded. We constitute the form of passive justification in the apprehension and sense of both in capable subjects.

VII. Justification Before God: Not by Works, but by Christ’s Imputed Righteousness

Man is justified before God not because of his works, or charity, obedience, or even faith itself, but because of the sole righteousness of Christ imputed to the man receiving it by faith.

Luther saith: This is the mystery of God, astonishing His saints, which is not only impossible to the understanding of the wicked, but also wonderful and difficult to the belief of the godly themselves. For nature, corrupted and blinded by the vice of original sin, cannot imagine or conceive any justification beyond and above works. Hence that strife of the hypocrites against the faithful concerning justification, to be decided by the judgment of God alone.

Melanchthon saith: And in sum, when we are accused with this dogma, that we say that man is justified by faith, we are accused only for this, that we say that we receive reconciliation because of the Son of God, not because of our own dignity, and that this is to be believed, or that this benefit is to be apprehended by this faith or confidence, and the merit of Christ is to be opposed to our sin and to our condemnation, and that in this faith or confidence looking upon the Son of God, God is to be invoked. But that these opinions are the very voice of the Gospel and the perpetual consent of the true Church is most certain.

Junius saith: We absolutely remove from the cause or act of justifying not only all our works and merits, or those of others, but also faith itself insofar as it is considered as a work or quality. That is, we deny that we are justified by its virtue or merit, but only correlatively (as they speak), insofar as it apprehendeth the merit of Christ, as the hand of a beggar the alms, and that therefore the untainted glory may remain to the Lord, and true peace to our consciences. And therefore, we exclude charity and other virtues from the act of justifying, not that faith is alone, that is, idle and dead, but that it justifieth alone instrumentally and metonymically.

Professor saith: The matter of justification kata arsin, our works are denied to be. For we are not justified by the law, in the law, from the law, through the law, Galatians 3:11; 2:21, not by the law of Moses, Acts 13:39, not from works, Romans 4:2; Ephesians 2:9, not by the works of the law, Romans 3:20; 4:2; Galatians 2:16, not by the law of righteousness, Romans 9:31, not by our righteousness which is of the law, Philippians 3:9, not by our own righteousness, Romans 10:3, not from works which are in righteousness, which we have done, Titus 3:5. In like manner, no one is justified, Romans 3:20; Galatians 2:16, not Abraham, the father of the faithful, Romans 4:2, nor David, a man after God’s own heart, Romans 4:6, nor Paul, that chosen vessel, even if he were conscious of nothing, 1 Corinthians 4:4; Philippians 3:8-9, or any of the saints, Psalm 143:2, but those at last who justify themselves, but falsely, Luke 10:29; 16:15. And antithetōsit is done without works, Romans 4:6, without the works of the law, Romans 3:28, by which all legal righteousness, of whatsoever kind, and of whomsoever, and all boasting, are excluded from justification, Romans 3:27; 1 Corinthians 1:31. Kata thesin, it is affirmed that we are justified freely and by the grace of God, if we respect ourselves, Romans 3:24. Which in some measure supplieth the reason of matter; and it is opposed to the debt and merit of our works, Romans 4:4-5; 11:6. So that not only without merits, but also against our demerits, it is given unto us, Romans 3:23; 8:4-5; Ephesians 2:8. And indeed by the righteousness of God, Romans 1:17; 3:24-25; 2 Corinthians 5:21,which is without the law, Romans 3:21, the righteousness from God, 1 Corinthians 1:30, from God, Philippians 3:9, namely, not that whereby God is just in Himself, but that which He hath prepared for us, giveth, and imputeth, Romans 5:15, directly opposed to the righteousness of the law, and to ours or proper, Jeremiah 23:6; Romans 3:21-22; 10:3; 2 Corinthians 5:21; Philippians 3:9. And that is of Christ, who is our righteousness, Jeremiah 23:6; 33:16, made unto us by God righteousness, 1 Corinthians 1:30, the righteousness of God in Christ, 2 Corinthians 5:21, in whom we are justified, Galatians 2:17, and in the name of the Lord Jesus, 1 Corinthians 6:11, through Christ, Romans 5:11, through the grace of Jesus Christ, Acts 15:11, through the redemption made through Christ, Romans 3:24, in the blood of Christ, Romans 5:9. Which is that very righteousness from the satisfaction of the Son of God, considered in its amplitude, both passive and active, as hypakoē, obedience, is opposed to disobedience; and that whereby we are justified is opposed to the law and to ours or inherent, Philippians 3:9.

Walaeus saith: Certain Remonstrants, as Bertius, Vorstius, Arminius, will that faith itself is imputed unto us for righteousness. For because the covenant of the law could not save man because of the weakness of the flesh, they will that God entered into another covenant with men, evangelical, whereby the obedience of faith is required, and therefore that faith, as a part of due obedience, is imputed unto us for righteousness; as if a debtor be not solvent, the creditor entereth into another compact with him, whereby, some things being remitted, he pay some part thereof. This opinion agreeth not first with their own hypotheses; second, it fighteth with Scripture and with the glory of Christ. It agreeth not with their hypotheses; because they will that a more perfect obedience is required from us in the Gospel than was exacted in the old law; for this Socinus clearly showeth in his book De Officio Hominis Christiani, and the Remonstrants in their Confession. For first they treat of the explanation of the Decalogue, then they subjoin certain other evangelical precepts; and then at last they treat of justification and sanctification. That I may be silent that they establish such perfection in the third kind of the regenerate. See chapter 10 of the Confession, whence it would follow that a much heavier yoke is imposed upon us than was upon the Israelites themselves, against the express words of Christ, Matthew 11:29-30, “Take My yoke upon you, and learn of Me; for I am meek and lowly in heart: and ye shall find rest unto your souls. For My yoke is easy, and My burden is light.” And Acts 15:10-11. But that it fighteth with the truth, I thus demonstrate:

  1. Because grace and works, as causes of our justification, cannot be conjoined; as Romans 11:6, “And if by grace, then is it no more of works: otherwise grace is no more grace.” But on the contrary, therefore by faith, that it may be by grace, that the promise may be sure, Romans 4:16. Therefore, faith is not considered as a work in this matter, nor do works justify us.
  2. Because faith is opposed to works in the matter of justification; as Romans 4:5, “But to him that worketh not, but believeth on Him that justifieth the ungodly, his faith is counted for righteousness.” So Galatians 2:16, “Knowing that a man is not justified by the works of the law, but by the faith of Jesus Christ, even we have believed in Jesus Christ, that we might be justified by the faith of Christ, and not by the works of the law.”
  3. Because Scripture attributeth perfect satisfaction and payment only to the merit of Christ; therefore, our faith is not any part of that price. The antecedent is proven from many places, of which before. See Matthew 20:28, “The Son of man came to give His life a ransom for many.” Likewise, Romans 3:25, “Whom God hath set forth to be a propitiation through faith in His blood, to declare His righteousness for the remission of sins that are past.” Likewise, 2 Corinthians 5:21; Hebrews 1:3.
  4. If this were true, then Christ rather died for faith, that He might acquire for it the dignity of justifying, not for us; and since one faith is more imperfect, another more perfect, there would be need of multiple imputation: first, whereby imperfect faith is for perfect; second, whereby perfect faith is reputed for the whole obedience of the law. All which are unheard of in the Scriptures.

First Objection: Faith is called the work of God, John 6:29.

Response: Because God effecteth this in us and requireth it from us. But yet not as a work doth it justify.

Second Objection: Faith is said to be imputed for righteousness, Romans 4:5. But that which is perfect, as is the obedience of Christ, is not properly said to be reputed for righteousness.

Response:

  1. It is rightly answered by ours that faith is here considered relatively and instrumentally; as if one should say that a ring, in which a union of immense price is enclosed, enricheth someone.
  2. It is false that what is perfect is not imputed; for sins also are imputed, which are truly sins.

Third Objection: But Romans 4:3, it is written, “Abraham believed God, and it was counted unto him for righteousness,” namely, that believing.

Response: That believing, as faith, namely, considered relatively, insofar as it respected that blessed seed, and as it were included it.

The Romanists establish that faith indeed justifieth, but neither alone, nor as it is the instrument whereby the righteousness of Christ is apprehended, but dispositively and radically, that is, that faith is the first of virtues through which man is disposed unto future justification, that is, the infusion of charity, which the remission of sins accompanieth. Nor that faith alone performeth this office, although it be first, but also fear, contrition, imperfect acts of hope and love, the vow and desire of the sacrament of baptism, etc. Holy Scripture, in places where is the proper seat of this matter, expressly excludeth works from the causes of justification. As Romans 3:28, “Therefore we conclude that a man is justified by faith without the deeds of the law.” And verse 27, “Where is boasting then? It is excluded. By what law? of works? Nay: but by the law of faith.” So Romans 4:5, “But to him that worketh not, but believeth on Him that justifieth the ungodly, his faith is counted for righteousness.” Galatians 2:16; Ephesians 2:8; Titus 3:5, etc.

Objection: But James 2:24 expressly saith that by faith alone man is not justified.

Response: There James treateth, verse 14, of the profession or boasting of faith, which alone sufficeth not for the demonstration of righteousness. And that he treateth of such profession of faith is proven:

  1. Because he saith, “What doth it profit, my brethren, though a man say he hath faith, and have not works?”
  2. Because he compareth it with the profession of charity, which is without beneficence, verses 15-17.
  3. Because he compareth it with the faith which the devils have, who tremble from the knowledge of God; but this without works he saith is dead, and therefore doth not justify us, that is, prove and demonstrate us righteous and justified. But faith joined with works proveth us righteous before God and men:
    1. He showeth by the example of Abraham, when he offered his son, Genesis 22:12, who yet long before was justified by faith before God, Genesis 15:6, and therefore of this justification it cannot be understood. Second, because God Himself saith, “Now I know that thou fearest God,” Genesis 22:12. Therefore, Abraham by this deed of his demonstrated before God and men that he had accepted the promises of God by true faith; and therefore the Scripture was fulfilled, that is, was proven to be fulfilled, “Abraham believed God, and it was imputed unto him for righteousness.”
    2. The example of Rahab maketh to the same, that he may show that not only the Jews, sprung from Abraham, but also the Gentiles inserted into his family, are justified in the same manner. Then James treateth of dead faith, verse 20, and such as the devils also have, verse 19, from which he taketh away justification.

Objection: But yet he saith that it is made perfect by works, verse 22.

Response: That is, is demonstrated to be perfect, or is perfected unto the demonstration of justification; as he speaketh, verse 18, “Show me thy faith by thy works.”

Objection: But verse 21, it is said, “Abraham was justified by works when he offered his son.”

Response: The word “to justify” here must necessarily be taken otherwise than in Romans 4:2, because there it is denied that Abraham was justified by works, namely, to be shown and proven righteous; as verse 18, he said, “Show me thy faith,” and Genesis 22:12, God saith, “Now I know that thou fearest God.” Therefore, Abraham by this deed of his demonstrated before God and men that he had accepted the promises of God by true faith; and therefore the Scripture was fulfilled, that is, was proven to be fulfilled, “Abraham believed God, and it was imputed unto him for righteousness.”

But that the righteousness of Christ is imputed unto us, which the Romanists pertinaciously deny, is proven by the following arguments:

  1. In justification, some righteousness is necessarily required whereby we are righteous before God. But that is not righteousness inherent in us; therefore, it is alien righteousness. But there is no other than that of Christ; because there is no other name given under heaven among men whereby we must be saved, Acts 4:12. The minor was invincibly demonstrated before. Therefore, the conclusion is certain and evident.
  2. If we are justified by the blood and death of Christ, then the righteousness of Christ is imputed unto us for justification. The antecedent is of the Apostle, Romans 3:25, “Whom God hath set forth to be a propitiation through faith in His blood, to declare His righteousness for the remission of sins that are past.” And chapter 5:9, “Being now justified by His blood.”
  3. In the manner whereby the righteousness of Christ is made ours, our sins were laid upon Christ. But our sins were laid upon Christ not otherwise than by imputation, that is, that because of our sins imputed to Him, He was punished. Therefore, etc. The major of this syllogism is of Scripture, Galatians 3:13, “Christ hath redeemed us from the curse of the law, being made a curse for us.” 2 Corinthians 5:21, “For He hath made Him to be sin for us, who knew no sin; that we might be made the righteousness of God in Him.” Isaiah 53:6, “The LORD hath laid on Him the iniquity of us all.” And Psalm 69:5, “Then I restored that which I took not away.” And throughout in the New Testament, He is said to have borne our sins. The minor is beyond controversy; because it could not be that our sins were laid upon Him save by imputation, since He is in Himself holy, innocent, and separate from sinners.
  4. If we are righteous in Christ, 2 Corinthians 5:21; Philippians 3:9, made accepted, kecharitōmenoi in Christ, Ephesians 1:6, beloved in Christ, Romans 8:39, yea, and our works acceptable, Hebrews 13:21, and hypodekta, 1 Peter 2:5, in Christ; then certainly the righteousness of Christ, whereby we are gracious and accepted, and whereby also our works please God, is made ours by imputation. But the antecedent is manifest in the places aforementioned; therefore, etc.
  5. The fifth reason is taken from places where it is said that righteousness is imputed unto us, Romans 4:11, and faith is imputed for righteousness, verse 5, which cannot otherwise be understood than of the righteousness of Christ, which faith respecteth and as it were includeth. Whence also Romans 8:3, “For what the law could not do,” etc.

VIII. Imputation of Christ’s Active and Passive Righteousness

Unto us is imputed the righteousness of Christ, both active and passive, the obedience of life and of death.

Trelcatius saith: For the obedience of Christ is double, opposed to the double obligation of man after the fall: active for guilt, passive for punishment, or both for both. Active obedience is the perfect performance of the divine law, which Christ fully and perfectly executed unto the last jot of the law, 1 Corinthians 1:30; Romans 5:19; 2 Corinthians 5:21. The necessity of this in the work of redemption and of our justification is proven by three things: the righteousness of God, the office of the Mediator, and our salvation. The righteousness of God, whether thou respect His nature, whereby He is infinitely just, He ought not to save man save through this manner of righteousness, Proverbs 17:15; Exodus 20:5, whether proper to us or gratuitously imputed; or His will revealed in the law, which is the immovable rule of righteousness, hath prescribed no other way unto life than obedience. The office of Christ the Mediator; for since as our surety, by voluntary dispensation, He was bound to undergo and perform what we ourselves were necessarily bound to undergo and perform, it was necessary not only to undergo death for us, but also to perform the law, because we were bound to both, Romans 8:3; Galatians 4:4-5. Our salvation; for since two things were required for it, liberation from death and donation of life, it was necessary that that be obtained by the expiation of sin, this by the gift of righteousness, Romans 3:24-25.

Gomarus saith: Since universal righteousness is two-membered, namely, the compensation of anomia, or unrighteousness from the law, through the lytron of sufficient punishment, that satisfaction may be made to the transgression of the law and to the anathema of the curse, Deuteronomy 27:26; Romans 6:23, and the payment of universal eunomia, or perfect righteousness (of nature and life) due to the precepts of the law, Leviticus 18:5; Romans 2:13; 10:5; Matthew 22:37,39; so the righteousness of Christ the Redeemer, performed for us, comprehendeth these two parts (distinct in nature, not in time), Psalm 40:8; 2 Corinthians 5:21; Matthew 3:15; Romans 10:4; Galatians 4:4; Philippians 2:8. Whence rightly, by the Prophet, treating of the gifts of Christ, both are coupled, the deletion of iniquity and the bringing in of everlasting righteousness, Daniel 9:24, and Christ is the holiness of the LORD (foreshadowed by the title of the frontlet of Aaron, commanded for expiating and reconciling the Israelites, Exodus 28:36,38), the Sun of righteousness, Malachi 4:2, the LORD our righteousness, Jeremiah 23:6; 33:16, and the true Melchizedek, that is, king of righteousness, is called, Hebrews 7:1-3.

Maccovius saith: Christ, from the time He was given as Mediator for us, took upon Him our debts to pay them, that is, both the debt of punishment, into which we had incurred because of our sins, and the debt of duty, which ought to have been performed by us, if we were to be made partakers of eternal life, and this sponsorship was accepted by God. Therefore, as Christ is He who in our stead underwent the curse of the law, so also Christ is He who in our stead fulfilled the law.

Spanheim saith: There is the highest consent among the Orthodox, although some dissensus intercedeth in the explanation of the material cause, some urging both the active and passive obedience, and conjoining satisfaction through obedience and through punishment, others seeking the material cause of our justification in satisfaction through punishment alone. The foundation is safe between both. For both admit and also require the obedience of Christ perfectly performed to the law for our justification, but they differ in the manner of explaining the thing; for some have it for the meritorious cause of our justification, others for the condition of the person, and the cause without which justification is not to be merited, such as befitted us, etc., Hebrews 7:26. Concerning which, with the unity of faith and charity saved, there is some dissensus of opinions, since each part apprehendeth the whole and only Christ, and in that whole and only seeketh righteousness and life.

IX. Of the Careful Preservation of This Doctrine

The doctrine of justification must be carefully preserved in the Church of God, the errors of adversaries being rejected.

Spanheim saith: This orthodox doctrine concerning the justification of sinful man before God, asserted from the Word of God, the errors fall:

  1. Of the Libertines:
    1. Who fabricate diverse manners of justifying, diverse ladders into heaven, another for the Gentiles, another for the Jews, finally another for the Christians.
    2. Who draw gratuitous justification in Christ into an argument of license.
  2. Of the Romanists, who interpret justification as justifaction, not justihabition, urge inherent righteousness, infect imputed righteousness as putative; confound the righteousness of the cause and of the person, legal and evangelical, and weave a patchwork from both, and therefore divide the matter of justification between God and man, grace and free will. Who dispense justification into first and second, to the former they prestruct preparations and merits of congruity, to the latter they substern merits of condignity. Who confound justifying faith with other species of faith; deny that it is fiducial apprehension of Christ, establish it separable not only from actual but also from habitual charity, contend that it justifieth only initially and inchoatively, but that good works justify completively. Who deny either the perfection, or the stability, or the sense of justification in the justified, and so concenturiate whole carts of errors.
  3. Of the Socinians:
    1. Who take away justification through and because of Christ the Mediator and His lytron, admit no redemption and satisfaction of Christ save metaphorical.
    2. Who contend that Christ is our Savior only because of His prophetical and kingly office, as who claimed this title to Himself, only by announcing salvation, confirming and communicating it, but not by meriting it.

These and other errors of this kind are monstrous and adverse to Holy Scripture, and to the saving doctrine of justification drawn and derived from it.

X. Of Adoption as Sons of God

With justification, the faithful are also adopted into the sons of God.

Peter Martyr saith: This place seemeth to require that something also be said concerning the adoption of the sons of God. The Jurisconsults, as is had in the Institutes, define adoption to be a legitimate act, imitating nature, found for the solace of those who have no children आणिdistinguish adoption from arrogation. For arrogation, they say, is when one who is sui juris is received into the place of a son. But adoption, when one who is under another’s power is adopted. It is also provided by the laws that an elder be not adopted by a younger. For it seemed like a monster that a son should exceed the father in age. And therefore, Cicero often gravely exagitates that adoption of Clodius. Now God adopteth His elect unto Himself, not as if He had not another son; for He had Christ the only-begotten, in whom He was well pleased; but because in the whole human nature He had as yet no sons. For through Adam we all were alienated from Him. Wherefore, God for that cause sent His natural and legitimate Son into the world, that through Him He might co-opt many others from our race as sons unto Himself. But this is not wont to be done commonly; for those who have an only son seek not others unto themselves; yea, they rejoice that that their son is not compelled to share the inheritance with brethren. But so great was the charity of God and of Christ toward us that He willed to adopt us, although unworthy, unto so great dignity. Nor is that heavenly inheritance of such nature that, being communicated to many, it is diminished.

Ames saith: Adoption is the gracious sentence of God, whereby He accepteth the faithful because of Christ unto the dignity of sons, John 1:12, “But as many as received Him, to them gave He power to become the sons of God, even to them that believe on His name.” It is called the gracious sentence of God because it manifesteth the gracious will of God toward men, 1 John 3:1, “Behold, what manner of love the Father hath bestowed upon us, that we should be called the sons of God.” This sentence is pronounced with the same variety of degrees as justification: for it was first in the divine predestination, Ephesians 1:5, “Having predestinated us unto the adoption of children.” Afterward in Christ, Galatians 4:4-5, “But when the fulness of the time was come, God sent forth His Son, made of a woman, made under the law, to redeem them that were under the law, that we might receive the adoption of sons.” Afterward in the faithful themselves, verse 6, “And because ye are sons, God hath sent forth the Spirit of His Son into your hearts, crying, Abba, Father.” It is properly conversant about the faithful called and justified, John 1:12; for by adoption we are not constituted righteous, which would necessarily follow if adoption were a part of justification, as some will; neither is it the calling unto Christ, but a certain sublime dignity flowing from His application, Romans 8:17, “And if children, then heirs; heirs of God, and joint-heirs with Christ.” Yet calling and justification have the reason of a foundation to that relation of adoption; for through faith and the righteousness of faith the right of adoption is acquired, John 1:12. But adoption, although it followeth from faith, yet it followeth not so immediately that justification doth not intercede; for adoption by its nature requireth and presupposeth that reconciliation which is found in justification. Hence all the faithful expect eternal life by a double title, as it were: by the title of redemption, which they have from justification; and by the title as it were of sonship, which they have from adoption. Which yet is so to be understood that the title of redemption is the foundation of this right; and adoption addeth a certain manner of excellency and dignity to the same.

XI. Of Christian Liberty

The justified in Christ enjoy true and evangelical liberty.

Junius saith: The parts of Christian liberty are three: manumission from the curse of the law, from the servitude of sin, and from the yoke of legal ceremonies and of things in themselves indifferent. The first part, namely, manumission from the curse of the law, is seated in this, that the consciences of the true faithful are free and immune from the severity and rigor of the law, Romans 8:1; Galatians 3:10-11; 5:1,5,which denounceth the curse to all who have transgressed even in one jot, James 2:10; Galatians 3:10, from Deuteronomy 27:26, neither conferreth salvation upon any unless he have plainly and fully kept all the commandments, Romans 10:5; Ezekiel 20:11. And therefore, as long as that rigor obtaineth dominion and flourisheth, all men universally are shut up under condemnation and excluded from the hope of eternal inheritance, since there is none who can in any wise satisfy the requirements of the law from himself. But that being taken away, the law is not superfluous to the faithful; for its doctrine remaineth immovable and immutable, which aimeth at this, that it show the way and manner whereby they may conform their life to the will of God, yet with a conscience free and pacified before His tribunal. The second part of Christian liberty is immunity from the servitude of reigning sin, so that it no longer exerciseth its tyranny in the faithful, John 8:34-36; Romans 6:14,17-18, who have addicted themselves into subjection to righteousness, delight in the law of God, and obey His will; not as compelled by the command of the law, but with a free and pacified spirit, because of the Spirit of regeneration inhabiting in them. Whence Ambrose, Book 2, Epistle 7, “He is free to whom sin is forgiven, no longer drawing the chain of sin.” Hence the pious, for their consolation, certainly persuade themselves that their obsequies, yea, even their endeavors only of instituting a better life, please the most good God, Malachi 3:17, and therefore gird themselves more clearly and promptly to His true worship, 1 John 5:2-3. The third part is manumission from the yoke of legal ceremonies, because the truly faithful, the ceremonies being abrogated by the advent and death of Christ, are liberated from the obligation of observing them, Ephesians 2:15; Colossians 2:14,16, and therefore also in general their consciences can be bound by no necessity from external things in themselves indifferent. But things indifferent are called those which by their own nature are neither good nor evil, and therefore such as one can either well, or ill, or not at all use, Matthew 15:11; Romans 14:14,20,22; 1 Corinthians 6:12-13; 8:8; 10:23,25,27; Colossians 2:16-17, the use of which is adiaphoron to those who are not yet certain of their liberty, and therefore hesitate, doubt, or labor under some superstitious opinion in the use, it is not conceded, because what they do, they do not from faith, Romans 14:5,14,22-23. But this liberty concerning things indifferent hath not place always and everywhere, but can and ought to be coerced, both in general and in species. In general, through the law of charity, which is catholic, and requireth that regard be had to the weak brethren, who are ruder in the doctrine of the Gospel and not sufficiently instructed concerning the prerogative of their liberty, Romans 14:1,13,15; 15:1-2; 1 Corinthians 8:9,13; 9:12,22; 10:24,28; Galatians 5:13, lest anything in itself indifferent be done whereby they be destroyed, or not be done whereby they be edified. But that it may appear what ought to be done or not to be done, it is always to be judged from the Word of God; for as charity judgeth what profiteth, so faith determineth what is lawful. But it is to be known that although we accommodate ourselves to our neighbor, yet liberty remaineth unimpaired, only the use is restrained, 1 Corinthians 9:4,15,19,22; for liberty is one thing, the use of liberty another. For liberty is in the conscience and respecteth God; but use is in external things and hath business not with God alone, but with men, among whom not all things are expedient, although all things are lawful, since liberty is not to be used unless it edify, 1 Corinthians 6:12; 10:23; Romans 14:19; 1 Corinthians 10:27-28. But in species, the use of things indifferent is coerced through constitution, which is double: political and ecclesiastical. For although properly God alone bindeth consciences, yet the magistrate, when he commandeth or forbiddeth something in itself indifferent for the good of the commonwealth, and the Church for the sake of syntaxis and edification establisheth some laws concerning things indifferent, Acts 15:28-29, no one can resist them without sin by the study of rebellion; and this is what Paul saith, Romans 13:5, “Wherefore ye must needs be subject, not only for wrath, but also for conscience sake,” which suffereth damage by our rebellion; nor yet in such case is the liberty of conscience properly impeded, since not it, but the external work is bound.

Calvin saith: But it is diligently to be observed that Christian liberty in all its parts is a spiritual thing; whose whole force is seated in pacifying fearful consciences before God, whether they be disquieted and solicitous concerning the remission of sins, or anxious whether imperfect works polluted with the vices of our flesh please God, or tormented concerning the use of things indifferent. Wherefore, they interpret perversely, either who pretext it for their lusts, that they may abuse the good gifts of God unto their lust, or who think that there is no liberty unless it be used before men; and therefore, in using it, have no regard to the weak brethren. In the first kind, there is sinning in this age in a greater manner; there is scarcely any to whom it is lawful to be sumptuous according to his faculties, whom luxurious splendor in the apparatus of suppers, in the culture of the body, in building houses, doth not delight; who would not be eminent among others by every kind of dainties; who doth not wonderfully flatter himself in his own splendor. And all these are defended under the pretext of Christian liberty. They say they are things indifferent; I confess; provided one use them indifferently. But where they are too greedily desired, where they are proudly boasted, where they are luxuriously poured out, it is certain that those things which otherwise were lawful are defiled by these vices.
