CHAPTER VIII. CONCERNING THE COVENANT

I. For the Sum of God’s Dealings with Men, It Pleased Him to Enter into a Covenant with Them: The First Was of the Law, the Second of the Gospel; That of Works, This of Grace; The Former Struck in Adam, the Latter Established in Christ.

Maccovius: A covenant betwixt God and men is a pact whereby God, of His own free will, doth promise some good unto men and, in return, bindeth them to perform those duties which He requireth of them. Its parts be twain: the promise of some good from God’s side, and the stipulation of duty from man’s side. For God doth freely promise some good, and again demandeth from us the duty to be rendered unto Him. And it is twofold: the covenant of works and the covenant of grace.

The covenant of works is that wherein God promiseth eternal life unto man, if he render obedience unto the law of works in all points perfect, with the threat of eternal death annexed if he fail to yield such perfect obedience. This covenant God did establish at the beginning with the first of mankind, Adam and Eve, in their state of primal integrity, as it is written, Genesis 2:17. And its sacraments were two: the Tree of Life and the Tree of the Knowledge of Good and Evil. From this covenant man fell straightway after creation through disobedience, becoming a breaker of the covenant and a liar, and was made subject unto death—spiritual, corporal, and eternal. This same covenant God did renew through Moses with the people of Israel, Exodus 19:5; Deuteronomy 5:2.

There followeth the covenant of grace, wherein God promiseth unto us that He will be our God freely, for Christ’s sake, and we, in turn, are bound to be His people.

Ames: The law, or covenant of God with man in creation, was this: “Do this, and thou shalt live; if thou do it not, thou shalt die the death.” In which words are contained:

  1. The precept: “Do this.”
  2. The promise joined thereto: “If thou do it, thou shalt live.”
  3. The like threat: “If thou do it not, thou shalt die the death.”

The former covenant being broken, the second is called in the Scriptures the new covenant, Hebrews 8:8, 10; the covenant of life, salvation, and grace, Romans 4:16; Galatians 3:18. In the same sense, it is also named the Gospel, Romans 1:16; the good word of God, Hebrews 6:5; the faithful saying and worthy of all acceptation, 1 Timothy 1:15; the good doctrine, 1 Timothy 4:6; the word of life, Philippians 2:16; the word of reconciliation, 2 Corinthians 5:19; the Gospel of peace, Ephesians 2:17; 6:15; the Gospel of salvation, and the word of truth, Ephesians 1:13; the arm of God, Isaiah 53:1; the savor of life unto life, 2 Corinthians 2:16. It is called a covenant because it is a firm promise; for in the Scriptures, any steadfast statute, even concerning things without life, is termed a covenant, Jeremiah 33:20, 25: “My covenant of the day, and my covenant of the night; if my covenant be not with day and night, if I have not appointed the ordinances of heaven and earth.” Yet, seeing it consisteth in a free gift and is confirmed by the death of the giver, it is more properly called a testament than a covenant, Hebrews 9:16. This distinction, not found in the former, meaneth that the first is not so fitly called a testament as a covenant.

This new covenant differeth from the old in many ways:

  1. In kind: For that was, as it were, a covenant of friendship betwixt the Creator and the creature; but this is one of reconciliation betwixt enemies.
  2. In the efficient cause: For in that, it was an agreement of two parties, namely God and man; but in this, God alone maketh the covenant. For man, being now dead in sins, had no power to enter into a spiritual covenant with God. Yet if two parties be considered for the covenant’s nature, then God alone is the party assuming and establishing, and man the party assumed.
  3. In the object: For that extended unto all men; but this pertaineth unto certain particular ones by a special regard. Though its proclamation be oft set forth broadly after the manner of men, yet by a peculiar right it belongeth and is directed unto those for whom God intendeth it, who are therefore called the children and heirs of this promise and salvation, Genesis 15; Acts 2:39; 3:25; Romans 4:16; 9:7-8; Galatians 3:21, 29. Even as to the promulgation and outward confederation, many are strangers, Ephesians 2:12.
  4. In the principle or moving cause: For there, God acted by command from His wise and just counsel; but here, mercy alone hath place. There, indeed, some measure of grace shone forth in the appointing of the reward due to obedience, yet it was not properly guided by grace, and so not that, but this covenant was perfected—that is, it actually brought man unto blessedness.
  5. In the foundation: Which in the former was man’s own strength, but in this, Christ Jesus.
  6. In the matter or goods promised: For in that, God promised life only; but in this, righteousness also, and all the means of life, for to man now dead, not the continuation or perfection of life, but its very restoration was needful.
  7. In the conditions: For that required perfect obedience of works, to be rendered by man’s own power afore any effect of the promise, that it might have the nature of merit toward it; but this demandeth no condition properly so called or antecedent, but only a subsequent or intermediate one, and that to be given by grace, that it may be a means of the same grace being perfected—which is the proper nature of faith.
  8. In the effects: For that teacheth and showeth what is just; but this bestoweth righteousness itself. In that was the dead letter, deadly to the sinner; but in this is the life-giving Spirit, 2 Corinthians 3:6-8. Hence that never brought salvation unto any man, nor could it unto any sinner, but only death; but this, of itself and properly, bringeth death or condemnation unto none, but unto all who truly receive it, it bringeth certain salvation, verse 1.
  9. In the adjunct of duration: For that is outdated with respect to those who partake of this new one; but this is everlasting, both in its own continuance, for it admitteth no end or change in substance, and in respect of those to whom it is given, for the grace of this covenant endureth forever with those once truly covenanted.

Maccovius: Here it may not unfitly be asked whether only the covenanted have a right unto the temporal goods of this life, or also those who are not covenanted?

Response: The right unto these temporal goods is twofold: civil and spiritual. The civil right is that which holdeth among men and resteth upon their laws and customs. In respect of this right, men are called lords of these or those temporal goods, whose possession they have gained in the court of men. Thus, the most mighty Turkish emperor is named lord of the greatest part of the earth this day. But the spiritual right is that which is valid and approved before God Himself. Such a right our first parent Adam had in the whole world afore his fall, but by sin he lost it, both for himself and his posterity; yet in Christ it is restored unto all the covenanted. And in this regard, the Turk and other infidels and wicked men are but usurpers of those things which they otherwise lawfully possess civilly.

II. It Is Called the Old and New Testament, but Not in One Sense; Either for the Law and the Gospel, Simply Considered and Opposed; or for the Various Economy and Period of the Doctrine of Salvation, Formerly Before Christ, Which Is the Old Testament, and Now After He Is Revealed, Which Is the New Testament; or Finally, for the Sacred Books of This and That Time and Testament.

Professor: They are called the Old and New Testament either properly or figuratively, and then in a broad or strict sense.

Properly: By the name of the Old Testament is signified the Law, as it was given through Moses unto the people of the Jews, promising life under the condition of perfect obedience, with the threat of a curse against transgressors. Opposed hereto, in its proper sense, is the New Testament, namely, the doctrine of spiritual grace and salvation, fully revealed by the Son of God from the bosom of the Father and published by the preaching of the Apostles; whereby is promised free righteousness and eternal life, through and for the death of the testator Christ, unto all who will believe in Him, through the grace He shall bestow.

In a broad sense: With the addition of palaias (old), the term diatheke (covenant) includeth the Law given through Moses, of which we have spoken, and the promise made unto Adam concerning the seed of the woman, repeated unto Abraham in his seed, in whom all nations should be blessed. This promise the Apostle expressly calleth, Galatians 3:17, “the covenant confirmed before,” and in Ephesians 2:12, “the covenants of promise.” But unto this promise is joined the Law given through Moses, insofar as they are comprehended under the broad meaning of the Old Testament, only so far as the Law is a schoolmaster unto Christ, Galatians 3:24. For what the moral law most strictly required of mankind, the ceremonial law proclaimed was exhibited only in Christ and His sacrifice; the political law served both, procuring outward order as was meet from the use of each. So also the New Testament is taken broadly for the doctrine of both grace and faith, and of repentance and gratitude, or new obedience, because the word of the Gospel is, “Repent ye, and believe the Gospel,” Mark 1:15. And thus it requireth faith in the Gospel and repentance, which twain necessarily draw with them new obedience, whereby we live worthily of God. In this sense the Gospel is understood when the Apostle saith he is separated to preach the Gospel of God, Romans 1:1; that God will judge according to my Gospel, Romans 2:16; and the like. Nothing hindereth but that the covenant of grace, or the promise made unto the fathers, clad in its pedagogical circumstances, may come under the name of the Old Testament, for there was somewhat in it to be renewed and antiquated. Thus Paul, Hebrews 8:13, “In that he saith, A new covenant, he hath made the first old,” taketh the first covenant for the whole worship of the old tabernacle, wherein the promise was surely contained and confirmed. The two testaments are so called not by division of genus into species, but of the subject according to accidents, for the same thing, remaining in kind and substance, is varied by the diverse manner of economy and administration, both on God’s part and man’s.

Figuratively: By metonymy, the Old and New Testament are named the books contained in the code of the Old Testament and the writings of the Apostles and Evangelists, as in human affairs the tablets and signs of testaments are called testaments, because the sacramental signs also take the same name of testament. Thus the Apostle, 2 Corinthians 3:14, speaketh of the veil remaining in the reading of the Old Testament. By analogy hereof, the reading of the Gospel may be called the reading of the New Testament, in which sense the name of the New Testament is commonly used for the code of the Gospels.

III. The Law Is the Rule of Universal Justice and Equity, First Impressed on Nature, Whence It Is Called Natural; Then Given and Repeated Through Moses, Which Is the Mosaic Moral Law.

Junius: Among the Latins, the word lex (law) cometh from legere, that is, to read or publish; among the Greeks, nomos from distributing to each his own; among the Hebrews, Torah, in turn from publishing, hath gained its name. We thus define it generally: Law is an ordinance of reason for the common good, from him who hath care of the community. Some laws are human, others divine. Human laws are made by men, whose nearest rule is twofold: one innate, which we call the natural law; the other revealed from heaven, which we call divine. If they stray from these, they are corrupt, though commonly so named. We must treat only of the divine laws according to our purpose.

Professor: The natural law is the light and dictate of right reason in man’s understanding, informing him by prolepseis or common notions unto the distinction of just and unjust, honest and dishonest, that he may know what is to be done or shunned. Of these notions, some are primary, which we call practical principles; others secondary, which we call conclusions built from those principles with the aid of reason. Therefore, Paul saith that the law of God was acknowledged by the Gentiles, once given over to impiety and injustice, Romans 1:32, and the work of the law written in their hearts, Romans 2:14.

IV. The Mosaic Law Is Threefold: Moral, Ceremonial, and Judicial.

Junius: The divine law is the law of our Lord, delivered through Moses unto the people of Israel; which is partly ethical, partly hierarchical, partly political. These laws, in the five books of Moses, are as blood diffused through the whole body; as they were given by God at sundry times, so they were not described by Moses in the same order: and they are a perfect pattern of the eternal, natural, divine, and human law. From these laws, the whole moral law, and the judicial laws in part, and the ceremonial laws existed afore Moses, but unwritten, handed down by the fathers unto their posterity as by hand, instructing them in the ways of the Lord.

V. The Sum of the Moral Law Is Contained in the Love of God and of Neighbor.

Junius: The moral law (which is scattered throughout the whole Scripture of the Old and New Testaments, and whose sum is contained in the Decalogue) is the unchangeable rule of living rightly, wherein part of the law is expressed the nature and justice of God, and which was in uncorrupted nature the natural law, but now, through sin, so marred that a good part thereof is darkened, and only a small portion remaineth in human minds, so that one scarcely tasteth faintly what worship is acceptable unto God. Therefore, for our dullness’ sake, the Lord hath set forth the written law, which more surely testifieth what was too obscure in the natural law, and more sharply striketh the mind and memory.

VI. The Foundation of Divine Laws Is Not Everywhere the Free Good Pleasure of God, as If He Could Equally Establish and Prescribe the Contrary; but Some Are Right Because God Willeth Them; Others Are Founded in the Nature of God, and These God Willeth as Being in Themselves Just and Good.

Voetius:

First Argument: The dependence of the rational creature upon God, as it is rational, and in the realm of morals and in order unto right reason, is so necessary that its opposite would imply a contradiction in terms and involve absurdity. Hence, unto the passive dependence on the creature’s part correspondeth the active dependence on God’s part, whose opposite would likewise involve contradiction.

Second Argument: Because punitive justice is in God afore any free decree, or prior unto God’s will: therefore, there is some right of God in respect of the rational creature.

Third Argument: From the absurd consequences:

  1. For it would follow that there be no atheism, Epicureanism, diabolism, magic, hatred and denial of God, blasphemy, etc., which God could not freely will to command and by that very act command as morally or spiritually good and pleasing unto Himself.
  2. It would follow that God could deny Himself, not only indirectly and implicitly, but expressly and directly, by commanding man or angel to believe these falsehoods: “I am not your God, to be worshipped and loved by you.”
  3. That God could freely decree, and according to His decree create men and angels, who would have such a law as would prescribe each sin against the Decalogue, both of commission and omission, and would remove the nature of sin or lawlessness from all human and angelic acts, and confer upon them the nature of opposite goodness and obedience.
  4. That God could decree and create men or angels without synteresis (moral awareness) or conscience, and thus without any natural or eternal law: among whom the conformity of right reason with the first and supreme right reason, and consequently the distinction of good and evil in rational acts, both in kind and in particular, would have no place, no more than in a brute, a stone, the sun, or a tree. In brief, that a rational creature could be made wholly lawless.

VII. The Ceremonial Law Containeth Precepts Concerning the Rites of Sacred Things Observed in the Old Testament.

Junius: The ceremonial laws are precepts concerning the outward worship of God to be observed in the Church of the Old Testament, which either signify or pertain unto order. They signify through sacrifices, the Sabbath, circumcision; but they pertain unto order through the circumstances of time, place, and the like.

Maccovius: The doctrine concerning ceremonies is called the Mosaic ceremonial law. This law is that which prescribed ecclesiastical rites and outward ceremonies, such as:

  1. Sacrifices.
  2. Sacred things, whether places, vessels, times, and solemnities.
  3. Sacraments.
  4. Certain observances in food, Deuteronomy 14; Leviticus 17; in clothing, Deuteronomy 22; in prayer, sowing, Deuteronomy 22; in the foreskin of trees, Leviticus 19; in fowling, and some others: and outward worships, whose use endured during the Mosaic polity, both in the tabernacle, which was portable, and in Solomon’s temple, which was fixed in one place only.

The ends of the ceremonial law were sundry and diverse, and may be referred unto these two kinds: some were principal, some less principal:

The principal ends are:

  1. That the Israelites might understand the uncleanness and gravity of sin, and what it meriteth in itself, namely, eternal death.
  2. That in these, as in a mirror, men might behold Christ to come, for in them Christ’s passion and death were vividly set forth, Galatians 3:24; Colossians 2:17; Hebrews 10:1.

The less principal end was that they might be marks of their profession, whereby they might be distinguished from idolaters.

VIII. The Judicial Law Containeth Precepts Directing the Polity of the Jews.

Junius: The judicial laws are precepts concerning the defense of outward discipline according to both tables of the Decalogue, or concerning political governance in the Jewish people, that is, order, the duty of magistrates, judgments, contracts, distribution of goods, and the like. These laws are of two sorts: some pertain unto common law, namely in their substance, but in their circumstances unto particular law; others simply unto particular law, as the cities of refuge.

Professor: The political law is that which determineth the duties of magistrates and subjects of the republic of Israel by certain civil ordinances, and sanctioneth them with bodily punishments against rebels, according to the norm of both tables of the Decalogue, and especially of the second.

IX. The Moral Law Is of Perpetual Right, Binding All: On the Contrary, the Ceremonial and Political Laws Ceased with the People of the Jews.

Junius: All these laws, as shadows, were abolished by the rising of the sun of righteousness, by their just fulfillment. The moral law is abrogated unto the faithful as to the curse, Galatians 3:13; Romans 6:14, not only by the application of Christ’s obedience (as afore Christ), but also by its exhibition; but not as to obedience, Deuteronomy 32:46; Matthew 5:48; 19:17, since Christ exhorteth unto each precept of the law, bidding us in this life to strive toward that perfection which shall be in heaven. For what is more absurd than that the Son of God should die for us, that we might wallow in the mire of sins? The ceremonial law, because it is not of the law of nature (which alone is immutable), is abolished in use and as to obedience; but not in effect, in whose abolition the truth shineth more brightly, Matthew 11:13; Mark 15:38; Acts 15:28; Romans 6:14. For the law was given by Moses, but grace and truth by Christ, John 1:17. But since the reason of ceremonies was not the same at all times, three times are to be distinguished:

  1. Afore Christ’s passion, when the legal ceremonies were ordained for the life of the godly.
  2. After the time of the Gospel, sufficiently published unto the public, when the legal typical ceremonies were deadly.
  3. Finally, the intermediate time, from the passion of Christ unto the promulgation of the Gospel, when, since it was needful to carry out the now dead legal ceremonies and bury them honorably, they were borne with for a time by divine wisdom for men’s sake.

The judicial laws, with piety safe and sound, are abrogated as to circumstances and outward constitution, but not as to equity, on which reason the constitution is founded and resteth. Therefore, full liberty is left unto each nation to make such laws as they foresee will profit them, provided all be weighed by the perpetual rule of charity, so that the form may vary, but the reason remain the same. Oaths remain, and the right of waging war, and magistrates.

Professor: What in this law are of common right, those bind all kinds of magistrates and subjects even now; but what are of particular Jewish right, those expired with the polity of Moses.

X. From the Law, the Transition Is to the Gospel, Which Is the Doctrine of Grace and Salvation in Christ.

Junius: Since the law is a schoolmaster unto Christ, and Christ exhibiteth Himself unto us by His Gospel, reason requireth that from the law, of which we have just spoken, we pass unto the place concerning the Gospel. Euangelion is taken sometimes more broadly, sometimes more strictly: more broadly, either for the reward wont to be given unto one bringing good news, Romans 10:15; or for the solemn sacrifice made for a thing happily done, Jeremiah 31:33; Romans 11:27; Hebrews 8:8, 10. More strictly and properly, for the good and joyful news itself; in which way, by the name kat’ exochen (preeminently), the Apostles called the promulgation of grace exhibited in Christ, Ephesians 1:10. Therefore, the Gospel is, as I may briefly say with the Apostle, “the power of God unto salvation to every one that believeth,” that is, the doctrine of God, joined with the power of the Spirit, unto salvation to every believer, Romans 1:16. It is also called the free and new covenant of God, which (the former covenant of works being forsaken, from whose necessity of obligation this free one absolveth us) God hath made with us, giving unto us the hope and promise of lost salvation, under a condition (without which it remaineth useless and unfruitful unto men) which can stand with Christ and the mere grace of God: and that condition is faith, having for its object Christ and the mercy of God in Christ, for Christ Himself with the mercy of God in Him is properly the cause why God fulfilleth the promise of the covenant in justifying and saving us.

Gomarus: The Gospel, in this wise, may not unfitly be defined as the divine doctrine whereby the secret covenant of God concerning free salvation through Christ is announced unto men fallen into sin, and is begun and preserved with the elect, unto their salvation and the glory of God the Savior. By reason of the subject of the benefit, the Gospel in the sacred writings is called the word of the grace of the Lord, Acts 14:3; the Gospel of the grace of the Lord, Acts 20:24; the Gospel of peace, Ephesians 6:15; Isaiah 52:7; Romans 10:15; and a covenant, 2 Corinthians 3:6; and from the prescription of the condition, the duty of the subject, it is called the law of faith, Romans 3:27; the law of God, kat’ exochen, and the word of faith, Romans 10:8; and by metonymy, faith, Acts 6:7; Galatians 2:5. From which subject it also followeth that the object of the Gospel is, in truth, men—not those persevering in their original righteousness (for the whole need not a physician, nor the righteous reconciliation, Mark 2:17), but those fallen into sin, and therefore guilty of the broken covenant of works and transgressors of the law. Unto whom the covenant of salvation through Christ is proclaimed and offered, Genesis 3:15; Isaiah 61:1; Luke 4:18.

Professor: The proper subject of the Gospel is Christ incarnate. For in His redemption, satisfaction for our sins, and the recounting of His other benefits, it is wholly occupied. In which sense it is called the Gospel of Jesus Christ, the Son of God, that is, concerning Jesus Christ the Son of God, Mark 1:1; Romans 1:16; 15:19; 2 Corinthians 9:13; Galatians 1:7. Likewise, the word of Christ, Colossians 3:16; and His testimony, 2 Timothy 1:8.

XI. The Same Doctrine, and the Way of Salvation Through the Remission of Sins and Faith in Christ, Was Known and Prevailed Also Under the Old Testament.

Luther: The faith of all the ancient faithful was faith in Christ to come, as it is written, “Christ yesterday and today.” They believed in God, but in Him who had promised Christ, and they looked for Him.

Junius: That this Gospel is not a late invention and fiction of men, but was revealed unto the Church of old and ever retained therein, we have both weighty and authentic witnesses and the firmest reasons drawn from Holy Scripture. For what saith Christ Himself unto the unbelieving Jews? “The Scriptures are they which testify of me,” and a little after, “If ye believed Moses, ye would believe me; for he wrote of me,” John 5:39, 46. What say the Apostles? Peter saith that the prophets inquired and searched diligently concerning this salvation of the Gospel, yea, that they bore witness unto this Christ, that whosoever believeth in Him shall receive remission of sins, Acts 3:18; 10:43. Paul likewise saith that this Gospel was promised afore by the prophets in the holy Scriptures, Romans 1:2. Then, if God of old did not in vain will to testify Himself unto them as a father through expiations and sacrifices, nor in vain to consecrate a people unto Himself in this manner, it surely followeth that Christ was known in the same image wherein He now appeareth unto us in full splendor. For the fathers were not so dull and blind as to hope for expiation of sin from a slain beast, or to seek the cleansing of the soul in the outward sprinkling of water, as if they wished to appease Him with cold ceremonies (as though God took great delight therein). But they were shadows and types whereby God willed to instruct His Church in its infancy, yet raising it through these, as means, unto the body itself and the truth shadowed by the type, and unto the hope not of carnal, earthly, temporal things, but of spiritual, heavenly, and eternal goods, and of blessed immortality, whereof, as sons, they had the promises with us in Christ the Mediator, through whom they doubted not that the Spirit was ministered unto them to do good, and that they were forgiven whensoever they sinned; for these benefits are of Christ offered in the Gospel.

Gomarus: Though the Gospel be in truth simple, Genesis 3:15 with John 12:31; Colossians 2:15; Hebrews 2:14; and Genesis 12:3 with Galatians 3:6-8; and Genesis 22:18 with Galatians 3:8; Romans 4:22-24; Acts 2:25; 10:43; 4:12-13; 15:11; Hebrews 11:12, yet a double circumstance is found. For the Gospel of the promise of Christ is one thing; that of His mission another. Whence the former is called promising, Romans 1:2, and premised, Galatians 3:8; Hebrews 3:6; but the latter is called the promised Gospel, Romans 1:2 (since it is the Gospel of the promise of Christ, whereby God of old announced and made the covenant of free salvation through Christ, Hebrews 1:1; Acts 10:43, and promised the coming of Christ, Acts 3:22, 24; 13:32). For which cause this Gospel is called epangelia, promise, Acts 13:32; Galatians 3:17-18, distinctly and kat’ exochen (as far more sublime than other promises).

Maccovius: And from these things it now shineth forth that the Socinians greatly err herein, who teach that the doctrine of the Gospel was not known in the Old Testament. And surely, if the matter be as they would have it, then it followeth that those ceremonies which endured in the Old Testament pleased God in themselves, and not for that they were a shadow and type of Christ and of the benefits obtained through Him; which how absurd it is, no sane man seeth not. For hence, the slaughter of beasts (to omit other things), which was done in sacrifices and is most tedious in itself, would have pleased God per se. They falsely teach that the fathers in the Old Testament had only corporeal promises, but no spiritual ones. Others teach this, as doth Ostorodus in his Institutions, chapter 5. We oppose unto this claim these foundations:

  1. It is taken from the very form of the covenant: “I will be unto you a God, and ye shall be unto me a people,” Leviticus 26:12, which very form is applied by Paul unto the promises of the New Testament, 2 Corinthians 6:16-18.
  2. It is drawn from the object of the spiritual promises, which is the Messiah in the Old and New Testaments, Acts 10:43: “To Him give all the prophets witness, that through His name whosoever believeth in Him shall receive remission of sins.”
  3. It is deduced from the reckoning of the Apostles. For that Gospel which was announced by Christ in the New Testament was that which God had promised afore through the prophets in the holy Scriptures, Romans 1:2, concerning which the prophets inquired and searched diligently, 1 Peter 1:10. And it was the Gospel whereof Isaiah complaineth, “Lord, who hath believed our report?” Romans 10:16. Paul testifieth, as he also expressly declareth, that he said nothing beyond what the prophets and Moses said should come to pass.

Objection: From Hebrews 8:6, “But now hath He obtained a more excellent ministry, by how much also He is the Mediator of a better covenant, which was established upon better promises.” From which words, the adversary saith it appeareth that God inserted spiritual promises into the New Testament.

Response: By denying the consequence. For this covenant is said to be established upon better promises, not because unto the fathers, who lived under the law and that burden of ceremonies as under tutelage and pedagogy, the same hope of eternal life did not shine forth, but because, though God had promised them the same salvation and eternal life, the measure of spiritual revelation was not the same nor equal. Whence this comparison instituted by the Apostle is to be referred rather unto the form—that is, the diversity of divine ministration—than unto the matter itself.

It is also false that eternal life was not promised in the Old Testament unto the fathers. The adversaries deny that eternal life was promised in the Old Testament unto the fathers, which denial most openly contradicteth the word of God. Let us weigh the matter: Christ expressly teacheth, Mark 10:17, that eternal life is promised by the law itself unto him who fulfill it; whence it may be seen what life the clause of the law treateth: “He that doeth them shall live in them.” If, therefore, the law was in the Old Testament, which the adversaries cannot deny, eternal life was promised unto both. It is added that the doctrine of justification which is by faith, which hath annexed the promise of eternal life, was in the Old Testament; therefore, eternal life was. The antecedent is clear from Romans 10, where the words of Moses are expressly cited, and from them this consequence is drawn: “If thou shalt confess with thy mouth the Lord Jesus, and shalt believe in thine heart that God hath raised Him from the dead, thou shalt be saved.” But also there is a most clear testimony in Hebrews 11:13-15, which alone can settle this matter.

Objection: From 2 Timothy 1:10, “But is now made manifest by the appearing of our Savior Jesus Christ, who hath abolished death, and hath brought life and immortality to light through the Gospel.”

Response: It is said to be made manifest because it is more clearly set forth through Christ. For this is the manner of the Holy Spirit, oft to say that something is done when it is more revealed, Proverbs 17:17. And indeed, that what is said concerning the manifestation of the Gospel through Christ must be taken thus is clear from what is said immediately afore.

XII. That the Gentiles Could Be Saved Outside the Gospel, Besides the Word of God, Is Denied.

Gomarus: Whether the Gentiles, whom God formerly suffered to walk in their own ways, Acts 14:16, could be saved through faith in the general mercy of God (revealed by natural benefits) and through repentance, without the knowledge of the Gospel of Christ? We deny it.

Maresius: Those who would have all and every one invited unto the covenant by a universal calling are bound to confound the effects of common providence with the voice of the Gospel, and nature with grace, and to ascribe unto the book of nature and the voice of creatures the proclamation of divine mercy in Christ alone, revealed from the Scriptures; and moreover (since a universal objective grace would be vain and illusory unless subjective grace be equally extended, nor should the operation of the Holy Spirit in this economy be narrower than the philanthropy of the Father or the redemption of the Son), to feign sufficient powers restored unto all and every one, by which they can, if they will, obey God calling, and be received into this free covenant—which is nothing else but to offer sacrifice unto the idol of free will, to suspend the efficacy of vocation therefrom, and to abolish wholly discriminating grace, against Paul saying, “For who maketh thee to differ from another? and what hast thou that thou didst not receive?” 1 Corinthians 4:7; also, “For it is God which worketh in you both to will and to do of His good pleasure,” Philippians 2:13.

XIII. The Doctrine of John the Baptist Was the Same, as to Essence, with the Doctrine of Christ and the Apostles, Differing Only in Circumstances.

Junius: Therefore, John the Baptist, though he was greater than the prophets in the light of preaching, yet was neither an Evangelist nor a minister of the New Testament, taken oppositely; wherefore a certain middle place is rightly assigned unto him, as the commendations with which he is distinguished by Christ confirm, Matthew 11:11. For he was the forerunner preparing the way of the Lord, and warning that the kingdom of heaven was at hand, not that it was present.

Professor: Betwixt the dispensation of the Old and New Testaments there intervened the ministry of John the Baptist, which, as it yielded in excellence unto the Apostolic, so it excelled the Prophetic, whose baptism, instituted by God, we establish to have sealed the remission of sins, and to have been the same in substance and signification with the baptism now received in the Church.
