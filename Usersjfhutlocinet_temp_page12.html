CHAPTER XII: OF FAITH

I. Faith is the Means Whereby We Are Made Partakers of Christ’s Benefits

CALVIN. But all these things will be easy to understand when a clearer definition of faith shall have been set forth: that readers may grasp its power and nature. Now it is convenient to consider what this faith ought to be, through which all who are adopted by God as sons enter into possession of the heavenly kingdom: since it is evident that not any opinion or even persuasion is sufficient for effecting so great a matter.

GOMARUS. Moreover, πίστις, that is, faith, first properly and generally is the persuasion of the mind concerning the truth of anything. Acts 17:4, Heb. 11:13 with Rom. 4:20-21 and 14:23 with v. 14. Whence Philoponus saith: πίστις, γνῶσις τοῦ πειθομένου, faith is the knowledge of that which is persuaded. Then improperly, by metonymy, it is used in two ways. Faith toward God, for the object divinely proposed, is divided into diverse significations also. For one is the faith of working miracles (Matt. 17:20, 1 Cor. 12:9, 13:2), or of obtaining them (Matt. 9:21-22, Acts 14:9); another is called the faith of the Gospel (Phil. 1:27). And the same distinction between faith of signs and of doctrines is indicated by Chrysostom on 1 Cor. 12:9. But the faith of the Gospel is understood either absolutely, of the whole; or relatively, by reason of a part. And the former is the certain persuasion concerning the truth of the whole evangelical doctrine concerning God and Christ the Savior (Phil. 1:27, John 20:31, Heb. 11:6). Which Basil in his Ascetics on faith thus delineates: “Faith therefore is an undoubted assent to those things which are heard, with full persuasion of the truth of those things which, by God’s grace, are preached.” But the latter is a certain assent concerning Christian liberty delivered in the Gospel, because it is sometimes designated by the word faith taken synecdochically (Rom. 14:20, 23 with v. 2 and 14).

II. The Definition of Faith

Faith is the certain and holy persuasion of the mind concerning the truth of the promises and salvation in Christ.

CALVIN. Now a right definition of faith will be established for us if we say that it is a firm and certain knowledge of the divine benevolence toward us, which, founded upon the truth of the gratuitous promise in Christ, is both revealed to our minds and sealed in our hearts by the Holy Spirit.

GOMARUS. Living faith is a supernatural virtue, freely infused by God into the hearts of the elect who are penitent for sin and desire deliverance, through the Gospel and regeneration, unto righteousness and their salvation and His glory: whereby they acknowledge the truth of the divine word, and especially that Jesus is the Christ, the only and perfect Savior of all true believers, and therefore their own.

III. The Object of Faith

The general object of faith is the whole word of God; the special object is the Gospel.

JUNIUS. The object or matter about which faith is concerned, in general is the whole word of God declared by the Prophets and Apostles: nor can faith be separated from it any more than rays can be separated from the Sun whence they arise: so that it is not undeservedly said to be the fountain and basis of faith: whence if it declines, it falls. Again, the object of faith in particular is the goodness, mercy, and promise of God founded in Christ the Mediator and our Lord, and set forth to us in the Gospel (Rom. 4:16). But this object both in general and in particular proposeth to us that which is not seen: for faith is of things not seen (Heb. 11:1).

GOMARUS. But the material object of faith, universal indeed, is only and wholly the word of God proposed to be believed and comprehended altogether in Holy Scripture (1 Cor. 4:6, 2 Tim. 3:15-16, Acts 24:14, 26:24, 27, John 5:39, 2 Pet. 1:19). But the particular object is the word of reconciliation and the word of salvation (2 Cor. 5:19, Acts 13:26), or the Gospel (Mark 1:15, 16:15-16, Rom. 1:16), namely the doctrine of God concerning the person of Jesus Christ and His attributes, with His offices and benefits (John 3:16, 17:3, 1 Cor. 2:2, 3:11, 1 John 5:10-11). The sum of which is: that Jesus is the Christ, that Son of God (Matt. 16:16, John 6:69, 11:27, 20:31, Acts 8:37, 1 John 5:1), the only and perfect Savior of all true believers (John 14:6, Acts 4:12, 13:38-39, Heb. 9:16, 10:14, 1 John 1:7, John 3:16, Rom. 3:25, 4:25, 1 Cor. 1:30, Heb. 2:10, 5:9-10). Thus far concerning the object, as the Gospel is called the law (that is, doctrine) of faith (Rom. 3:27) and the word of faith (Rom. 10:8), so conversely, faith is called the faith of the Gospel (Phil. 1:27), faith in God (1 Thess. 1:8, 1 Pet. 1:21), and faith of Jesus Christ (Rom. 3:22), and faith of the Son of God (Gal. 2:20), and knowledge of salvation (Luke 1:77), knowledge of the grace of God (Col. 1:6), knowledge of Christ (Isa. 53:11). This concerning the matter of faith.

IV. Faith Requires Knowledge, Assent, and Trust

It requires knowledge antecedently, assent formally, and trust consequently.

PROFESSORS. That saving faith is, from certain knowledge of divine revelation, a firm assent begotten in our minds by the Holy Spirit through the word of the Gospel, to all things which God hath revealed to us in His word, but especially to the saving promises made in Christ, whereby resting with certain confidence in God, each believer firmly determineth that not only remission of sins is promised to believers in general, but granted to him in particular, and eternal righteousness, and from it life, is given from God’s mercy for the merit of Jesus Christ alone.

MACCOVIUS. Hence therefore it is clear that justifying faith consisteth in knowledge and confidence, and so is of that kind of things which are called aggregate entities, such as are original sin and our regeneration. But that it is knowledge is clear, both from many other places, and from 1 Cor. 13:9, Heb. 11:3, Isa. 53:11. When we say faith is knowledge and cognition, we understand not any kind whatsoever but solid and true. That this may be understood, we distinguish knowledge and cognition, that one is prescribing, another persuading, one theoretical, another practical, one which rendereth inexcusable, another which justifieth. This distinction hath most clear foundation in Holy Scripture. For there is set forth in it a certain cognition which is not the same with eternal life, which those who have it perish; and another, which Christ called eternal life (John 17:3), which he who hath cannot perish. It is said further in Scripture that there is one cognition which is not cognition as it ought to be (1 Cor. 8:2), and solid and true cognition; likewise cognition such as they had who crucified Christ, whose princes sinned so against the Holy Spirit; and cognition which if they had had, they would certainly not have crucified Christ (1 Cor. 2:8). When therefore we say faith is cognition or knowledge, we understand practical, which justifieth, which is wholly different in kind from that other knowledge of which we have made mention from Scripture. That faith is confidence, or consisteth in confidence, is taught in the holy letters (Matt. 9:2, Mark 11:24, 9:23, James 1:5-6). Finally, all places which command us to believe in God, in the Son of God, in Christ, which cannot be understood of bare assent, but openly include confidence.

WALAEUS. First therefore we must examine the opinion of the Papists, who will have faith to be assent which is made without knowledge. We acknowledge indeed that in true faith there is also this affection, that we believe that there are true things in the word of God even which we do not yet understand. Why? Because we know this from God’s word, that God’s word is mere and pure truth, and therefore we assent to this proposition not ignorantly, but knowingly. We acknowledge also that many are true Christians who have not yet attained due knowledge in many articles of faith; as in the disciples of Christ before the resurrection of Christ there was yet some ignorance; but that is the imperfection of faith, not perfection, as the Papists will have it: whence also Christ calleth them of little faith, and the Apostles themselves ask that their faith be increased. We therefore will treat of true faith in its form and perfection, considered as it ought to be; and we say that there can be no true faith without previous knowledge; because there can be no faith had of an unknown thing, or no confidence can be placed in an unknown thing. We demonstrate this:

I. From many places of Scripture where faith is called knowledge: as Isa. 53:11, “By his knowledge shall my righteous servant justify many.” Luke 1:77, “To give knowledge of salvation.” Job 19:25, “I know that my redeemer liveth.” John 17:3, “And this is life eternal, that they might know thee.”

II. From places where faith and the word are put as correlates: as John 20:31, “These are written that ye might believe,” etc. Isa. 54:13, John 6:54, “They shall be all taught of God: every one therefore that hath heard, and hath learned of the Father, cometh unto me.” John 5:24, “He that heareth my word, and believeth on him that sent me,” etc.

III. From Rom. 10:14, “How shall they believe in him of whom they have not heard? And how shall they hear without a preacher?” etc. and v. 17, “So then faith cometh by hearing, and hearing by the word of God.”

IV. From places where confession is required in faith: Rom. 10:10, as also John 11:26, Christ asketh Mary and Martha, “Believest thou this?” Likewise Acts 8:37, Philip the Eunuch: and all Christians in the creed say, “I believe in God the Father,” etc.

V. Finally, those who are ignorant are everywhere reproved in Scripture; Christ reproveth His disciples, John 6. The Apostle the Hebrews, chap. 5:2. Hence also believers are called disciples.

Moreover, that faith is assent is beyond controversy, because to believe someone teaching something is to assent that what he saith is true; whence elsewhere Scripture saith that “he that believeth on him hath set to his seal that God is true,” etc. But it is asked whether also the special assent whereby we believe the evangelical promises pertain to us, be a property of true faith? The Papists deny this, who teach us to doubt concerning the special grace of God, or at least to proceed by conjectures. For they will have there to be only general assent to the mysteries of faith from the word of God, which they establish as Catholic and Dogmatic faith, which justifieth us dispositively. But that faith is not only general assent given to the word of God, or to the truth of promises in general, as the Papists will have it, is demonstrated by the following arguments:

I. Because thus the faith of the devil and many hypocrites would be true faith, whereby they believe that God is (James 2:19), and that Christ came, etc. But this is false: otherwise they would also be truly faithful.

II. The places John 3:16, Acts 13:39, Rom. 3:24, before alleged by us, say that faith is in the blood of Christ; which is more than what is believed by general dogmas.

III. From places where faith is called πεποίθησις (Rom. 5:1), παρρησία (Heb. 4:16), πληροφορία (Rom. 4), ὑπόστασις, ἔλεγχος, etc.

IV. From places where we are said to know specially that Christ is our Savior, or God, and our Father. 1 John 3:2, “We know that, when he shall appear, we shall be like him,” etc. and v. 5, “And ye know that he was manifested to take away our sins.” and v. 14, “We know that we have passed from death unto life.” and chap. 5:10, “He that believeth on the Son of God hath the witness in himself.” and v. 11, “And this is the record, that God hath given to us eternal life, and this life is in his Son.” See also the place Rom. 8 from v. 31 and following. Likewise, 1 Cor. 2:12, “Now we have received, not the spirit of the world, but the spirit which is of God; that we might know the things that are freely given to us of God.” And v. 16, “For who hath known the mind of the Lord? or who hath instructed him? But we have the mind of Christ.” These places invincibly demonstrate this certainty to be in faith. Nor doth the exception avail, that these are said of extraordinary revelation made to the Apostles. Because manifestly in these and similar places it treateth of all believers, and this certainty is gathered from common documents of faith, as is evident to one inspecting the places.

V. The places demonstrate it which conjoin confidence in faith or with faith, of which matter afterward. These now suffice us against the Papists: namely that singular confidence is in faith, or with faith. Ps. 25:2, “O my God, I trust in thee: let me not be ashamed, let not mine enemies triumph over me.” See also 31:6 and 72:4. Rom. 5:5, “And hope maketh not ashamed.”

VI. From the testimony of the Holy Spirit, whereby believers are sealed: as Rom. 8:15, “For ye have not received the spirit of bondage again to fear; but ye have received the Spirit of adoption, whereby we cry, Abba, Father.” 2 Cor. 1:22, “Who hath also sealed us, and given the earnest of the Spirit in our hearts.” So Eph. 1:14. Since moreover He is the Spirit of truth (John 15:26), He cannot deceive us.

Instance: But the testimony of the Spirit indeed doth not deceive, but we can deceive ourselves, thinking we have this Spirit, which yet we have not.

Answer: I acknowledge many deceive themselves, because they do not sufficiently prove themselves: yet true believers are certain of it, as all the antecedent places evince: otherwise these things would be said in vain. Which also the effects of it evince, namely firm consolation, certain peace of conscience, etc. of which we glory (Rom. 5:1).

VII. Finally, all places evince it in which believers acknowledge God as theirs, and glory that Christ died for them: as Job 19:25, Matt. 6:9, Gal. 2:20, “Who loved me, and gave himself for me.” So 1 Tim. 1:15, “I obtained mercy,” etc. Whence also in true invocation we ought to pray (Matt. 21:22), “All things, whatsoever ye shall ask in prayer, believing, ye shall receive.” Likewise Mark 11:23, “Not doubting.” James 1:5.

AMES. That justifying faith by its nature produceth, and so hath conjoined with itself, a special and certain persuasion concerning the grace and mercy of God in Christ: whence also justifying faith is not ill often described by the Orthodox by that persuasion, especially when they impugn that general faith to which the Papists attribute all things. But I. that persuasion as to the sense of it is not always present. For it can be and often is, either through weakness of judgment, or through various temptations and disturbances of mind, that he who truly believeth, and through faith is justified before God, yet for a time thinketh according to what he feeleth, that he neither truly believeth nor is reconciled to God. II. There are various degrees of this persuasion, so that neither do all believers have plainly the same certainty of grace and divine favor, nor the same believers at every moment of time: which yet cannot be affirmed of faith properly justifying, without immense detriment to that consolation and peace which Christ left to believers.

V. The Form of Faith

Neither charity nor obedience to Christ’s precepts is the form of faith.

WALAEUS. There remaineth to be examined the question which hath much vicinity with the antecedent; namely, whether faith be rightly divided by the Papists into unformed and formed? They call unformed that which is without hope and charity: formed, that which is with hope and charity. But they make charity properly the form of faith, not indeed internal, but external and assisting: as Aristotle understood the intelligences in heaven. We confess indeed that some faith is without charity, but this is not justifying faith, but historical or temporary. Two things therefore are here to be proved by us: First, that true and justifying faith is never without piety and charity: Then, that yet it is ill established that the form of justifying faith is charity.

The first is proved from manifest places of Scripture, in which these two are asserted always to be conjoined. 1 John 1:6, “If we say that we have fellowship with him, and walk in darkness, we lie, and do not the truth: but if we walk in the light, as he is in the light, we have fellowship one with another, and the blood of Jesus Christ his Son cleanseth us from all sin.” No one therefore hath justifying faith, through which sins are purged by the blood of Christ, unless he walk in the light. So Paul Rom. 6 immediately from the beginning gathereth our sanctification from justification by faith. So also James 2:17, “Faith without works is dead, being alone.” But dead faith is not true and quickening faith, of which we treat. Then it is proved from the effects of true faith, which not only justifieth us, but also sanctifieth, and obtaineth the spirit of adoption, as Scripture everywhere speaketh, which cannot be without charity. Rom. 8:1, “There is therefore now no condemnation to them which are in Christ Jesus, who walk not after the flesh, but after the Spirit.” Gal. 5:6, “That faith alone availeth with God which worketh by love.” Matt. 13:23, faith alone is conceived in a good and honest heart, which bringeth forth fruit in constancy. “But he that received seed into the good ground is he that heareth the word, and understandeth it; which also beareth fruit, and bringeth forth, some an hundredfold, some sixty, some thirty.” Thirdly, because Christ discerneth His true disciples by these signs, as John 13:35, “By this shall all men know that ye are my disciples, if ye have love one to another.” And 14:23, “If a man love me, he will keep my words.” And immediately after v. 24, “He that loveth me not keepeth not my sayings.” And chap. 15:5, “I am the vine, ye are the branches: He that abideth in me, and I in him, the same bringeth forth much fruit,” etc.

Objection: 1 Cor. 13:2, “And though I have all faith, so that I could remove mountains, and have not charity,” etc.

Answer: It is manifest that this treateth of the faith of miracles, which can be in those not justified.

LUTHER. It is answered to adversaries that Paul in this place taketh faith for the gift of the spirit: but since charity is the greatest of all spiritual gifts, it is deservedly preferred to such faith. To the contentious and disputatious it is answered that Paul here speaketh by a hypothetical proposition, “if I have faith,” etc. To the pious also it can be answered that faith here, even if it be taken for that justifying faith, yet charity is greater than it, and without this that is nothing.

WALAEUS. The other question is, whether charity be the true form of faith? which we deny:

I. Because faith hath another essential form, namely assent and confidence.

II. Because it is a distinct virtue.

Then neither is it an assisting form, which giveth to it life and power of justifying:

I. Because charity is from faith (1 Tim. 1:5).

II. Because it is opposed to works in the business of justification.

III. Because Scripture never attributeth to charity the power of justifying, which power would more belong to charity than to faith, if faith borrowed the power of justifying from charity. For that by which each thing is such, that is more such. Or if together with faith it had the power of justifying, at least it ought to be attributed to it equally as to faith. But Scripture always attributeth the power of justifying to faith alone, as shall be proved afterward.

Objection 1: The place of Paul Gal. 5:6, “Faith worketh by love.”

Answer: And the understanding by the will, the soul by the body; not therefore its form.

Objection 2: The place James 2:22, “Faith is made perfect by works,” therefore its form.

Answer: The consequence is denied: because they are pure affirmatives in the second figure. If conceived in the first, the major is to be distinguished: because works do not perfect faith internally, but externally in respect of other offices of faith, whereby it serveth the glory of God and offices to our neighbor. II. “Is perfected” can be conveniently taken for “is demonstrated to be perfect,” as “my strength is made perfect in weakness” (2 Cor. 12:9).

It remaineth that we examine the opinion of the Socinians concerning faith, which the Remonstrants follow in their Confession. Socinus, in the book which he published in the year 1611, whose title is “That Evangelicals ought to join themselves to their company who are falsely called Arians and Ebionites,” p. 22, among opinions adverse to piety, numbereth also this definition of faith, whereby we say we are justified for Christ’s sake, when we believe that we are received into grace, and sins are remitted for Christ’s sake, who by His death satisfied for sins. But he himself will have faith to be confidence, whereby we trust God through Christ, that through Christ we shall receive those things which are promised by God through Christ to those who keep His precepts, obey God and Christ, and in one word, who walk not after the flesh, but after the spirit. Therefore they include evangelical works in faith, as its part, and will have faith to be confidence, whereby we trust that those who obey Christ shall receive remission of sins and eternal life; in one word, not only to promises, but also to evangelical precepts, or to those for the observation of these. The Remonstrants in their Confession manifestly follow this opinion, in several places where they treat of faith and justification. That this opinion is false is demonstrated:

I. From the very nature of faith and obedience. For faith respecteth promises (Mark 1:15). “The time is fulfilled, and the kingdom of God is at hand: repent ye, and believe the gospel”; obedience of life, precepts; faith respecteth the merit of Christ; obedience of life, our own works; faith seeketh life outside itself, in Christ (1 John 1:3, Phil. 3:9, 2 Cor. 5:21); obedience within itself, as all Evangelicals have hitherto acknowledged. Whence also the Apostle Rom. 4:16, “Therefore it is of faith, that it might be by grace; to the end the promise might be sure to all the seed”: and v. 20, “He staggered not at the promise of God through unbelief; but was strong in faith, giving glory to God,” etc.

II. Holy Scripture expressly distinguisheth faith from repentance, charity, obedience of life, and evangelical works. Therefore faith is not that obedience. The antecedent is proved. Mark 1:15, “Repent ye, and believe the gospel.” Acts 20:21, “Testifying both to the Jews, and also to the Greeks, repentance toward God, and faith toward our Lord Jesus Christ.” Gal. 5:6, “Faith which worketh by love.” Therefore it is distinct from charity and obedience.

III. Thus legal and evangelical faith would be confounded, and justification by the law and by the Gospel. For the law saith, “He that doeth these things shall live in them”: and by this very thing requireth confidence from man, that if he do them, he shall receive eternal life from God: and therefore as by the law we have this confidence, so we would have no other under the Gospel, than that he who obeyeth the evangelical precepts shall be saved.

IV. From many places where faith and works are opposed in the business of justification, and not only external, but also internal; otherwise how would God justify the ungodly, or faith be opposed to works? For neither charity nor repentance is ever opposed to works in the business of justification, or can be opposed. Therefore works are not faith itself. Rom. 4:5, “But to him that worketh not, but believeth on him that justifieth the ungodly, his faith is counted for righteousness,” etc. Eph. 2:8, “For by grace are ye saved through faith; and that not of yourselves: it is the gift of God: Not of works, lest any man should boast.”

V. Finally, where examples of faith are described to us, as of Abraham, Rom. 4, it is not described by holiness, or by charity, or by works; but by πληροφορίαν, πεποίθησιν, ὑπόστασιν, ἔλεγχον. See Heb. 11:1, “Now faith is the substance of things hoped for, the evidence of things not seen.”

VI. Of Hope

Faith is the foundation of hope.

ZANCHI. We believe also that hope is born from faith, and that it is the foundation of this, the Apostle saying that faith is the substance of things hoped for (Heb. 11:1). For therefore we hope for future good things, and by patience certainly expect them, because we believe the divine promises. But hope is (Rom. 8:24) the gift of God, whereby promised good things, which are not yet had, nor seen, through patience, from God’s mercy, and for the merit of Christ alone, we as certainly expect as we also certainly believe.

VII. The Certainty of Faith

True faith hath conjoined with it certainty of itself.

JUNIUS. We know that we have this faith:

I. From the testimony of the Holy Spirit (Rom. 8:15-16, 26, Gal. 4:6, 2 Tim. 1:12, 1 John 5:10).

II. From the struggle of faith with doubting: for such is the nature of our faith that it always fighteth with some doubting. We have an example in David, whom the innumerable complaints of the Prophet himself sufficiently show was not always of peaceful mind (Ps. 27:14, 31:23, 42:6, 43:5, 77:10, 116:7).

III. From the effects and fruits of faith (Gal. 5:22), which effects yet do not inform faith itself, as the Sophists most ineptly dream, but are tokens of faith.

GOMARUS. The certainty of which, although it be not expressly named in God’s word, yet from its doctrine concerning faith, and the sense of faith (as a true and certain antecedent of practical reasoning) is solidly concluded. For the Gospel extendeth the proposition of it, confirmed by the authority of divine testimony (1 Cor. 2:1, 1 John 5:9-11): whosoever by living faith believeth God’s Gospel concerning the free justification and salvation of all true believers through Christ, is justified and shall be saved (John 3:16, 20:31, Rom. 8:30, 10:10, Gal. 5:6). The assumption, certain experience of conscience concerning such faith, dictateth; I so believe (1 Tim. 1:15, 2 Tim. 1:12, Rom. 8:16, 38, 2 Cor. 4:13, 13:5, Acts 8:37, Heb. 10:39, 1 John 3:19). Whence it deduceth this necessary conclusion: Therefore I am justified and shall be saved. Which testimony and judgment of the spirit and conscience of believers, the consonant testification of the Holy Spirit, in their minds (coming as it were as an earnest and seal) particularly sealeth (Rom. 8:16, Eph. 1:13). Nevertheless this action of faith is indeed proper, but not perpetual. Since sometimes by the temptations of the flesh, the world, Satan (by which either the memory of that proposition established by the Gospel, or of the assumption of conscience is clouded) it becometh weak, or is suppressed for a time, but is never extinguished (Ps. 30:7-8, 42:6, 73:2, 22 with 17, Matt. 14:30-31, 26:72, 74). Even as also the Holy Spirit provoked to chastise the sins of believers deferreth for some time His sealing of faith and His consolation unto serious repentance, but doth not utterly take it away (Ps. 51:12, 14, Ezek. 36:27).

MACCOVIUS. The certainty of justification followeth, concerning which the enunciation is most certain: that this certainty both can and ought to be in believers. I seek the argument from faith and the perseverance of faith. From faith thus: everyone who certainly believeth in the Son of God receiveth remission of sins, and can and ought to be certain of grace by the proper certainty of faith. But every believer believeth in the Son of God: Therefore. The major is Scripture’s (Acts 10:43, 13:38). The assumption is evident by the internal vision of faith. For everyone who hath faith (as Augustine testifieth) seeth it in his heart, and holdeth it with most certain knowledge, and with conscience crying out. It is evident also by the testimony of the Holy Spirit witnessing with our spirit that we are the sons of God. Nor can this be doubtful, which is evident from the contrary: for if anyone who hath unbelief, distrust, perfidy, contrary to faith, holdeth it with most certain knowledge, and seeth it in his heart, what madness will it be to deny that anyone can be certain of the faith of his heart? Yea, the Holy Spirit commandeth the Corinthians (2 Cor. 13:5) to prove themselves whether they be in the faith. Which would certainly be vain if believers could not know that they are believers. Nor this only, but moreover they can be certain (lest perchance anyone should say that believers can and ought to be certain of remission of sins as long as they retain faith, but that these can lose faith) that they shall persevere. We give two arguments for this matter. The first we seek from 1 Cor. 10:13, “God is faithful, who will not suffer you to be tempted above that ye are able; but will with the temptation also make a way to escape.” Where these words are to be observed, “will make a way to escape.” For if in temptation God always giveth believers a way to escape, then they can be certain that they shall persevere.

Exception: If I shall believe: God will give a way to escape.

We answer: This is truly nugatory.

For it is the same as if they should say, God if He will give a way to escape, will give a way to escape: For to persevere in faith is to obtain a way to escape. Therefore believers who implore God’s help ask from Him that they may always believe. Certainly none of the pious asketh from God that he may be able to believe if he will, but that he may always believe. Therefore either God doth not hear their prayers, or He giveth them that they may always believe. Then (which is our second argument) unless we can certainly determine that we shall persevere, all the consolations of Scripture will be vain. For when we tremble, the cause of trembling is this, I doubt whether I shall persevere. Therefore consolations must come, by which I may be certain that I shall persevere. And to this all the consolations of Scripture tend, which otherwise would be void. An argument for our opinion Catharinus the Monk supplieth, who defended this opinion in the Council of Trent, as Peter Suave Polano testifieth p. 232, whose words are these: “Scripture obligeth us to give thanks to God for our justification, which unless we are certain that we have received, with what face shall we return thanks? Since it is inept to acknowledge a benefit, whether it be given or not, thou understandest not.” Lastly we argue from Acts 26:16-18, the argument is of this sort: True believers receive remission of their sins by faith in Christ: Therefore by faith they are certain that their sins are remitted to them.

ARETIUS. But to doubt concerning divine grace, or, as they speak, not to be able certainly to determine whether thou art in grace or not, ought to be alien from pious men. For first it maketh impious and frivolous Pyrrhonians of Christian men, such as true Philosophy cannot bear. For the first heads of any confession ought to be certain and undoubted, such as are among Christians, that God is, that the Son of God satisfied for us, and that that sacrifice is acceptable to God the Father, and that we please Him for the merit of the Son, if we believe in Him. That doubting taketh away these principles from us. Thus Pyrrho sported in Philosophy, who introduced ἐποχήν, from which were called ἐφεκτικοί, σκεπτικοί, ἀπορητικοί, his defenders, and ζητητικοί. Because they had many things probable, but nothing certain in any question. Which kind of doctrine Favorinus also the celebrated Academic followed, as is in Galen, “Of the best kind of speaking,” but as it was ridiculous for them so to defend ἐποχήν that they should say it could not be certainly known whether the sun existed or not; so it is shameful for a Christian man to wish to doubt concerning the foundation of his salvation; for what firm thing will he retain in others? Will he doubt whether Christ died for him? Whether Christ was also in these lands? Whether He was at all? Whether he himself is a Christian? And whether he simply is a man? Wherefore this doctrine is diligently to be avoided.
