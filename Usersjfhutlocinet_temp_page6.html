Chapter VI. On the Providence of God

I. Creation Immediately Followed by Providence

Creation is immediately followed by the providence of God over the things created.

Calvin saith: Moreover, it would be a cold and jejune thing for God to be a momentary Creator, who only once completed His work, and in this it especially behooveth us to differ from profane men, that not less in the perpetual state of the world, than in its first origin, the presence of divine power may shine forth unto us.

II. Providence Governs All Things

That all things are governed by the providence of God, both He Himself sufficiently, and the Holy Scriptures, do approve.

Piscator saith: This is enough in general to know, that there have been chiefly three opinions of the philosophers concerning the providence of God. The first is of those who simply denied that there is any providence concerning those things which are and are done in these inferior parts of the world. Of this number are Protagoras, Melius, Democritus, Epicurus. The second is of the Stoics, who instead of providence feigned a certain absolute necessity of all things and motions, existing in the very nature of things; unto which not only all these things, but also God Himself is bound and subject: which necessity they called fate. The third is of those who, affirming that God indeed seeth and understandeth all things, yet denied that He governeth and moderateth all things; but only exciteth the celestial motions, through which He giveth the power of acting and operating unto the inferior parts of nature, as are Averroes, and other Peripatetics.

Walaeus saith: But that there is this divine providence, and that through it all things are sustained and governed, even against the Epicureans, the saner philosophers have acknowledged, although they have gone astray into certain other errors; as is to be seen in Cicero, book 2, De Natura Deorum. But the arguments demonstrating this are chiefly these:

  1. It is taken from the creation of the world. For if God created this world, as was demonstrated before, therefore He also conserveth and governeth the same. For neither the wisdom, power, and goodness of God permitteth that there should be one creator of the world, another governor: for so both would labor under infirmity, this of making, that of not providing, and there would be given two Gods, of whom neither would be omnipotent. Nor could the Creator of the world permit the world unto chance or fortune, for then He would be neither wise, nor good, nor powerful: not wise, because He would not attain the end of the world created by Himself: not good, because He would neglect the world made by Himself: not powerful, because He could not care for and govern what He made. All which are blasphemous.
  2. It is taken from the constancy and perpetual order in things, both celestial and elementary. For those also in their mutability are constant. For it is of wisdom to order all things unto a certain end. So the course of the heavens is constant, and the variation of the elements and of these natural things hath constant orbs and vicissitudes, as is manifest unto all. Which argument in Holy Scripture is sometimes amplified: as in the last chapters of Job, Psalm 19; 104, and elsewhere throughout.
  3. It is sought from the perpetual supply or restitution of flowing and perishing things. For nothing which perisheth can be restored or again supplied by itself. As a house, of which some part is collapsed, cannot restore itself, which yet in herbs, trees, animals, etc., we see done daily, and now for almost 6000 years we have seen it done perpetually.
  4. It is sought from the erection of monarchies and republics, and from the raising up of diverse excellent men and heroes, who not seldom are singularly raised up for the conservation of the human race.
  5. Scripture here is manifest. For the Apostle saith, Romans 11:36, “For of Him, and through Him, and to Him, are all things.” And Colossians 1:17, “And He is before all things, and by Him all things consist.” And Hebrews 1:3,“Upholding all things by the word of His power.”

III. Providence Defined

But providence is that action of God, whereby He hath care of all things, sustaineth and moderateth them.

Calvin saith: Therefore, let the readers hold first that providence is called, not that whereby God idly beholdeth from heaven what is done in the world, but whereby as it were holding the helm, He moderateth all events. So it pertaineth no less unto the hands, than unto the eyes.

Trelcatius saith: But this providence is an external and temporal action of God, whereby He conserveth all and singular things which are, and disposeth all and singular things which are done unto the end which He hath determined, according to the liberty of His will, and that so that in all and singular things He may be glorified.

Ames saith: The providence of God is that efficiency, whereby He provideth for creatures now existing in all things, according to the counsel of His will.

IV. Providence Consists of Conservation and Governance

It consisteth of two parts, the conservation of things, and governance.

Ames saith: The providence of God is either conservation or governance. Conservation is that whereby God maketh all things, both universal and singular, to persist, as well as touching essence as touching powers, Psalm 104:19-20; Acts 17:28; Hebrews 1:3, which by the Schoolmen is not ineptly called the maintenance of God, because through it God sustaineth all things as it were with His hand. This conservation necessarily intercedeth between creation and the governance of created things: because whatsoever is created is created for some end and use, unto which also it ought to be directed and governed; but it cannot attain unto that end nor be directed unto it, unless its being be continued or conserved. The conservation of God is necessary unto the creature, since the creature dependeth altogether from the Creator, not only as touching its being made, but also as touching its being, existing, remaining, and operating: so that every creature would return into that nothing out of which it was made, if God did not sustain it, and that very cessation of divine conservation, without any other operation, would immediately reduce any creature into nothing, Psalm 104:29, “Thou hidest Thy face, they are troubled: Thou takest away their breath, they die, and return to their dust.” Some things are conserved immediately, which namely are subject only unto God. This conservation with creation is in very deed the same, differing only in reason, in that creation includeth a certain newness, which conservation excludeth, and excludeth a preceding existence, which conservation includeth: so that this conservation is nothing else than as it were a continued creation, and therefore it is joined with creation, Nehemiah 9:6, “Thou hast made, and Thou preservest them all.” Governance is that whereby God directeth and leadeth all creatures unto their proper ends, Psalm 29:10, “The Lord sitteth King for ever.” The governance of things ought to be from God: for unless they were governed by the same power by which they were created, they would never certainly attain the end for which they were created: and it proceedeth from imperfection in the artificer, when he leaveth the work which he hath made to be afterward directed by another. This governance includeth intrinsically not only the reason of means convenient unto the end, but also their certain efficacy or the very attainment itself. Therefore, the order of this governance is certain, immovable, and indissoluble, so that from all the order of governance the creature cannot totally withdraw itself; although from some particular order thereof it can decline, Genesis 50:20.

V. Governance: Common and Special

Governance is either common of all things in general, or special and moral, of intelligent beings.

Junius saith: Of this providence (which we define), there are as it were two parts, one universal, Psalm 104:27; Proverbs 15:3; Romans 11:36, the other particular, Deuteronomy 32:10; 1 Kings 17:4; Psalm 23:1; 34:8,16; 119:11; Isaiah 43:1; Matthew 6:33; 16:9; Acts 9:4; Hebrews 1:14; 1 Peter 5:7,12, and in six hundred other places. That is, whereby God preserveth the order of nature both superior and inferior posited by Himself, and is commonly called the universal conservation of the world; but this is, whereby He hath a peculiar care of every creature, but chiefly of the human race, and most of all of the faithful, whom He vouchsafeth with a nearer intuition, and for whose good He created the rest. But this work of God, which in created things God effecteth according to His twofold providence, is also twofold, the work of nature, and of grace. We call the work of nature, that which He effecteth both in nature, both superior and inferior, and in the singular things of nature according to that manner of nature, which He hath expressed in the nature of things as in a most clear mirror of His nature. We call the work of grace, that which He effecteth in the elect according to the good pleasure of His will. The work of nature He effecteth according to that reason, whereby He is the universal principle in the nature of things: but the work of grace He effecteth singularly, whereby He is the unique and immediate principle of grace.

Ames saith: I. Common governance is that whereby God governeth all things in a like manner.

Unto this governance pertaineth:

I. The law of nature common to all things: which is a certain participation of the law and will of God inbred in all things from the beginning, Job 38:12, “Hast thou commanded the morning since thy days? and caused the dayspring to know his place?” etc.

II. The inclination of nature: which is the principle of acting according to that law, Job 39:27, “Doth the eagle mount up at thy command, and make her nest on high?”

III. Natural instinct; which is a peculiar instigation of animals, unto certain nobler acts, with a certain species or vestige of reason, Proverbs 6:6, “Go to the ant, thou sluggard; consider her ways, and be wise.” And 30:24-28, “There be four things which are little upon the earth, but they are exceeding wise: the ants, the conies, the locusts, the spiders.” Jeremiah 8:7, “The stork in the heaven knoweth her appointed times; and the turtle and the crane and the swallow observe the time of their coming.”

IV. A certain obediential power, whereby all creatures are apt to obey the command of God, Psalm 103:22; 148:8, “Doing His pleasure, fulfilling His word.”

This governance shineth forth in the operation of all things:

I. That they always respect some certain end; and therefore it is necessary that they be acted and governed by intelligence everywhere present and omnipotent, that is, by God Himself, Job 38:27, “To satisfy the desolate and waste ground; and to cause the bud of the tender herb to spring forth?” Isaiah 55:10, “The rain cometh down, and the snow from heaven, and returneth not thither, but watereth the earth, and maketh it bring forth and bud, that it may give seed to the sower, and bread to the eater.”

II. That the works of nature are so accurately ordered, and consonant unto reason, that they cannot but proceed from the highest reason, Proverbs 30:25-28.

III. That besides their proper ordering, whereby each seek their own perfection, they cultivate as it were a common society, and all more desire the conservation of the whole universe, than of themselves: as is to be seen in those heavy things, which are carried upward, to avoid a vacuum.

By the power of this governance, all secondary causes are in some manner predetermined, that is:

I. They are excited unto acting by influx, or previous motion, insofar as to produce that into act, which before was in the potency of the creature (besides the communication of power, and the sustentation thereof) such a thing is necessarily required.

II. They are applied unto a certain object, about which they are conversant in acting, Ezekiel 21:21-22; 2 Samuel 16:10.

By the power also of the same governance they are ordered, that is:

I. Limits and bounds are set unto action, Job 1:12; 2:6; 38:10.

II. Some good is elicited from the same, Genesis 50:20.

II. Special governance is that whereby God governeth rational creatures in a moral manner. The singular condition of these creatures maketh the difference. For since they are created after the image of God, and have an intrinsic principle of their acts from counsel, therefore they are to be governed congruently with counsel and liberty. Yet this special governance excludeth not that real governance of the creature of reason which is common to all creatures, but accedeth unto it. This moral governance consisteth in teaching, and in fulfilling according to that which He taught before, Micah 6:8, “He hath shewed thee, O man, what is good.” Deuteronomy 30:15, “See, I have set before thee this day life and good, and death and evil.” Hither properly pertaineth that revealed will of God, which is the rule of acting to the rational creature as touching manners.

VI. Providence: Immediate and Mediate

The providence of God is either immediate or mediate.

Junius saith: This work is either mediate or immediate. Immediate is that which both in the nature of the universe, and in the singular parts thereof, is performed without means or secondary causes, and besides, or even where it shall seem good, against all means, which work maketh not a little for the illustrating of the glory of God, and the manifesting of His power, as which is bound to no external means. But we call the mediate work of God, that which is done through various means, now through that superior nature, now through this inferior, both common and singular, now finally through the singular parts of nature according to nature, and above nature itself, as it hath seemed good to His majesty. And this is a singular testimony of the divine goodness, conjoined with that His manifold wisdom, Ephesians 3:10, that since He Himself alone is the efficient cause of all things, and hath the ends, forms, and ideas of all things in His own mind, and so hath them that He alone sufficeth for the conserving and governing of this whole world made by Himself out of nothing, yet He willed to communicate also with creatures the dignity of causality (as I may so speak), according to their manner.

Ames saith: The providence of God is either immediate, whereby God through Himself, as the absolutely solitary cause, provideth for things; or mediate, whereby He provideth by means adhibited. Immediately God worketh all things which are done, both by reason of power, with respect to all entity which is found in the effect (for the power of God reacheth every effect, Deuteronomy 8:3; Isaiah 28:26), and by reason of the suppositum, with respect to that being which it hath, whereby it is a being. For that also in all things God Himself worketh, who is immediately and intimately always and everywhere present. Yet with respect to those things into which secondary agents inflow by the virtue of their proper form, God is not said to act immediately, but mediately, since He acteth also by mediating supposita, and the virtues of secondary causes. Therefore, God useth means, not because of defect of His power, but because of the abundance of His goodness: that, to wit, He might communicate also unto creatures some dignity of efficiency, and in them might render His efficiency more perceptible, 1 Samuel 14:17, “It is no restraint to the Lord to save by many or by few.” Hence God often useth those means for producing most noble effects, which of themselves have no aptness for effects of that kind, 1 Corinthians 1:27-28; Amos 5:9; 2 Chronicles 24:24. Often also He rendereth most apt means inefficacious, Psalm 33:16; 127:1-2; Hosea 4:10. Hence our faith respecteth not properly those means which God useth, nor dependeth from them, but from God alone, who can succor all our necessities either through means or without means, as it seemeth good to Him, Daniel 3:17, “Our God whom we serve is able to deliver us from the burning fiery furnace, and He will deliver us out of thine hand, O king.”

Walaeus saith: Therefore, it is to be held that God governeth all things, either immediately through Himself, or also mediately and through secondary causes. So God immediately and through Himself governeth the Angels, likewise the heavens, and the things which are in them: so speaketh Jeremiah 31:35, “Thus saith the Lord, which giveth the sun for a light by day, and the ordinances of the moon and of the stars for a light by night.” Likewise, chapter 33:25, “If My covenant be not with day and night, and if I have not appointed the ordinances of heaven and earth.” But the elementary and inferior things God ordinarily governeth through the heavens, as speaketh Job 38:33, “Canst thou set the dominion thereof in the earth?” Likewise, Hosea 2:21, “I will hear the heavens, and they shall hear the earth,” etc. Yet these things are so to be held, that God ordinarily permitteth those causes to perform their courses and operations: yet so that sometimes He useth them not, yea, He governeth the inferior things against the natures of the superior, as we see that He made the course of the sun and moon to stand still, in the time of Joshua, and of King Hezekiah; that He drew water out of the rock, in the time of Moses; that He inhibited the power of fire, in the time of Daniel; that He fed Elijah 40 days without food, etc. Which show that God ordinarily useth mediate causes, yet so that without them, and against them, He can act when He will, as when He bestowed victory through a few and through the weak, against the strong and many.

VII. Providence: Ordinary and Extraordinary

Then, ordinary, or extraordinary.

Junius saith: But ordinary, which observeth the common order of nature or of grace. Extraordinary, which existeth besides the common order, as when God fed the Israelites with manna, Deuteronomy 8:3, and with quails, Exodus 16:13, Elijah with a raven, 1 Kings 17:4. By which documents He showeth His fatherly care toward the faithful. Likewise, when He made the sun to stand still in one degree at the prayers of Joshua; and for the sake of King Hezekiah, made the shadow thereof to go back ten degrees, Joshua 10:13; 2 Kings 20:11. By which few miracles God testified that, although unto each is inbred its proper property naturally, yet they exercise not their power, save insofar as they are directed by the present hand of God, since they are nothing else than instruments, unto which God continually infuseth as much efficacy as He will, and according to His arbitrament bendeth them unto this or that action, as even the very vicissitude of times demonstrateth.

Ames saith: The providence of God is either ordinary or usual, or extraordinary and unusual. Ordinary providence is that whereby God observeth the order established in things from the beginning. The reason of which order requireth that something certain precede, and from that being posited, something else certain follow, Hosea 2:21-22, “I will hear the heavens, and they shall hear the earth; and the earth shall hear the corn, and the wine, and the oil; and they shall hear Jezreel.” That order in natural things is the law of nature common to all things, or the very nature of things as it is established by a certain order, proceeding from the power and efficacy of that never-frustrated word adhibited by God in the beginning: let it be, be it, be it so. Which, expressing the reason of the future, indicateth perpetuity and constancy, and by its power effecteth all things which are done ordinarily concerning the same things, Jeremiah 31:35-36, “The ordinances of the moon and of the stars,” and 33:20, “My covenant of the day, and My covenant of the night.”

VIII. Extraordinary Providence: Miracles

Extraordinary providence, because it is done, is called a miracle.

Ames saith: Extraordinary providence is that whereby God provideth for things beyond their usual and established order, by which manner whatsoever is effected, by metonymy of the effect, is called a miracle. A miracle is an operation above the constituted order: whence true miracles always attest the omnipotence of the doer. Hence God alone is the author of true miracles. Men can be moral causes of miracles, insofar as they obtain from God that He do them, or insofar as God useth their work as a sign or indication of the miracle to be done by Himself, yet they can no more be causes really efficient, whether principal or instrumental, of a miracle properly so called, than in creation itself.

IX. Providence Embraces All Things, Even the Smallest

Not only the great, but the smallest things also, the providence of God comprehendeth.

Walaeus saith: That even the smallest things are cared for and governed by God, very many places of Scripture also evince, as the place of the 104th Psalm. For if God reneweth the whole face of the earth, if He quickeneth all things which are in the earth, who dareth to exempt from thence single ears of corn, grains, gnats? So Nehemiah 9:6, “Thou, even Thou, art Lord alone; Thou hast made heaven, the heaven of heavens, with all their host, the earth, and all things that are therein, the seas, and all that is therein, and Thou preservest them all.” Therefore, He excepteth nothing. Yea, Christ in Matthew 10:29 thus speaketh, “Are not two sparrows sold for a farthing? and one of them shall not fall on the ground without your Father.” Luke 12:6, “One of them is not forgotten before God,” and “all the hairs of your head are numbered.” And finally, Matthew 6:28, He saith that He clotheth the lilies, and the flowers, and the grass of the field, which is cast into the oven.

Objection: But it is unworthy of the divine majesty, saith Jerome, and certain others, that He should know through every moment how many gnats are born or die, or how great is the multitude of flies, and how many fishes swim in the waters.

Response: Nay, it is unworthy of a Christian man to assert these things. For if they are created by God, is it unworthy that they should be conserved, and that their number should be before His eyes, not by laborious, but by intuitive knowledge and care? Since concerning the sparrows Christ saith that not one of them is forgotten before God: and all the hairs of your head are numbered. Secondly, the whole wisdom and power of man cannot by itself produce even one gnat, and from those smallest animals also the power and wisdom of God shineth forth.

X. Providence Governs Necessary, Contingent, and Free Things

God governeth by His providence necessary, contingent, free things, each in their manner.

Junius saith: But necessary things, which according to their nature produce a certain effect necessarily: but contingent things, which contingently. Those are called by the Philosopher definite causes: these indefinite, for this cause, book 2, Physics, chapter 5. Of those which we call necessary, there are two kinds. For some are per se and absolutely necessary, by necessity (as they call it) of the consequent; others from hypothesis, by necessity of consequence. The reason of contingent things, epi to pleiston, is attributed unto causes and effects by reason of future, casual, and fortuitous things, all future things as they are uncertain unto us, so we have them in suspense, as if they were inclined unto either part. Likewise, of all things which happen by chance or fortune, the cause is uncertain: since they can be done from more causes. For chance is not a determined cause, Aristotle, book 2, Physics, chapter 5, but a cause per accidens in those things which operate for an end, but without election: and that in things rarely contingent. But the fortuitous differeth from chance in this one thing, that it is in those things which have election. Nor doth this opinion fight with divine providence: for although with respect to the eternal decree of God as the first and remote cause all things are necessary, yet this necessity taketh not away contingency, because although the supreme cause be necessary, namely, the will of God, the effect can be contingent, because of the proximate cause contingent and indeterminate, but things are denominated more from the proximate cause, than from the remote. Add that things are more considered in themselves, than as they are in the divine mind also: as a stone will not be called immaterial, although it be immaterially in the soul or mind. Thirdly, the providence of God changeth not the natures of things, but governeth all things according to their natures: and so He governeth contingent and voluntary causes, that they act contingently, and the voluntary act voluntarily. Finally, those very things which are contingent in things, were ordered by this providence in that manner, unto which they are not contingent, but some things to others, although by the whole necessity of providence.

Calvin saith: Meanwhile, what God hath established, it is so necessary to come to pass, that yet it is neither precisely nor of its own nature necessary. An example in the bones of Christ occurreth familiarly. Since He took upon Him a body like unto ours, no sane man will deny that His bones were fragile: yet it was impossible that they should be broken. Whence again we see that not rashly in the Schools were invented the distinctions concerning necessity according to something, and absolute: likewise of the consequent, and of consequence: when God subjected the bones of His Son, which He had exempted from breaking, unto fragility, and so restrained them unto the necessity of His counsel, what could happen naturally.

Trelcatius saith: Necessity is some from the prior, or constituting; some from the posterior, or consequent; some internal, some external, some of coaction, some of immutability, some absolute, some from hypothesis, of all which the distinction in this place concerning providence is most necessary.

Walaeus saith: We affirm therefore that all actions, whether natural and necessary, or contingent and free, are subject unto divine providence. Concerning necessary and natural things, see Job 38:26 and following, where God saith that He sendeth rain upon the land, ice, frost, etc., which are generated from natural causes. Matthew 5:45, Christ saith that God maketh His sun to rise on the evil and on the good, and sendeth rain on the just and on the unjust. 1 Corinthians 15:38, the Apostle saith that God giveth to every seed his own body, as it hath pleased Him. Concerning actions which arise from contingent and freely acting causes, see Proverbs 16:33, “The lot is cast into the lap; but the whole disposing thereof is of the Lord.” Concerning the life of man and his term, Job 14:5 saith that God hath determined his bounds that he cannot pass. So Psalm 107:25, He raiseth the winds in the sea, and maketh the storm to cease. And Christ, Matthew 10:29, A sparrow falleth not to the ground without your Father. If anyone imprudently and by misfortune slayeth a man, as by a tool falling out of the handle while he was hewing wood, God is said to have delivered him into his hand: as is gathered from the collation of two places, Exodus 21:13; Deuteronomy 19:4. These are evident: but the chief question is concerning human actions, and which proceed from the will and arbitrament of men. For they will that these are so in our power, that they are not governed by divine providence. Among whom Damascene, De Orthodoxa Fide, book 2, chapter 30, thus speaketh, ta gar eph’ hēmin, e tēs pronoias epei, alla to hēmeteron autexousia: but that these actions also are subject unto divine providence, the following places demonstrate. 1 Samuel 2:6, Anna saith in her song, “The Lord maketh poor, and maketh rich: He bringeth low, and lifteth up.” Daniel 2:21, Daniel saith, “He changeth the times and the seasons: He removeth kings, and setteth up kings.” Likewise, in general concerning all human actions, Solomon, Proverbs 16:1, “The preparations of the heart in man, and the answer of the tongue, is from the Lord.” And verse 9, “A man’s heart deviseth his way: but the Lord directeth his steps.” And 21:1, “The king’s heart is in the hand of the Lord, as the rivers of water: He turneth it whithersoever He will.” Concerning spiritual and good actions, it is said especially, Philippians 2:13, “For it is God which worketh in you both to will and to do of His good pleasure.” Also concerning evil things, Scripture speaketh throughout. So the selling of Joseph by his brethren, the affliction of Job by Satan, and the deception of Ahab, likewise the persecution and crucifixion of Christ by the wicked, are throughout attributed unto God. Therefore, these things are so certain that no Christian can doubt of them, they are also full of consolation for the faithful, as the Catechism rightly collecteth.

XI. God Governs Free Actions by Premotion

God so governeth free actions, that He premoveth and determineth the secondary cause by His influx to elicit them freely.

Walaeus saith: But the most difficult and chief question is to be considered by us here, and more accurately examined, namely, concerning the manner of divine governance in the free actions of the human will, and consequently of the Angelic. But they are either good or evil. Concerning both we must treat especially in the following. Durandus seemed to think that God only sustaineth the powers, and the proximate potency of acting, as touching the first act, as they speak in the Schools: but that the second acts or the actions themselves are left by God in the arbitrament and election of men, nor is there need of any other concourse of divine governance for producing them further. But this is rejected by almost all. Because it fighteth with the nature of secondary causes, that in their actions they depend not from the first: and because every being is from God; but action, as action, is altogether to be had among beings. Then, because Scripture manifestly affirmeth the contrary, for the Apostle saith, Acts 17:28, that in Him we live, and move, and have our being. And Philippians 2:13, “It is God which worketh in you both to will,” etc. As also Solomon speaketh concerning the will of kings, who yet are wont to be most free, Proverbs 21:1, “The king’s heart is in the hand of the Lord, as the rivers of water: He turneth it whithersoever He will.” Therefore, other Christians also willingly confess this, but concerning the manner whereby God and His providence concurreth with the free actions of the human will, it is sharply disputed. Bellarmine, and certain others, establish that God exciteth the potency unto act through objects, and concurreth with the action by an indifferent influx. As the sun infloweth by a general influx into all elementary things; but its influx is determined by the proximate causes, unto this or that effect, unto bearing these or those fruits, etc. So he who bloweth the pipes in an organ, indeed by a common influx maketh that those pipes sound; but that they sound in this or that manner, the pipes have from their singular conformation. So also they will that God infloweth into human actions by a general influx, but that it is in the free will of man, to use that general influx in this or that manner, and to determine it thus or thus as it seemeth good. But as touching that opinion, we say that it in no wise satisfieth, because Holy Scripture speaketh far otherwise concerning actions also singular, both evil and good. For:

  1. Singular actions are throughout in Scripture ascribed unto God, as such, as Genesis 45:8, “So now it was not you that sent me hither, but God.” Job 1:21, “The Lord gave, and the Lord hath taken away.” Nor had He given by a general influx, but by a special gift. And so throughout.
  2. This opinion taketh away the special care of God, in benefits toward the godly, and in judgments against the ungodly.
  3. It taketh away all Christian consolation in adversities, and gratitude in prosperities, which cannot be sought from that general influx.
  4. It maketh man as free in good and evil, after the fall, as he was before the fall: which is against the whole Scripture.
  5. Finally, it enervateth all prophecies and predictions concerning future things, depending from the will of men.

We posit therefore beyond controversy, that the concourse of God unto the actions of free will is not general and indifferent, but indeed special and determining. But this special and distinct, or determining, concourse is considered either in the works of common providence, or in the actions of singular and saving grace. In the works of common providence, either the acting principles themselves are restrainingly from all action, or from this or that action, or they are excited in a peculiar manner unto acting, and are helped in good actions, or are ordered and tempered in evil, as will be seen afterward. Concerning the first, David speaketh, Psalm 33:10, “The Lord bringeth the counsel of the heathen to nought: He maketh the devices of the people of none effect.” And Job 12:17 and following, “He leadeth counsellors away spoiled, and maketh the judges fools. He removeth away the speech of the trusty, and taketh away the understanding of the aged.” And verse 24, “He taketh away the heart of the chief of the people of the earth, and causeth them to wander in a wilderness where there is no way.” Concerning the other, Jacob speaketh, Genesis 43:14, “And God Almighty give you mercy before the man, that he may send away your other brother, and Benjamin.” And concerning Saul, 1 Samuel 11:6, Scripture saith: “And the Spirit of God came upon Saul when he heard those tidings, and his anger was kindled greatly” against the Ammonites. These and infinite others which occur in Scripture evince a far other divine operation in the will of men than indifferent or such as Bellarmine, Suarez, and the like propose: as also very many promises made by God unto the Churches, and threatenings pronounced against the enemies of the Church demonstrate: of which examples will also occur in the following. But in the works of saving grace, there are also clearer places. For He circumciseth the hearts, maketh that they walk in His ways, Ezekiel 11; 36. Paul planteth, Apollos watereth, but God giveth the increase, 1 Corinthians 3:6.He worketh in the godly according to the working of His mighty power, Ephesians 1:19. And worketh both to will and to do, Philippians 2:13. It is therefore profane to say that the influx of God here is only general and indifferent. For it is efficacious, distinct, and special, otherwise man would discern himself, not God the man: the praise of conversion would be from men, and not only from God. It would also be of him that willeth and of him that runneth, not only of God that showeth mercy. Let them turn themselves into all forms, they will never escape the force of these places. But if these things are so, how is the liberty of the will preserved? How is not violence or absolute necessity inferred upon the will? How is God free from fault in evils? Unto the first it is answered, that God by His operation in the will of man taketh not away the liberty of man: because God by His providence violateth not the nature of secondary causes, but confirmeth it; as in necessary things He taketh not away necessity, but conserveth it. And here shineth forth the greatest wisdom of God, that He not only always attaineth His end, but attaineth it in that manner and with that form whereby it is to be attained. Therefore, God here, from a free cause, produceth the effect through a free action, as from a necessary cause a necessary effect. And that not every necessity fighteth with free will is demonstrated by the following arguments:

  1. Because God is necessarily good, and acteth well, and yet most freely is good and acteth well.
  2. Because the blessed Angels and men in heaven are confirmed in good; yet not the less do their actions proceed from free will. But that in the will of wayfarers also it is not necessary, is evinced from the example of the elect, who without respect to the good use of free will were elected unto eternal life.
  3. It is also demonstrated by the examples of those who, even according to the opinion of the Romanists, were certain of their salvation, such as were the Prophets, Apostles, and Evangelists, and Mary the mother of the Lord herself. For their will, the Romanists themselves confessing, was confirmed certainly and indefectibly in grace, not only by consequent determination, but also by antecedent, from which that certainty of perseverance dependeth. For an indifferent action cannot produce a certain and infallible event. Some theologians and philosophers establish that the will of man is determined by his intellect: others in some actions by the end, and yet therefore, according to their opinion, the will ceaseth not freely to will, which here willeth or willeth not. The will itself, when it determineth itself, now acteth freely. Why therefore should it not act freely, when it is determined by God, since God moveth it according to its nature? The example of Christ Himself here is evident. For He both freely obeyed, and merited all things for us through His obedience, and that necessarily and infallibly He obeyed His Father, and that from the hypothesis of the union of the human nature with the Divine, and of His sanctification through the Holy Spirit from the first moment of His conception.

Maccovius saith: Premotion unto the end consisteth either in the premotion of created things unto acting, or in concourse. This premotion is given. It is evident even from this, that all created things are called secondary causes; but whence this, unless in acting they depend from the first, which is God?

Objection: They are called secondary causes, not because they depend from God in acting; but because they were made by God.

Response: From this cause they are not called secondary causes, but dependent beings, and beings by participation, that is, because of being they are called such beings; but because of acting, secondary causes. That premotion so hath, that unless God move the secondary agents unto acting, they act not, and if He move, they cannot but act.

Objection: If God concurreth with all created things in acting, then it must be established that God feedeth in the bread, shineth in the sun, burneth in the fire, believeth and willeth in man, which was the delirium of the Manichees, and of Servetus who followed them.

Response: Distinguish between operating formally and efficiently together, and between operating efficiently. Unto operating efficiently God concurreth, for He is and can rightly be called the efficient cause, but unto operating formally and efficiently, that is, where what is the efficient cause in a diverse respect is the form, He concurreth not, because God is in no wise the form of any thing, therefore it is rightly said that God effecteth that we eat, but Himself eateth not, effecteth that we walk, but walketh not, maketh that the fire burneth, but Himself burneth not, because these effects are the formal effects of these things, of which they are said.

XII. Providence: Effective Concerning Good, Permissive Concerning Evil

Concerning good things, the providence of God is conversant effectively; concerning evil things, permissively.

Junius saith: Some means are good, both natural (as substances, motions, actions, and perfections of things) and voluntary and moral (such as are civil and spiritual virtues), others are evil, and in themselves inordinate. Both these God ordereth, both good and evil: for although evil is not good, yet the ordering of evil unto good is good. Therefore, good things He both ordereth and powerfully effecteth: evil things He ordereth and permitteth, effecteth not. But this permission is not bare, or also from impotence, as for the most part human permissions are, but from free will, which cannot be censured as unjust, since God is not bound by office to inhibit, because He is bound to no one by any law. For who hath first given to Him, and it shall be recompensed unto him again?

Piscator saith: And although God is the primary cause of every human action, yet He is not at all, nor can be called, the author of sin (which is nothing else by itself than irregularity in the action itself, declining from the law of God). For neither doth He delight in sin, yea, He abhorreth it, nor doth He sin, nor can sin: then neither doth He solicit others unto sinning either by persuasion or exhortation, or compel by force. Finally, He directeth all things unto the best end.

Maccovius saith: But while we treat here of permission, we treat not of ethical permission, which is nothing else than concession, as Matthew 8, etc., but of physical, that is, of not hindering.

Walaeus saith: But that this may be rightly explained, these two things are to be held as certain and undoubted:

  1. That all things, even evils, are subject unto the singular providence of God.
  2. That yet God is not the author of sin.

The first is proven by most certain places and examples of Holy Scripture. Genesis 45:8, Joseph saith: “So now it was not you that sent me hither, but God.” So Exodus 4:21, God saith to Moses, “I will harden Pharaoh’s heart, that he shall not let the people go.” Likewise, 1 Samuel 2:25, concerning the sons of Eli, “And they hearkened not unto the voice of their father, because the Lord would slay them.” So also 2 Samuel 12:12, concerning the incest of Absalom, “Thou didst it secretly: but I will do this thing before all Israel.” And again, 1 Kings 22:23, “Now therefore, behold, the Lord hath put a lying spirit in the mouth of all these thy prophets.” So in the New Testament, Acts 2:23, “Him, being delivered by the determinate counsel and foreknowledge of God, ye have taken, and by wicked hands have crucified and slain.” And 1 Peter 4:19, “Who suffer according to the will of God.” That God, however, is not the author of sin, is also manifest from Scripture:

  1. Because God hateth sin, Psalm 5:5; 45:7, “Thou lovest righteousness, and hatest wickedness.”
  2. From manifest places, 1 John 2:16, “For all that is in the world, the lust of the flesh, and the lust of the eyes, and the pride of life, is not of the Father, but is of the world.” Likewise, James 1:13, “Let no man say when he is tempted, I am tempted of God: for God cannot be tempted with evil, neither tempteth He any man.” So Deuteronomy 32:4,“He is the Rock, His work is perfect: for all His ways are judgment: a God of truth and without iniquity, just and right is He.” And Habakkuk 1:13, “Thou art of purer eyes than to behold evil, and canst not look on iniquity.” Finally, God punisheth sins; therefore, He cannot be the author or favorer of them. So speaketh Paul, Romans 1:18,“For the wrath of God is revealed from heaven against all ungodliness and unrighteousness of men.” And Romans 6:23, “For the wages of sin is death.”

In sin or in action which is conjoined with sin, the following are to be considered: First, the action itself, insofar as it ariseth naturally from the will, insofar it is to be established that God, as the conservator of all things, concurreth with it: for in Him we live, and move, and have our being, Acts 17:28, and all things consist by Him, Colossians 1:17. Therefore, also actions, as such. But properly perverse actions are not peculiarly attributed unto God in this respect: as Calvin, book 2, Institutes, chapter 4, section 2, rightly noteth; because every action, in itself, is good. But that the action of God can thus be rightly distinguished from the action of a vicious instrument, is wont to be best proven by the example of a lame horse, which is driven by the rider, so that the rider indeed is the cause of the motion, but not of the viciousness in the motion. Likewise, of a carcass warmed by the sun, for the sun indeed by its heat exciteth vapors from the carcass, but the stench of the vapors is not from the sun, but from the vice of the carcass. Likewise, of light passing through a colored window, etc.But further, besides these things which respect the next subject of sin, two other things are to be considered in sin: first, the viciousness itself inhering or cohering in that action: second, the order which God by His governance imposeth upon it. As touching the viciousness inhering in the action, God is rightly said to permit it, and not to effect it: but God is said to permit this, because He opposeth not an efficacious impediment to the evil will, that it act not thus or thus: or because He hindereth not that action, but suffereth that the will here freely fail. But examples of this expression occur also in the Scriptures: as Genesis 20:6, “Yea, I withheld thee from sinning against Me.” And Genesis 31:7, “God suffered him not to hurt me.” So in the contrary, Isaiah 64:3, “Wherefore hast Thou made us to err from Thy ways?” See also Job 8:4. So Psalm 81:13, “So I gave them up unto their own hearts’ lust: and they walked in their own counsels.” And in the New Testament, Acts 14:16, “Who in times past suffered all nations to walk in their own ways.” And 1 Corinthians 10:13, “God is faithful, who will not suffer you to be tempted above that ye are able.”But it is also rightly noted here that this permission is not idle, but efficacious; because God, whether He suffer His own to fall into sin for their trial, or deliver the reprobate unto their lusts, is not an idle spectator, or awaiter of what man or Satan will do, but an efficacious moderator of both. For when God suffereth His own to fall into sin for their trial, He is said to desert them with His grace: of which thing a notable example is extant, 2 Chronicles 32:31,where God is said to have left Hezekiah the king, to try him, that He might know all that was in his heart: as also the place cited above from Psalm 81:13 indicateth. But concerning the reprobate, Scripture speaketh also much more efficaciously: for He delivereth them unto a reprobate mind, Romans 1:28, and sendeth them strong delusion, in punishment of antecedent crimes, 2 Thessalonians 2:11. Then, God governeth and ordereth all that viciousness, from the beginning of the acts unto the end, and that in a secret manner, both for the good of His own, and for the punishment of the rest: as the following examples manifestly demonstrate. An illustrious example is extant in the history of Joseph and his brethren, Genesis 50:20, where Joseph thus speaketh to his brethren, “But as for you, ye thought evil against me; but God meant it unto good.” Also a notable example of this governance is extant, Isaiah 10:5 and following, where the Prophet thus speaketh: “O Assyrian, the rod of Mine anger, and the staff in their hand is Mine indignation. I will send him against an hypocritical nation, and against the people of My wrath will I give him a charge, to take the spoil,” etc. “Howbeit he meaneth not so, neither doth his heart think so,” etc. Likewise, Luke 22:22, in the example of Christ, “And truly the Son of man goeth, as it was determined: but woe unto that man by whom He is betrayed!” Hither pertaineth the example of a leader governing an army consisting of various tempers of men, and having various ends, whom yet he directeth all for the good of the commonwealth.

XIII. Avoiding Errors: Fortune and Fate

Two rocks on either side are to be avoided in this doctrine of the providence of God: fortune and fate.

Calvin saith: Those who will cast odium upon this doctrine, calumniate that it is the dogma of the Stoics, concerning fate, which also was sometimes reproved by Augustine. We, although we are unwilling to contend about words, yet we receive not the word fate: both because it is of their kind, whose profane novelties Paul teacheth us to shun: and because by the hatred thereof they endeavor to burden the truth of God. But the dogma itself is falsely and maliciously objected unto us. For we do not with the Stoics feign necessity from a perpetual connection and implicated series of causes, which is contained in nature: but we constitute God the arbiter and moderator of all things, who according to His wisdom, from the last eternity decreed what He would do, and now by His power executeth what He decreed: whence we affirm that by His providence not only heaven and earth, and inanimate creatures, but also the counsels and wills of men are governed, that they are directly carried unto the scope destined by it: What then? sayest thou, doth nothing happen by chance, nothing contingently? Response: It was truly said by Basil the Great, that Fortune and Chance are words of the Heathens, with the signification of which the minds of the godly ought not to be occupied: for if all success is the blessing of God, calamity and adversity, His curse: now in human affairs there is left no place either for fortune or for chance. And that of Augustine also ought to move us, in his books against the Academics, “It pleaseth me not,” saith he, “that I have so often named fortune, although I would not have any goddess to be understood by this name, but the fortuitous event of things in external goods or evils.” Since, however, the slowness of our mind subsideth far beneath the height of the providence of God, a distinction is to be applied which may raise it up. I will say therefore, that although all things are ordered by the counsel and certain dispensation of God, yet unto us they are fortuitous, not that we repute that fortune ruleth the world and all things, and tosseth all things up and down rashly: (for it becometh the breast of a Christian to be far from this madness) but because the order, reason, end, necessity of those things which come to pass, for the most part lieth hid in the counsel of God, and is not apprehended by human opinion: those things are as it were fortuitous which are certain to proceed from the will of God.

Maccovius saith: Yea, this necessity which we establish, plainly differeth from the necessity of the Stoic fate, first, because this necessity is said from the hypothesis of the divine will, whence it dependeth from God; but the necessity of the Stoic fate, according to the opinion of the Stoics, God is subject unto, hence not even Jupiter himself (in Homer) when he most willed, rescued his Sarpedon from the bonds. Secondly, the necessity which is of the Stoic fate floweth from the things themselves among themselves, whence the necessity which we establish dependeth not from things, which considered in themselves have partly natural potency that they can be thus or thus, as it seemeth good to God, partly subjective or obediential, and hence this necessity is called not absolute, but from the hypothesis of the divine will.

XIV. The Term of Each Man’s Life Is Appointed by Providence

Finally, also the term of the life of each man is constituted by divine providence.

Paraeus saith: Therefore, the term of the life of men, although as touching secondary causes, it can be prolonged by piety and prayers, abbreviated by the contraries: yet, as touching the counsel of God, much more than the hairs of our head, the days of our life are numbered, which no one shall pass, Isaiah 38:5; Psalm 55:23; Matthew 5:10,30; Job 14:5.

Maresius saith: Unto the same providence of God we subject not only the rising and setting of the sun and stars, but the birth and death of every one of us, whose term immovable and fixed with all its circumstances against the Arminians, Lutherans, and any Semipelagians, we affirm from invincible places of Scripture. For the days of man are determined, the number of his months are with Thee, Thou hast appointed his bounds that he cannot pass, Job 14:5. And David, concerning his generation and formation in the womb and its circumstances, “All these things are written in Thy book,” Psalm 139:16. So the time preappointed for the death of Christ is called His hour: and when His adversaries studied to take Him, yet no man laid hands on Him, because His hour was not yet come, John 7:30. Nor is it doubtful that our days are as much numbered as our hairs, Matthew 10:30. Nor is prolongation beyond the bound, but absolute longevity promised unto the godly, Exodus 20:12, “That thy days may be long upon the land:” and the days of Hezekiah were not prolonged beyond the term fixed by divine providence, Isaiah 38:5. But only beyond that time, unto which from the disposition of secondary causes and the threatening of God, which although conditional, yet was absolutely expressed, because he seemed now near unto death.
