Chapter V. On Creation

I. The Actions of God: Internal & External

The actions of God are some internal, others external, immanent, transient: of those it hath been spoken, whether personal or essential: these are common, of creation and governance; or special, of grace and redemption in Christ.

Junius saith: As the philosophers have observed a twofold kind of actions in men: one, which besides the action leaveth no work after it, pertaining unto the perfection of the agent: the other, which leaveth a work after it, and is rather the perfection of the thing made than of the agent: of which this they signified by the name of external work, that of internal: so also both may be contemplated in God, since Jehovah is, and pure act, who from eternity always understandeth, willeth, loveth, and is the creator and prince of all things. Concerning the first of which, since it hath been treated in the superior theses, the instituted order seemeth to require that we pass unto the contemplation of the external works of God, by which He hath exhibited Himself and His nature unto us to be known. Of the external works of God there are two first and chief kinds: each of which is distributed into various species. Of the former kind are those works which are called of the creation and conservation of the world in the nature of things: of the latter, the works of redemption and grace pertaining unto the Church.

Maccovius saith: It hath been treated of God, considered absolutely: it remaineth that we treat of God related unto creatures. And thus God considered is either Creator, or Redeemer, or Sanctifier, or Glorifier. But the names which indicate relation between God and the creature, as is the name of Creator, Lord, Savior, Redeemer, and the like, are predicated of God in time, not from eternity, yet so that nothing new accrueth unto Him, or mutation is made in Him, but only in the creatures themselves. The reason is, because creatures have a real relation unto God; but God undergoeth no real relation unto creatures, but only of reason, always remaining the same, and existing always outside the order of creatures: therefore God is referred unto no creature, but all creatures are referred unto God, and unto Him as unto their cause, He also hath His own order.

II. Creation: God’s Powerful & Wise Act

Creation is the most powerful and wisest action of God, whereby according to His free goodness, He produced all things out of nothing.

Junius saith: Creation is that whereby God immediately, heaven and earth, according to His eternal decree, wisely, freely, and powerfully, in the beginning, in a moment of time, out of nothing, by His word produced: and then out of them, and in them, all other things in the most fitting order, for His own glory, and the utility of man, He founded very good, Genesis 1:1, etc. The internal causes follow, namely, the matter and the form. There was no pre-existing matter, out of which God created this world, and that by the word of creating (which in its proper signification noteth production from not being unto being); for since God alone is eternal, without beginning, without end, and without succession, and immortal, 1 Timothy 6:16, and hath founded all things without exception, it followeth that not out of any eternal matter, much less out of the essence of God, but out of nothing as from a term, by the power of God they came forth, Psalm 33:9; Romans 4:17; Hebrews 11:3. And so, whatsoever is, that either immediately, as heaven, earth, angels, souls; or mediately, as others, by reason of the first matter, emanate out of nothing. For the matter of corporeal things (which Moses calleth tohu and bohu, others chaos, the Septuagint akataskeuaston, and Philosophy amorphon, aneideon, aschēmatistin, apoion have said it to have been) God first in time founded out of nothing, that out of it He might produce all visible things from privation into natural form: whence better in Scripture God is called poiētēs, who maketh something out of nothing, than by Plato and the Philosophers dēmiourgos, who only ordereth and constituteth another out of the unordered: Justin Martyr in his book of exhortation to the Gentiles. But that physical axiom, out of nothing nothing is made, because it is only physical, and agreeth with the constituted order of natural things, but not with the constituting, here it obtaineth not, where the discourse is of creation and the divine power (which the Philosophers were ignorant of). Now let us see concerning the form. Therefore, as there are two parts of this creation, the production of things, and their most wise disposition, both proper unto God, Job 9:8; Isaiah 40:22; 44:24. So also the form comprehendeth these two, and is considered internally and externally: internally, in the hidden power and nature of this universe; externally, in the manner, multitude, diversity, and beauty of all things founded, not so much for the fair face of heaven and of the rest sublunary, as for the most apt entaxion, philia, and koinōnia of all among themselves, freely proceeding from that one first and most perfect being out of His wisdom, goodness, and power: whence the world is called by the Latins mundus, from purity and neatness, Pliny book 2, chapter 3, as also by the Greeks kosmos, Etymologicon Magnum, Aristotle De Mundo, chapter 6, a to kosmein, ho ta panta en heautō echōn kekosmēmethra. Thus far concerning the causes. But the effect produced from these causes is the world, which is nothing else than the frame and system of things superior and inferior, out of heaven, earth, and the other things which exist in them, most beautifully ordered and distributed among themselves, by God, and for God compacted and conserved.

Trelcatius saith: The matter of creation ought to be distinguished in two ways: according as creation is other primary and immediate, other secondary and mediate. Nothing is said either privatively or negatively: negatively, of primary creation; privatively, of secondary.

Gomarus saith: For some creatures simply out of nothing (as from a term) and as the Apostle speaketh, Hebrews 11:3, out of things which do not appear: or, out of no matter, by reason of their whole essence, were founded by God, of which kind chiefly are heaven, earth, waters, Genesis 1:1-2; Psalm 24:2, and the primeval light, Genesis 1:3-4; 2 Corinthians 4:6. But others, in a certain respect, or by reason of their form only: as those which, not from the inbred and efficacious potency of matter, and by a certain succession (as happeneth in natural generation) but by the power of God’s omnipotence alone, and that in a moment, were produced. Such is reckoned the creation of herbs, trees, and of the human body, out of the earth, Genesis 1:11; 2:5,7, and of fishes out of the waters, Genesis 1:21.

Ames saith: Active creation is conceived by way of transient action, in which always an object is presupposed, about which the agent is conversant: yet it is not formally transient, but only virtually, since it presupposeth not the object, but effecteth it. Passive creation is conceived by way of change, which is properly called change. Creation respecteth the whole world, that is, whatsoever existeth outside God. Hence also all things which exist outside God are created, and are altogether created, that is, as well according to matter as according to form, Revelation 4:11—“For Thou hast created all things.” Colossians 1:16—“For by Him were all things created, that are in heaven, and that are in earth, visible and invisible.”

III. God Alone Is the Creator

God alone is the Creator, and can create, nor is the power of creating attributed unto any other.

Junius saith: Therefore, the efficient cause is God Elohim, the Father, the Son, and the Holy Spirit, Genesis 1:1,26; Psalm 102:26; 124:8; Colossians 1:16; Acts 17:24; 1 Peter 4:19, and that also the plural name joined with a singular verb, Genesis 1:1, we think denoteth, since creation is the work of the Trinity ad extra (as they call it), it pertaineth also unto the three Persons, yet with the manner and order in operating preserved, so that the Father through the Son and the Holy Spirit, the Son from the Father, through the Holy Spirit, John 1:3; Colossians 1:16, the Holy Spirit from the Father and the Son created, Genesis 1:2; Job 33:4; Psalm 33:6. Creation is indeed attributed unto the Father alone in the Symbol of our faith; as also redemption unto the Son; and sanctification unto the Holy Spirit, but this is because those actions are terminated most nearly in their Persons.

Trelcatius saith: The efficient cause of creation is God. For the created things were not conceived by one mind, procreated by another, as the Valentinians thought; not by another nature, which Cerinthus called now hystera, now demiourgos: not by Angels, as the Simonians thought; not by their demons, as the Philosophers; not finally by fortune, as Epicurus; but by God they were produced and disposed, Psalm 33:9; Isaiah 40:28, which besides Scripture, either the distinct operations of the three Persons, or the nature and manner of creation, or finally the reason of the principle proveth.

Calvin saith: There arose also Manichaeus with his sect, who fabricated unto himself two principles, God and the devil: and unto God indeed he attributed the origin of good things: but referred evil natures unto the devil as author. If this delirium should hold our minds entangled, the glory due unto God in the creation of the world would not stand. For since nothing is more proper unto God than eternity and autexousia, that is, existence from Himself, as I may so speak, do not they who attribute this unto the devil in some manner adorn him with the title of divinity? Now where is the omnipotence of God, if such empire be conceded unto the devil, that against His will and repugnance, he executeth whatsoever he willeth? But that which the Manichees have as their only foundation, that it is unlawful to ascribe unto the good God the creation of any evil thing, this in no wise hurteth the orthodox faith, which admitteth not that there is any evil nature in the whole world: since neither the depravity and wickedness both of man and of the devil, or the sins which arise thence, are from nature, but from the corruption of nature: nor was there anything at all from the beginning in which God did not exhibit a specimen both of His wisdom and justice.

Walaeus saith: It is here questioned, whether the creative power can be communicated unto creatures? This the Arians of old affirmed, and today also the Socinians affirm it. But this is invincibly refuted by the following arguments:

I. Because by this work God describeth Himself in Holy Scripture, and distinguisheth Himself from all creatures, and from false gods, as Isaiah 45:12—“I have made the earth, and created man upon it: I, even My hands, have stretched out the heavens, and all their host have I commanded.” Likewise, verse 18, “Thus saith the Lord that created the heavens; God Himself that formed the earth and made it; He hath established it.” Yea, afterward He exclaimeth, Jeremiah 10:11—“The gods that have not made the heavens and the earth, even they shall perish from the earth.”

II. Because He expressly claimeth this unto Himself alone, Isaiah 44:24—“I am the Lord that maketh all things; that stretcheth forth the heavens alone; that spreadeth abroad the earth by Myself.” So Job 9:8—“Which alone spreadeth out the heavens.”

III. Finally, the creation of things out of nothing is a work of omnipotence and infinite efficacy; therefore, it pertaineth unto God alone, as also Christ speaketh, Matthew 9:5—“For whether is easier, to say, Thy sins be forgiven thee; or to say, Arise, and walk?” By which words Christ indicateth that both are of the divine power alone, namely, to say these things with certain effect, and to do the same.

IV. God Created the World: Heaven & Earth

God created the world, that is, heaven and earth.

Junius saith: Therefore, the parts of the world are two, heaven and earth, as Scripture throughout proposeth them, Psalm 124:8; 134:3; Isaiah 37:16, and by these words most often synecdochically it comprehendeth also all, because they are either celestial or terrestrial, or composed of both, the sea also (unless it be expressly expressed), Proverbs 8:26-29, being comprehended under the name of earth, Psalm 146:6; Acts 14:15, because with it it maketh one globe.

Geneva Bible notes: There is one world, not many or infinite, as Democritus affirmed: and that we believe, not because of the reasons which Aristotle commemorateth in his book of Heaven: but because the Word of God calleth one founder and governor of the world, not of many worlds, one God: and saith that the Son was sent into the world, not into worlds. Yet to create many worlds would not have been more laborious unto God than only one.

V. With the World, God Created Time & Place

With the world, God created time and place, both finite.

Calvin saith: Nor indeed ought that profane whispering to move us, that it is wonderful why it did not sooner come into God’s mind to create heaven and earth, but He suffered an immense space to flow by in idleness, which He could have effected before many thousands of ages, since the duration of the world, now verging unto its last end, hath not yet attained unto six thousand years. For why God deferred it so long, it is neither lawful nor expedient for us to inquire: because if the human mind strive to penetrate thus far, it will fail a hundred times in the way: nor also would it be useful to know that which God Himself, to prove the modesty of our faith, purposely willed to be concealed. And wittily that pious old man, when a certain wanton fellow in mockery asked him what God had done before the world was created, answered that He had fashioned hell for the curious. And rightly Augustine complaineth that injury is done unto God, when a cause of things is demanded superior to His will. The same elsewhere prudently admonisheth that no less wrongly is the question moved concerning the immense spaces of times, than of places. Certainly, however widely the circuit of the heavens extendeth, yet there is some dimension thereof. Now if anyone should expostulate with God, that the emptiness exceedeth it a hundredfold, would not his petulance be detestable unto all the godly? Unto the same madness they rush, who agitate God’s idleness, because according to their judgment He did not create the world before innumerable ages.

Ames saith: That nothing, or the not being of things, preceded their being, not only in order of nature; for so they could have coexisted with God from eternity; but also in order of duration, according to our manner of conceiving. Hence that beginning, in which God is said to have created the world, was the end of that duration which nothing had, and the beginning of that which the world had. Therefore, God willed by creation to show both His perfection, that He needed no creature, or external thing; for then He would have created the world as soon as He could: and also that liberty, whereby He produced all things without natural necessity. If He had created necessarily, He would have done it from eternity, Revelation 4:11. Unto all created things time coexisteth, as appeareth from that phrase, in the beginning, for then was the beginning of time. Place also coexisteth, that is, a certain space, whereby the extension of the creature is defined, Genesis 1:22. But these are not properly created, but concreated, or annexed unto created things: because they have not absolute entity, but only relative.

Alting saith: The adjuncts of creatures are goodness, finitude, and mutability. Concerning goodness, Moses testifieth, Genesis 1:31, “All things were very good”: namely, both absolutely in their essence and qualities: and relatively unto others; unto God, whose glory they declare, Psalm 19:1, unto man, whom they attend with the office of ministry and honor, Genesis 1:28-29; Psalm 8:7, and unto other created things, which each in their order and measure respect. Finitude is in place and time. In place: because all creatures, as finite in essence, occupy some space either corporeal or spiritual, according to the condition of their nature, Genesis 1 throughout. In time: because they began in time, Genesis 1:1, and as they exist, they are subject unto the same. Finally, the mutability of creatures is, that all, because of dependence from the Creator, are mutable, He alone being simply immutable, Psalm 102:27-28. Which mutability, however, is not always reduced into act: as is to be seen in the blessed Angels, the heaven of the blessed, and others.

Maresius saith: The adjuncts of creatures are: I. Finitude of place and time, so that they neither existed from eternity, and the outermost heaven, since it is a body, and that more solid than the airy or ethereal region visible unto us, whereby it constraineth the parts of the universe in some measure among themselves, is finite and terminated by its place and space commensurate unto itself; for the world is not founded in the infinite, as Metrodorus of old would have it, but the space which it occupieth is adequate unto the located. And although the world be of indefinite magnitude unto us, according to Jeremiah 31:37, establishing that the heavens cannot be measured above, nor the foundations searched out beneath, yet in itself it is thereby finite, because besides God there is no infinite in act; because it consisteth of finite parts, because the heaven is moved in finite time, and is carried about in a sphere.

VI. All Things Created Very Good, for God’s Glory

God created all things very good; and those for His own glory.

Junius saith: The end is twofold, one of which, the other for which: that they call the end of the work, this the end of the worker, the Schools. The natural end of creation is, namely, that all things may have themselves commodiously according to nature: the heavenly and divine end, that each thing in nature may serve the glory of God.

Ames saith: Every creature was very good, because it was made neither rashly nor in vain, but for an end, which the efficient attained, Genesis 1:31—“And God saw everything that He had made, and, behold, it was very good.” 1 Timothy 4:4—“For every creature of God is good.” The goodness of the creature is that perfection, whereby it is apt for the use unto which it serveth: but that use is either particular or universal. The particular is that proper operation, unto which any thing serveth in its singular nature. The universal is the ordering of one thing with others, unto the perfection of the universe, Psalm 104; 148; Isaiah 40:13. Through this goodness all created things in their natural manner tend unto God, from whom they proceeded. For the second being is from the first, and for the first. Hence those phrases, “Of Him, and through Him, and to Him, are all things,” Romans 11:36. But natural things tend unto God in that they declare the glory of God, Psalm 19:1. II. That they afford us occasion both of knowing and seeking God, Romans 1:20; Acts 17:26. III. That they sustain our life, that we may live well unto God, 1 Corinthians 10:31.

VII. Creation in Six Days

He produced all things in six days, in each single moment of each day.

Gomarus saith: For although God by His omnipotence could have founded the universe in a moment: yet He willed to use these intervals (as Ambrose, Hexaemeron, book 1, chapter 7, rightly thinketh) whereby He might more openly declare that the creation of the world was not from eternity, but in time; and that He might retain men, in particular, by the meditation of distinct works, more attentive, as appeareth chiefly from Psalm 104; 136.

Maccovius saith: All things were made in a moment. This is evident from the book of Genesis, where at the nod of God all things are related to have immediately come forth: but also reason proveth this very thing: for as touching immediate creation, since between nothing and something there is no middle, it must be that out of nothing something is made in a moment. As touching mediate creation, there also this very thing obtaineth: for if in mediate creation the matter, that it may be made out of it, hath not natural potency, but subjective, it must therefore be that one thing is made out of another in a moment, because there all physical alteration is excluded, otherwise in that matter there would not be subjective potency, but physical and natural. Nor is it that anyone may object that God made in six days what He made: for He is said to have made in six days, not that He was continually making in each day what He made, but that in each single diverse moment of each diverse day He made what He is said to have made in each day.

VIII. The First Day: Heaven, Earth, & Light

On the first day, God created heaven and earth, and light.

Gomarus saith: But on the first day, in the beginning of things and of time, God created heaven and earth, Genesis 1:1. But the first night being ended, He produced light, verses 3-5, for the establishing of the day, succeeding unto the night opposed. But that heaven and earth, Genesis 1:1, not the rude and confused chaos of the world, and the first matter of celestial and terrestrial things, (as most think) but the very heaven and the very earth, Moses insinuateth when he expressly saith, with the demonstrative article, and as it were pointing with the finger, hassjamájim, haárets, this heaven, this earth, which are now seen.

Junius saith: But the third heaven we think was not educed out of that inform matter tohu and bohu, but together with it (for Moses by schamajim and arets, heaven and earth, Genesis 1:1, expresseth two) on the first day created out of nothing, that by situation and substance, so also by all manner of excellency, it might excel above the visible heavens, airy and ethereal; by situation, because it is constituted and formed by God above them; by substance, because subject unto no passion and mutation: by all manner of excellency, because most lucid, most pleasant, most beautiful.

Bucan saith: The creation of the world was made:

  1. By the production of the matter or seed-plot of the whole world out of nothing, made on the first day, which is properly called creation.
  2. By the information of it, and that all by the command of God alone in a moment of time. For He said, Let this or that be, and this and that was made.

Ames saith: On the first day, after the production of the highest heaven, of the Angels, and of the inform mass, by the most subtle part of that mass called upward, light was made, that is, shining fire.

Voetius saith: It hath always seemed safest unto us to establish that this light was produced and dispersed in the whole starry heaven: but on the fourth day contracted into the created stars (which to Aristotle are the denser parts of heaven), as the waters which at first were dispersed, were collected into the sea and whirlpools.

IX. The Creation of Angels

The Angels also were created by God, and placed in heaven.

Junius saith: Their creation, although Moses (unto whom it was chiefly proposed to weave through the series of visible creatures) doth not express it, yet they were created, Psalm 114:4; 1 Corinthians 1:7, and under the name of heavens, Genesis 2:1, they can be understood. II. But they are spiritual substances, simple, indivisible, subsisting only by themselves, in great number, Daniel 7:10; Matthew 26:53, and in a certain order, Ephesians 1:21; Colossians 1:16, on the first day, Genesis 1:1; 2:1; Job 38:6-7; John 1:3, by God, out of nothing, created for His ministry. And these indeed all were created good from the beginning, Genesis 1:31, (although mutably such): because, however, some of them by their free will fell from the truth and integrity, in which they were constituted by God through creation, John 8:44, hence it is that those are called evil, and were cast out of that heaven into the dark air, and adjudged unto eternal damnation, Matthew 25:41; 2 Peter 2:4; Jude 6, but others who remained in that their integrity by the grace of the Creator, and were confirmed, Matthew 6:10, are called good, and inhabit that third and highest heaven.

Maccovius saith: That Scripture maketh not mention of the creation of the Angels, (that is, it designated not the time when they were created, when the history of the creation of the world was described) this was done for a like cause, as in the histories of kingdoms, there is not always accurate mention of a neighboring kingdom: for in Scripture the history of the Church is described, and so Scripture maketh mention of the Angels insofar as it pertaineth unto the history of the Church, and yet the same Scripture teacheth sufficiently clearly that Angels are, and indeed that they were created.

Calvin saith: But this, which some restless men call into doubt, is to be held for certain, that Angels are ministering spirits, whose service God useth for the protection of His own, and through whom He dispenseth His benefits among men, and executeth also the rest of His works. For that was indeed the opinion of the Sadducees of old, that by Angels nothing is designed, save either the motions which God inspireth into men, or those specimens of His power which He putteth forth. But so many testimonies of Scripture cry out against this delirium, that it is wonderful that so gross ignorance could be borne in that people. For to omit the places which I have cited above, where thousands and legions of Angels are related, where joy is attributed unto them, where they are narrated to sustain the faithful with their hands, to carry their souls into rest, to see the face of the Father, and the like; there are others, from which it is most clearly evinced that they are truly spirits of subsisting nature.

Gomarus saith: The time of their creation is with the world, not before. First, because since this was created in the beginning, Genesis 1:1, nothing before it, save the eternal: as the periphrases of eternity, “before this world was,” John 17:5,24, “before the foundation of the world,” prove. Secondly, since in six days God made heaven, earth, sea, and all that in them is, Exodus 20:11, and that the Angels are in them, it is most certain: it is proven that they were created neither sooner nor later.

Maresius saith: Nor did they exist before that beginning of all things, which is described by Moses, Genesis 1:1, against what the Socinians pretend, that, driven from Photinianism, they may betake themselves unto Arianism; for this was the beginning of all things, before which whatsoever was, was eternal. Nor could the Angels have had their where, in which they might be, move, and act, and therefore not be.

Calvin saith: But concerning the multitude and orders, let those who dare to define see what foundation they have. Michael, I confess, is called the great prince in Daniel, and the Archangel in Jude, and Paul teacheth that it will be the Archangel who with the trumpet shall call men unto judgment. But who thence can establish the degrees of honor among the Angels, distinguish each with their insignia, assign unto each his place and station? That Dionysius, whoever he was, no one will deny that he hath disputed many things subtly and acutely in the Celestial Hierarchy: but if anyone examine it more closely, he will find that for the most part it is mere garrulity. But it is proposed unto the theologian not to delight the ears with prating, but by teaching true, certain, useful things, to confirm consciences. If thou readest that book, thou wouldst think that a man fallen from heaven relateth not what he hath learned, but what he hath seen with his eyes. But Paul, who was caught up beyond the third heaven, not only delivered nothing such, but also testified that it is unlawful for man to speak the secrets which he had seen.

Maccovius saith: But they are in place: Scripture testifieth this throughout, which teacheth that the Angels are now in heaven, now on earth, and on earth now here, now there, according as the reason of the ministry of the Angels requireth: but they are in place, yet not in the manner as bodies are, as we say that Angels are, when we deny that they are bodies. But they are not in place as bodies. Because, endowed with no bulk, they fill no place: then also they cannot be equaled unto place by any extremities (which they have not). Finally, because they need not place for their conservation. We must beware here of defining the manner and reason of the presence of the Angels, which we can comprehend only by negation. Indeed, many Angels can be in one place, but one cannot be in many distinct places. And the former is evident; for that which maketh that two can be together, is nothing else than that a body excludeth a body from place by its bulk and quantity, but where there is no bulk, no dimension, no cause can be said why they cannot be in one and the same place. The latter is not obscure. For if an Angel can be in many places, he will be able to be in all places at once, for if in two, why not also in three, or four, and so unto infinity? Certainly, that which prohibiteth that the Angelic nature should be present in all places, is the circumscription and finitude of its nature, which prohibiteth that it should not be in another place than where it is. The Angel understandeth, not through his essence, as some will; and therefore they compose, divide, and argue. For it is proper unto God to understand through His essence, nor to compose, divide, argue; and certainly since the Angels are ignorant of some things, it is that they often collect one thing from another, and therefore discourse. But it is here questioned, whence are those species, by whose intervention the Angels understand? And there are some who affirm that they were immediately implanted in them by God from the beginning: but those who assert this must equally assert that the Angel now understandeth nothing which he did not understand from the beginning, and that things were known unto the Angels before they were. Yea, truly, if they will be consistent with themselves, it is necessary that the Angels always understand, since understanding is nothing but the reception of the species. For that we do not always understand, proceedeth from nothing else, save that the species of the thing is not always in the intellect. Then, since the Angels understand through species, what prohibiteth that in those things which the Angel understandeth naturally, the species should be impressed by the thing understood itself, not indeed immediately, but yet by the intervention of some faculty in the Angel, which we are as ignorant of, as those who are by nature blind cannot perceive how anyone seeth light, and what light is.

Walaeus saith: As touching the species abstracted from things, some think that this manner of knowing pertaineth not unto the Angels, nor also unto the demons, because they have not external senses. But although they have not the instruments of external senses, as men: yet in their essence they have the power of abstracting images from things, and communicating them unto their intellect. Which appeareth from this, that in the colloquies of the saints, they perceive their voices, and answer them, as we see in the colloquies of the Angels with Abraham, and other Prophets: likewise with Zacharias the father of John the Baptist; and with Mary the mother of the Lord. Paul also testifieth, Ephesians 3, that from the benefits which God exhibiteth unto His Church, they profit, and admire them. And 1 Peter 1:12, the Apostle saith that they desire to look into these things. That the Angels know many things by extraordinary divine revelation, is clear even from the Apocalypse of John alone, in which the Holy Spirit testifieth throughout that Christ received it from His Father, who alone was found worthy in heaven and in earth to open that book of divine providence, and to make it manifest through the Angel unto the Apostle John, that he might then communicate it unto the Churches, see chapter 1:1; 5:2. See also Daniel 8; Zechariah 2, etc. In the third manner, namely, by ratiocination, whether they understand, is disputed by the Schoolmen. And although we grant that their ratiocination is much sharper and more agile than that of men, as the ratiocination of one man is much more agile than that of another, yet that hindereth not that true ratiocination be attributed unto them. For they collect the effects of divine mercy from their causes, and contrariwise: as is to be seen in diverse Canticles. So Isaiah 6:3, “And one cried unto another, and said, Holy, holy, holy, is the Lord of hosts: the whole earth is full of His glory.” So Luke 2:14, they sing, “Glory to God in the highest, and on earth peace, good will toward men.” So throughout in the Apocalyptic doxologies, they collect effects from causes, and causes from effects. The place Ephesians 3:10 cited before also showeth this. How many, or what, are the orders of the Angels, the Schoolmen anxiously define after Dionysius the Areopagite and Damascene. They establish nine choirs of them, and those divided into three triads. In the first triad they place the Seraphim, Cherubim, and Thrones. In the second, Dominions, Virtues, and Powers. In the third, Principalities, Archangels, and Angels. From these the Thomists will that Angels are set over single men; Archangels, over provinces: Principalities, over the whole human race: Virtues, over bodies: Powers have empire over evil spirits: Dominions care for good spirits. The other three always adhere most nearly unto God and His throne, and receive visions and commands immediately from God, which they then suggest unto the rest inferior. These are refuted from this: first, because more names of Angels are found in Scripture than these nine: as are Elohim, Watchers, Sons of God, Angels of light, Morning stars, etc. If therefore from the diversity of names the number of orders is to be constituted, more are to be established. Secondly, all are called Angels, that is, messengers, and all are called ministering spirits, sent forth to minister for them who shall be heirs of salvation; therefore, none of them is excluded from that mission, Hebrews 1:14. And the Angels of the little ones always behold the face of the Father in heaven, as Christ testifieth, Matthew 18:10. Therefore, it is not the privilege of a few only: as also in the Apocalypse throughout it is indicated: likewise Isaiah 6:2; Ezekiel 1:5; Daniel 7:10. And they also agree not in this order, because Gregory in Homily 34 explaineth those orders otherwise than Dionysius: therefore, one or the other necessarily erreth. We conclude therefore, with Augustine, chapter 10 to Orosius, against the Origenists, that there are seats, dominions, powers, etc., I most firmly believe, and that they differ among themselves in some manner, I hold with undoubted faith: but what that is, and how they differ, I know not.

Calvin saith: But whether single Angels are attributed unto single faithful ones, for their defense, I dare not affirm for certain. Certainly, when Daniel introduceth the Angel of the Persians and the Angel of the Greeks, he signifieth that certain Angels are destined as presidents over kingdoms and provinces. Christ also, when He saith that the Angels of the children always behold the face of the Father, insinuates that there are certain Angels unto whom their safety is committed: but from thence I know not whether it ought to be collected that each one hath his own Angel. This indeed is to be held for certain, that not only unto one Angel each one of us is a care: but all with one consent watch for our salvation; for it is said of all the Angels together, that they rejoice more over one sinner that repenteth, than over ninety and nine just persons which need no repentance. Also of more Angels it is said, that they carried the soul of Lazarus into Abraham’s bosom. Nor in vain doth the minister of God show unto Elisha so many fiery chariots, which were peculiarly destined for him. There is one place which seemeth somewhat clearer than the rest for confirming this. For when Peter, led out of prison, knocked at the door of the house in which the brethren were gathered, since they could not suspect that it was he, they said, It is his Angel. This seemeth to have come into their mind from the common conception, that unto single faithful ones their Angels are assigned as presidents. Although it can also be answered thus, that nothing hindereth but that we may understand any one of the Angels, unto whom the Lord had then committed the care of Peter, yet that he was not therefore his perpetual keeper: as commonly they imagine two Angels, good and evil, as diverse genii, to be attributed unto each. Nor yet is it worth the labor anxiously to investigate that which it little concerneth our salvation to know. For if it satisfieth not anyone, that all the orders of the heavenly host keep watch for his salvation, I see not what he can profit from this, if he understand that one Angel is peculiarly given unto him as a keeper. But those who restrict unto one Angel that care which God beareth concerning each one of us, do great injury unto themselves and unto all the members of the Church: as if those auxiliary forces were promised in vain, by which, being encompassed and fortified on every side, we may fight more courageously.

Voetius saith: There are among ours, who, the rest of the consentients being postponed, admit as a probable opinion, concerning a good Angel keeper assigned unto single men, at least unto the faithful, among whom is Zanchius, De Operibus Creationis, book 3, chapter 13. And lately Alstedius hath defended that opinion ex professo, in the supplement to Chamier, De Ecclesia, book 5, chapter 7. Most recently also Vossius indicateth not obscurely that he inclineth unto the same, book 1, De Idololatria, chapter 7. Grotius, in his notes on Matthew 18:10, seemeth to stand for this opinion: yet in the end he deserteth it, or leaveth it in the midst. Both seem to be moved in some manner by the authority of the Fathers (some of whom they cite there, more the Romanists). We, however, embrace the opinion of Calvin, in Institutes, book 1, chapter 14, section 7, and in his Commentaries on Psalm 91 and Matthew 18, and of other Reformers, who reject that opinion as vain and curious: and we think that unto the ancient Fathers, from Platonic philosophy, and the mythological theology of the Gentiles, something hath adhered here.

X. The Second Day: The Expanse of Heaven

On the second day, God created the expanse of heaven, rakiah.

Walaeus saith: On the second day was created the firmament or expanse (as Junius translateth the word rakiah) which divided the waters above from the waters below. By which expanse is understood by Junius and Tremellius that space which is intermediate between the true heaven and the earth: but by the waters above the expanse the same understand the frame of the clouds. But that opinion hath this difficulty, that those waters are here said to have been above the expanse, and that on the fourth day in that expanse the stars were created. And elsewhere Scripture by that expanse understandeth the heaven itself, not the air: as Psalm 105:2—“Who stretcheth out the heavens like a curtain.” Isaiah 42:5—“Thus saith God the Lord, He that created the heavens, and stretched them out.” We, however, do not curiously treat here of those superior waters, which some think to be the crystalline heaven above this visible heaven. This at least is read in Psalm 104:3, that God is said to cover the superior parts with waters. And Psalm 148:4, those superior waters are said to praise God: that is, to supply also a notable matter of divine praise. For that some think that true waters are there constituted by God, that from the heaven and seat of the blessed they might ward off the heat of the element of fire, also of the sun and of the rest of the stars, seemeth not probable unto us: therefore, we prefer here to suspend judgment.

XI. The Third Day: Separation of Land & Water, & Plants

On the third day, the earth was separated from the waters, and plants and trees were produced in it.

Ames saith: On the third day, the grosser parts of the mass were so distributed, that the waters, for the most part, being collected into their receptacles, the sea stood apart, and the earth appeared, adorned with herbs and trees.

Maresius saith: And He placed the earth itself in the center of the world, immovable. Whatever the Copernicans establish, concerning its continual motion and the consistency of the sun, not only all sense, but also Scripture crying out against it, for God hath founded the earth upon its bases that it should not be moved, Psalm 104:5, and hath established the world that it should not be moved, Psalm 93:1. Unto which add the miraculous standing of the sun and moon, Joshua 10:12-14, “And the sun stood still in the midst of heaven, and hasted not to go down about a whole day:” and the retrogradation of the sun, when the shadow went back ten degrees, 2 Kings 20:10. Also by reason, according to which, since it occupieth the very middle and center of the universe, it is more fitting that it stand still, than be moved.

XII. The Fourth Day: Luminaries in Heaven

On the fourth day, He constituted the luminaries in heaven, the Sun, the Moon, and the stars.

Walaeus saith: On the fourth day, God created the luminaries of the Sun and Moon, and the other stars, namely, out of that lucid matter which was produced on the first day. These are said to be made to distinguish between day and night, namely, by their ordinary course, which in the preceding days had been done by the extraordinary circumlation of light. These also are said to be made for signs, and for seasons, namely, of days and years, as is immediately added. But for signs of feasts and solemnities, which God was afterward to institute, and also of other natural effects. Concerning which Job 38:33, God Himself thus speaketh, “Knowest thou the ordinances of heaven? canst thou set the dominion thereof in the earth?” Not indeed of such ordinances or effects which are from the arbitrament of men, much less which depend from the singular providence of God in singular facts: for God in the Prophets reproveth those who endeavored to signify these from the course of heaven.

Geneva Bible notes: The other bodies are variously moved about the earth, by motion either straight, as the other elements, or spherical, as the celestial orbs. But the earth itself continually standeth, saith Solomon, Ecclesiastes 1:4. Therefore, that opinion of certain of the ancients is reproved (which lately Nicolaus Copernicus hath endeavored to demonstrate, rather to make trial of his wit, than that he so thought) who said that the earth alone is movable, the other bodies about it fixed and immovable.

Voetius saith: It shall suffice us to indicate from the Scriptures immovable arguments, by the example not only of theologians, but also of astronomers. These Peucerus placeth in the first rank in the praised treatise, De Circulis Caelestibus, folio 6. “Neither by straight motion,” saith he, “nor by circular is the earth moved, but resteth in the midst of the world, both the sacred letters confirm, and physical reasons approve.” The same hath most recently been performed by David Christianus, Professor of Mathematics at Marburg, book 1, Systema Geographicum, chapter 8, page 126, where he thus determineth: “But this opinion (namely, of Copernicus, Galileo) is adverse unto the sacred letters, unto sound reason, and experience, yea, it overthroweth the foundations of Astronomy and Geography.” We bring three classes of arguments, from the Sacred. The first hath those places which say that the earth resteth, Psalm 104:5; Ecclesiastes 1:4; Job 26:7; 38:4,6. The second containeth the places which attribute motion unto the Sun, Psalm 19:6-7; Ecclesiastes 1:5-6. The third, those places Joshua 10; Isaiah 37:8, which relate the standing and retrocession of the sun among miracles: therefore, that motion is natural.

XIII. The Fifth Day: Fish & Birds

On the fifth day, God produced fishes and birds, inhabitants of the water and air.

Walaeus saith: On the fifth day, God created all aquatic and creeping things out of the waters, then the flying things not out of the waters, as verse 20 at first sight seemeth to indicate; for Genesis 2:19 expressly saith that God formed out of the earth every beast of the field, and every fowl of the air: but that is to be understood of the flight of birds, also above the waters: or it is to be restricted unto those flying things which are called amphibia: and so there is a progress from the more imperfect unto the more perfect.

XIV. The Sixth Day: Terrestrial Animals & Man

On the sixth day, the terrestrial animals; finally, man was formed in a singular manner, after the image of God.

Ames saith: In this order, the wisdom, power, and goodness of God most clearly shine forth. Wisdom:

I. That the simple elements were created before the elementary or concrete and composite.

II. That among the simple, the more perfect first, which more nearly approach unto the nature of God.

III. That those were created first which have only being, then those which besides being have also life: afterward those which besides being and life have also sense, then finally those which besides being, life, sense, have also reason.

IV. That among the simple, there was progress from the more perfect unto the less perfect: but among the composite, from the less perfect unto the more perfect, from plants unto man.

The power of God shone forth, in that He created plants, herbs, trees before the sun and stars, which are wont to have the power of causes in their production.

The goodness of God shone forth, in that He created first the dwellings, then the inhabitants, first the food then the animals, first those things which would be of use unto man, then man himself.

Man, as he was the last of creatures, so he was the compendium of all creatures, both immediately and mediately perfect, participating of their nature as touching the soul, and of these as touching the body. He was the end of the mediately perfect, and therefore in them, and above them, as touching intention. Hence he is said to be created in another manner than the other creatures. For they were produced by the word alone, “Let there be light, let there be an expanse:” but man as it were by greater counsel and deliberation, “Let us make man,” Genesis 1:26. For the body was first prepared, and afterward the soul was inspired, Genesis 2:7. The body out of elementary matter, but the soul out of no pre-existing matter, but by the power of God was immediately produced.

Professor saith: The Academics indeed attribute the special seat of the soul unto the head or brain, the Peripatetics and Stoics unto the breast or heart. We, following Holy Scripture and almost the ancient fathers, embrace the latter opinion, and establish that the soul is in the heart as in the center and recess according to essence (whence also the heart is put for all the faculties of the soul, namely, wisdom, will, and affection): but that it exerciseth its powers in the body, and that through its proper instruments, as through the brain to understand.

Walaeus saith: All the Orthodox rightly establish that the soul is immortal in itself: not, because it hath immortality from itself, or because the soul cannot be reduced into nothing by God: for so God alone is immortal, as the Apostle testifieth, 1 Timothy 6:16. But, because its essence is simple, and therefore cannot be dissolved into the parts constituting it, as all bodies and elementary things: then, because the soul in itself hath no physical privation or principles mutually contrary, from whose conflict destruction followeth in elementary things.

Maccovius saith: But whether our soul is inspired in the same manner as the soul of Adam was created out of nothing, or whether it is propagated from the seed of the parents, or by traduction, is questioned: We establish that the rational soul is infused into the embryo, not indeed thurathen epeisienai, as Aristotle willeth, book 2, Generation of Animals, chapter 3, but we think that it is formed by God Himself in the very fetus and rudiment of the human body.

Voetius saith: The reasons unto us are these:

I. From Genesis 2:7, where the soul is not raised out of its matter passing, namely, the earth (which was analogically then the seed or seed-plot of the human body), but formed by God, united unto the body. But this history of the creation of man is to be compared with the creation of the rest of the animals.

II. From Ecclesiastes 12:7 with Hebrews 12:9, unto which may be added Zechariah 12:1. In which places something peculiar is attributed unto God, in the production of souls; opposite unto the production of other living things.

III. From the conception of Christ, who, since He was made partaker of flesh and blood in like manner as we, Hebrews 2:11-15, had a soul from creation, not from traduction. For if He had had it from traduction, certainly from the father (for so the author of traduction, by most, alone, at least the primary by all is established), but He had not a father. And although He had not taken the soul from the seed of the mother, yet He was the son of His mother, and of David, of Abraham, of Adam.

IV. No corporeal virtue can attain unto the effecting of a spiritual substance: because no virtue acteth beyond its kind. But the intellective soul exceedeth the whole kind of corporeal substance.

V. If the generation of the body be the producing cause of the soul, therefore the dissolution or corruption of the body will be the corrupting cause; but the latter is absurd: therefore. But the traduction of the seed is the proper cause of the generation of the body.

VI. Every natural agent, which acteth dependently from matter, produceth nothing save what dependeth from matter: but the soul dependeth not from matter, because it can subsist outside the body.

VII. There is added the consent of all the Fathers, (one or two excepted), and of the ancient Philosophers; whose words see in Zanchius; unto these of the writers of the middle age; and of the Schoolmen; finally of the more recent Theologians, Philosophers, Physicians, both Romanists and those who have departed from the Papacy; except Sohn, volume 2, page 563, Peucer, Tractatus de Ortu Animae, Combach, Anthropologia, chapter 2, Timothy Bricht, in the Physics of Scribonius, page 18, and all who call themselves Lutherans, whom the authority of Luther hath driven into the contrary opinion or at least into suspension. Among ours, Musculus, in Loci Communes, and Piscator, Disputationes de Peccato, leave it in the midst. Martyr thinketh that traduction cannot be refuted from Scripture.

Walaeus saith: We say that the image of God consisteth in those gifts and privileges, whereby man excelleth the rest of the animals. As David in Psalm 8 singularly extolleth the kindness of God toward men. Which privileges are first to be considered in the essential properties of man, but in the spiritual and immortal soul, likewise in the intellect, free will, and power corresponding thereunto. Wherefore men, even unregenerate, are called the offspring of God, Acts 17:28, and Genesis 9:6, the cause why the life of a murderer is to be taken away, is rendered by God Himself: because the image of God is violated in him. Which reason of the law, as also the law, always endureth. Then these privileges are to be constituted in original wisdom and righteousness. As Paul speaketh, Ephesians 4:24—“And that ye put on the new man, which after God is created in righteousness and true holiness.” And Colossians 3:10—“And have put on the new man, which is renewed in knowledge after the image of Him that created him.” Unto all which, as unto certain pillars, that dominion resteth, wherewith God adorned man above the rest of the animals, 1 Corinthians 11:7 and following. But since among the chief parts of the image of God is original righteousness, a few things are to be added concerning its nature. Original righteousness is called by theologians that holiness of all the parts and faculties of the soul, which man received from God in his first origin: and it is opposed unto original sin, with which man is born after the fall. We understand therefore by that righteousness the whole integrity of all the faculties of the human soul; namely, in the mind light and wisdom; in the will and affections conformity with the law of God. For the mind obeyed God, the will the sound mind, the affections right reason in all things. Of this whole rectitude the Wise man speaketh, Ecclesiastes 7:29—“God hath made man upright; but they have sought out many inventions.” Of the light and wisdom of the mind Paul speaketh, Ephesians 5:8—“For ye were sometimes darkness, but now are ye light in the Lord.” And 2 Corinthians 3:18—“But we all, with open face beholding as in a glass the glory of the Lord, are changed into the same image from glory to glory.” As also Colossians 3:10 in the place cited above. Of the holiness in the will and affections Paul speaketh in the place cited, Ephesians 4:24, and elsewhere in Scripture. Certain Schoolmen think that man was created in pure naturals, without original righteousness: but that God superadded this supernatural grace unto man already created, either from grace, or from the first merit of his will. But this opinion is refuted by the places produced before: namely, that man was also created upright, and after the image of God, which consisted in wisdom, righteousness, and holiness. Then, it also fighteth with other places of Scripture, which testify that no one first gave anything unto God that it should be recompensed unto him, Romans 11:35. Certain Remonstrants with Bellarmine, and some other Romanists, that they may maintain that all conflict of the flesh and spirit in man now regenerate is not sin, establish that original righteousness was wholly undue, and superadded unto nature by God, in the first creation of man, that it might be a bridle of inordinate appetites, which from the power of matter produced out of nothing inhered in the principles constituting man: since the inferior faculties by themselves were carried unto inferiors; the superior unto superiors. As rust is contracted by iron, not from the intention of the smith, but from the nature and power of the matter, besides the intention of the smith. But this opinion is little distant from blasphemy. First, because it establisheth that inordinate appetite was in man from the power of creation, which cannot but be habitual sin, since it is against the law, “Thou shalt not covet.” In which law not only the act, but also the habit is forbidden. For restraint taketh not away the viciousness itself or habit, but only the act, that it break not forth. Then, because, if from the necessity of matter those appetites inhere in those diverse principles, then something on is established in things, which is not from God: or if those contrary appetites also inhere in those principles from God, it presupposeth that God could not so moderate those appetites in those diverse principles, or create them with such mode and measure, that they should not be adverse among themselves, or should mutually serve one another: which is false, as is clear even from the beasts, whose appetite is not unlimited, but limited, and proportioned unto their nature in each species. Thirdly, John in the First Epistle, chapter 2:16, testifieth that concupiscence (namely, vicious) is not of the Father, but of the world, “The world passeth away, and the lust thereof: but he that doeth the will of God abideth for ever.” Therefore, it is not from the creation of those principles. Fourthly, the appetite unto inferiors by itself is not sin, which was also in Christ Himself, but only insofar as it is inordinate. But insofar as it is inordinate, it is sin, and against the tenth commandment. Which cannot be from God. Finally, if man could be created such as he is now born, then he could be created a child of wrath, a slave of Satan, hateful unto God, Ephesians 2. But that this is absurd is clear of itself.

Question: It is a question, whether original righteousness is to be called natural or supernatural unto man?

Answer: But that this question may be rightly understood, the word natural is to be distinguished. Aristotle, book 1, chapter 2, of Physics, bringeth some significations of the word natural:

I. Nature is taken for the whole essence of the thing.

II. For the single essential parts: but more chiefly for the form of the thing, which is the active principle: less chiefly for the matter, which is the passive principle of things.

III. For the natural and reciprocal properties of things: as it is natural unto man to laugh, unto birds to fly.

IV. For the inbred faculty of the thing, which resulteth from the principles rightly constituted, and helpeth and perfecteth nature, as health is natural unto the body, disease preternatural.

In the last manner we say that original righteousness was natural unto man, both because God communicated it in nature and with nature, and because it was to be communicated unto posterity by natural propagation, if Adam had remained. But it can also be called supernatural, with respect unto the objects: namely, of God and divine things, and with respect unto the end, which was the glory of God and eternal life.

But this is proven:

Reason I. Because the contrary is preternatural, as shown above.

II. Because conformity unto the law of nature is natural. But original righteousness is the conformity of man unto the law of nature: which is demonstrated thence, because Paul saith, Romans 2:14, that the Gentiles do by nature the things contained in the law. Therefore, the law of God is inbred in nature, and the perfect law was more perfectly inbred.

III. Because by natural generation it was to be propagated into the whole human race according to the institution of God; and the contrary thereof is called natural unto corrupt man.

IV. Because the desire of eternal blessedness is inbred in the nature of man: therefore, also the means of attaining unto it were natural, and granted unto man.

Trelcatius saith: Natural is said equivocally either causally, that which is from nature; or subjectively, that which is in nature; or finally by propagation, that which is communicated with nature: so also supernatural is said, either that which our nature according to itself cannot contain, and therefore is above the condition of nature; or in which nature acteth or operateth in no manner, but grace alone; or finally that which is not propagated by natural reason, but is communicated by a divine manner, above the order of nature. Original righteousness is called natural, both because God communicated it in nature and with nature, and because it was to be communicated unto posterity by natural propagation, if Adam had remained.

Maccovius saith: Theologians well distinguish that the image of God consisteth in nature, righteousness, and dominion, therefore it is not a simple being, but an aggregate, which in its compass comprehendeth many beings. That the image of God consisteth in righteousness is evident from Ephesians 4:24; Colossians 3:10. The Socinians will not admit this, nor of nothing, for if they should concede this, then they would be compelled to confess that man is not now born such as he was first created, but wanting this image, and therefore to be born with original sin, although original sin consisteth not only in the want of original righteousness, yet it consisteth also in this.

XV. Man Placed in Paradise

And man was placed in Paradise, where among other things, there was the tree of knowledge of good and evil, and the tree of life.

Professor saith: Therefore, the state of man was most blessed, unto which acceded external dignity, his placement in the garden or paradise, planted by God, which is graphically described by Moses as touching place, situation, and other circumstances. We, omitting what is disputed concerning its rivers, extent, and duration, think that it is to be placed in or near Mesopotamia, toward the Tigris and Euphrates. Moreover, He adorned it with every kind of most beautiful herbs and trees, which might serve for food and for the use of pleasure. Among which the Tree of Life, and of the knowledge of good and evil. That indeed so called, not because it had life inbred, or by itself, as a certain panacea, bestowed it upon the body or soul, as the Schoolmen, after certain Fathers, think: but rather by a sacramental reason, because it sealed both, yea, shadowed that spiritual which was to follow, Revelation 2:7. But this of the knowledge of good and evil named, not because knowledge inhered in it (which is absurd), or had an occult inbred power of effecting it, or of sharpening the wit, as Josephus would have it: but because it was a tree, by divine institution, sacramental, whereby man was commanded not to will to be wise beyond what God willed, or to measure good and evil by his own wit: and whereby God, trying his obedience by the eating of the fruit, he was to feel and experience the good whereof he was to be deprived, and the evil into which he was to fall. Unto this garden God placed man, as a lord in his palace, to dress it, and to keep it, Genesis 2:15. To dress it indeed with pleasant and holy work, without weariness and tedium, to keep it in that state in which it was, and from the incursion and overthrow of animals. And this placement of the first parents in Paradise pertaineth also in its manner unto their progeny.

XVI. Man Would Never Have Died in His Original State

And man would never have died in that his state.

Walaeus saith: Concerning the body, it is questioned whether it was created by God immortal? Socinus denieth this, and the Catechism of the Socinians; the Gentile Philosophers deny it. But we indeed confess that it consisteth of elements, into which it can be resolved; yet we assert that man would never have died, if he had not sinned. This is proven by evident places of Scripture. For death is called the wages of sin, Romans 6:23, and the body is said to be dead because of sin, Romans 8:10, and again, by one man death, and by one man the resurrection from the dead, 1 Corinthians 15:21. As also the divine threatening invincibly proveth it, Genesis 2:17—“In the day that thou eatest thereof, thou shalt surely die.”
