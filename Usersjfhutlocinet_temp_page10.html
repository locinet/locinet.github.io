Chapter X. On the Calling of Man to Salvation

I. The Benefits Following from Christ’s Offices

After the offices of Christ, there follow the benefits which, through the mediation of calling (as the first among them in execution), He confers, as it is written, Romans 8:30, “Whom He did predestinate, them He also called.”

Calvin saith: Now it is to be seen how those good things which the Father bestowed upon His only begotten Son come unto us, not for private use, but that He might enrich the poor and needy. And first it is to be held that as long as Christ is outside us, and we are separated from Him, whatsoever He suffered and did for the salvation of the human race is useless and of no moment unto us. Therefore, that He may communicate with us what He hath received from the Father, He must become ours and dwell in us. Therefore, He is also called our Head, and the firstborn among many brethren; we also in turn are said to be ingrafted into Him, and to put Him on; because nothing of all that He possesseth pertaineth unto us (as I have said) until we grow together with Him into one. And although it is true that we obtain this by faith, yet since we see that not all promiscuously embrace the communion of Christ which is offered through the Gospel, reason itself teacheth us to ascend higher and to inquire concerning the secret efficacy of the Spirit, by which it is that we enjoy Christ and all His benefits. And that this matter, most worthy of knowledge, may be more certainly clear, it is to be held that Christ came peculiarly furnished with the Holy Spirit, namely, that He might separate us from the world and gather us into the hope of eternal inheritance.

Junius saith: The first step and manner of God’s actions in the execution of predestination and the manifestation of that grace is Calling: therefore called by Augustine the entrance unto salvation and the first approach unto faith, in Book 1, Ad Simplicium, Question 2.

Ames saith: Thus was redemption; the application of the same redemption followeth. This application is that whereby all those things which Christ as Mediator did and doth are made actually efficacious in certain men. This application is attributed by singular appropriation unto the Holy Spirit, 1 Corinthians 12:13, “For by one Spirit are we all baptized into one body.” Yet it dependeth:

  1. Upon the decree and donation of the Father, whereby He gave certain men unto Christ to be redeemed and saved, John 6:39, “And this is the Father’s will which hath sent Me, that of all which He hath given Me I should lose nothing.” For all and only those whom the Father gave unto Christ come unto Him, verse 37.
  2. Upon the intention of Christ, whereby He destined His satisfaction for the good of those who were destined unto Him by the Father, John 17:9, 11-12, 19, “I pray for them: I pray not for the world, but for them which Thou hast given Me; for they are Thine.”
  3. Upon the acceptation of the Father, whereby He accepted and ratified that satisfaction for the reconciliation and salvation of the same, 2 Corinthians 5:19, “To wit, that God was in Christ, reconciling the world unto Himself, not imputing their trespasses unto them.”

This transaction between God and Christ was a certain previous application of our redemption and liberation unto our Sponsor, and unto us in Him; which hath the reason of a certain efficacious exemplar for the accomplishing of that secondary application in us, so that that is the representation of this, and this is produced by the power of that. Hence our liberation from sin and death was not only established in the decree of God, but also granted and communicated unto Christ, and unto us in Him, before it is perceived by us, Romans 5:10, “For if, when we were enemies, we were reconciled to God by the death of His Son, much more, being reconciled, we shall be saved by His life.”

II. The Nature of Calling

That calling is the action of God whereby He inviteth man out of the state of sin and misery, through the preaching of the Gospel, unto the kingdom of His Son and of salvation.

Junius saith: But this calling is the gracious action of God in Christ Jesus, whereby according to His good will He calleth certain men outwardly in common, or inwardly singularly, unto the knowledge and communion of adoption and of eternal life in heaven, for the declaration of His glory.

III. External and Internal Calling

One calling is only external, another also internal; one only illuminating, the other also converting; one common to many, the other singular; one not efficacious, the other efficacious.

Trelcatius saith: Of this calling, the notion is wont to be various, according as either from the manner of calling it is distinguished into internal and external, or from the diverse condition of the efficient cause into common and singular, or finally from the effect of calling into efficacious and not efficacious. The efficacy of calling is one external, another internal. External is when the external senses and corporeal things are touched and moved by the external ministry. Internal is when the intellect and will are touched and moved. Each efficacy again is double: one saving, proper to the elect; the other not saving, but previous according to the common order and respecting the called in common.

Junius saith: And so the form is double: one common, and the other particular and singular. Common calling is that whereby God outwardly calleth the common of the human race by the Word, the Sacraments, and the common ministry of the Church and of its pious members, according to the common and public manner of calling, and commandeth them to be converted from darkness unto light, and from the power of Satan unto Himself, as Paul speaketh, Acts 26:18, according to the pleasure of His providence. Singular calling is that whereby God inwardly calleth the common of His elect, and each of them, not only by the Word, the Sacraments, and the singular ministry of the Church and of its pious members, but also by the internal testimony and allocution of the Spirit of sanctification in their minds, and teacheth and giveth them to be converted from darkness unto light, and from the power of Satan unto Himself, according to the good pleasure of His predestination in Christ Jesus. This same calling is not unfitly called particular, as opposed unto the common, because through this internal calling God excepteth from the common of men or of the world a certain part or particular body, according to the election of grace; as Christ said, Matthew 20:16; 22:14, “Many are called, but few are chosen,” that is, gifted with singular calling according to His eternal and singular election. These two kinds of callings differ in that this is from singular grace, that from common grace; this is of the pious, namely, the elect, that is of many, namely, of men promiscuously; this hath the internal efficacy of the Spirit of sanctification, that hath the external or common, or certainly not so acting in the hearers that they attain saving sanctification from it; this is of mere salvation and perfection, that is of inchoation and only previous to salvation; finally, this calling is of the Church, whereby the invisible living inwardly in the body of Christ from the Spirit of Christ; that is of the Church in common, whereby it is visible, having in the body of Christ also adnascent and not living members from the Spirit of Christ.

IV. No Universal Grace of Calling

The grace of calling is one singular, another common; there is no universal grace.

Spanheim saith: But for the good constitution of the state of the controversy, it must be foreknown: I. That it is not inquired, whether any universal grace of God toward men be, for no man is, who oweth not some grace to God, and who ought not to impute whatsoever either he is, or hath, or can, to the grace of his Creator. II. Neither is it inquired, whether the grace either of justification, or of sanctification, or of glorification be universal; for all are compelled to confess that the sphere of this grace is more contracted. III. Neither is it inquired, whether all men absolutely, whether they believe, or believe not, are elected to eternal salvation, which was the figment of Samuel Huber, sometime a disturber of the Helvetic Churches, and constantly exploded by those who profess the name among the Evangelicals. IV. But the question is: whether the salutary grace of God be universal, or: whether there ought to be attributed to God a universal purpose of having mercy upon the whole race of mankind fallen into sin, of destining Christ the Mediator to them all and every one, of redeeming them all through him, and of calling them to the salutary communion of his benefits, and so of saving all men universally through Christ the Saviour to be apprehended by faith? But the sum of our opinion is, that neither the will of having mercy upon all and every man unto salvation can be ascribed to God: nor the will of calling all and every one to salvation through Christ; and so universal grace neither ought to be established, nor can be defended.

By Authority: I. Because Scripture testifieth, that God in the Old Testament declared his words to Jacob, his statutes and judgments to Israel, hath not done so to any nation, therefore they have not known those judgments. Ps. 147:19,20. Neither is there σύκρισις or ἀντίθεσις of manner and manner, but of thing and thing. II. Because Christ testifieth Matt. 11:25, that God hath hidden his mysteries from the wise and prudent, and hath revealed them unto babes. III. Because the same testifieth, Matt. 22:14, that many only are called, not all. IV. Because Paul teacheth Acts 14:16, that God in times past suffered all nations to walk in their own ways, although he left not himself without witness, leading indeed unto God the creator, not unto God the redeemer, and representing τὸ γνωστὸν θεοῦ, not σοφίαν τὴν ἐν μυστηρίω, and so sufficient only unto conviction, not indeed unto conversion. V. Because to go into the way of the Gentiles, and to enter into the cities of the Samaritans was long forbidden to the Apostles, Matt. 10:5. And when Paul and Timothy endeavoured toward Bithynia, the Spirit of Jesus suffered them not to go. Acts 16:7.

By reasoning also the particularity of calling can be proved: I. Because neither in the O.T. nor in the N. was calling ever universal. Not in the Old, as appeareth from the economy or dispensation of divine knowledge and covenant. Neither in the new, for many myriads of men, to whom not even a word concerning the Redeemer either in thesis, or in hypothesis became known. How then shall they call upon him in whom they have not believed? how then shall they believe in him of whom they have not heard? how then shall they hear without a preacher? how then shall they preach, except they be sent? Rom. 10:14,15. II. Because not only verbal calling is by no means destined to all, but neither real, either by subjective light, or by objective light, as appeareth from innumerable infants extinct in cradles among the Gentiles, Jews, Mahometans, outside the covenant of God.

V. Calling Made by God's Word and Spirit

Full and saving calling is made by God’s Word and Spirit.

Professor saith: The manner of calling, considered oppositely, is distinguished into external and internal. The former is performed outwardly through the administration of the Word and Sacraments; the latter inwardly through the operation of the Holy Spirit. The less principal or instrumental cause of faith ordinarily is the Word of faith which is preached, when it is near in our mouth and in our heart, Romans 10:8. For faith cometh by hearing the Word of God, verse 17. And although we willingly grant with Scripture unto the Word of God heard that it is the power of God unto those who are saved, that it is the seed cast into the hearts, that it is a two-edged sword, piercing the soul, 1 Corinthians 1:18; Luke 8:11; Hebrews 4:12, yet we do not assent unto those who attribute this power unto the external preaching of the Gospel, insofar as it striketh the ears, that it properly, and as it were by the work wrought, engendereth faith, and that the operation of the Spirit is included in it. For although it is necessary by precept and prerequisite for adults, yet its external action without the efficacy of the Spirit inwardly revealing the sense of the Gospel, and proposing and sealing it unto the ears of the heart, would not suffice for faith. For neither is he that planteth anything, neither he that watereth, but God that giveth the increase, 1 Corinthians 3:7. Here, therefore, it is to be observed that we do not rush into extremes and tear asunder things to be conjoined, or confound things to be distinguished, by attributing properly and in the same sense unto the instrument that which is of the primary cause, or by seeking the Spirit outside the Word, or by expecting the understanding of the Word, and from the Word understood assent and true confidence, without the internal revelation of the Spirit, from the Word itself or its ministry, and from the light of our intellect.

VI. The Spirit’s Operation is the Adequate and Effective Cause of Conversion

Zuinglius saith: I pray that at least by conjecture, which ye are wont to use, and which are sometimes alien from all reason, ye would answer unto this: Whether it seemeth that Adam would ever of his own motion have returned to seek grace? Ye will be compelled truly to confess that he who so regarded flight and hiding places that he could scarcely be drawn forth, by no conjecture seemeth to have been about to return, unless the Lord had pursued the fugitive. Why, therefore, will ye not acknowledge that acquired faith, concerning which ye speak so much and so greatly, is a fiction (for no man cometh unto Christ, John 6:44, except the Father draw him), and that it is not of him that willeth, nor of him that runneth, but of God that showeth mercy? When ye see the common parent, whose sin and death flowed into us, so to have abhorred from God that, following the folly which said he could lie hid, he hid himself, lest he should be compelled to hear the crime of desertion reproached. But what need is there of many words? Let God leave Adam, he will never return unto Him from whom he fled; let Him leave man, he will never seek Him by whom he was created.

Walaeus saith: The Socinians dare to say, and some of the ex-Remonstrants have formerly followed, that the Word of the Law and Gospel proposed through ministers is the very efficient cause of conversion and regeneration, so that they say the Holy Spirit regenerateth us only by morally acting through persuasions and exhortations, without any other real action, or infusing spiritual qualities. Others indeed acknowledge real infusions, but only into the intellect and affections; but into the will none. Finally, others acknowledge some also into the will, but not such as efficaciously determine the will, but only such as the will can receive or not receive such qualities; yea, that those holy habits are acquired through frequent actions of believing, loving, hoping, etc., as they express themselves in the Epistle to Walach. and elsewhere. But concerning the first question now briefly. We affirm, therefore, that the manner whereby the Holy Spirit converteth and regenerateth us is not only moral, that is, symbouleutikon, as Simplicius speaketh, but truly and in very deed, or, as others speak, by way of physical acting:

  1. Because Holy Scripture expressly attributeth unto the Holy Spirit in our conversion a distinct manner of operating, as John 6, when Christ proposeth the Word equally unto all, of whom many rejected it. Christ saith, verse 44, “No man can come to Me, except the Father which hath sent Me draw him.” And verse 65, “No man can come unto Me, except it were given unto him of My Father.” But now it was given unto them by Christ to hear the Word of the Gospel, and to see miracles, etc.; therefore, it is necessary that another donation and drawing be here understood. So Matthew 16:17, “Flesh and blood hath not revealed it unto thee, but My Father which is in heaven.” Yet the rest also had heard the Word of Christ from His mouth. So speaketh Luke, Acts 16:14, “The Lord opened the heart of Lydia, that she attended unto the things which were spoken of Paul.” Yet many others heard, and yet believed not. Therefore, it is necessary that here some other action be understood. More clearly yet, 1 Corinthians 3:6, “I have planted, Apollos watered; but God gave the increase.” Therefore, the giving of the increase is something other than planting and watering, that is, the preaching of the Word. It is proven from the prayers unto God which are subjoined by the ministers and by the faithful themselves unto the Word, that the Word itself may be efficacious in them. For no man prayeth for that which he hath already received; those who already preach or hear the Word ought to pray that God make it efficacious. Therefore, some further action is required.
  2. From many arguments and examples, as Hebrews 13:21. So David, Psalm 25:4; 119:12. So also the Apostles pray for grace and peace, and faith, charity, patience in the cross, unto the churches.
  3. Because otherwise the conversion of man would rather be ascribed unto the will of man than unto God, since it could either admit or reject the persuasion, against that, “It is not of him that willeth, nor of him that runneth, but of God that showeth mercy,” Romans 9:16, and man would discern himself, against the saying, 1 Corinthians 4:7.

It followeth, therefore, necessarily hence that there is another operation of the Holy Spirit than of the Word only. It remaineth that we inquire whether that operation is such that, all the actions of God which He applieth for the conversion of man being posited, it yet remaineth in the arbitrament of man to be converted or not to be converted, to obey those divine operations or to resist them. The latter say the Remonstrants, with the Socinians, Semipelagians, and some Romanists. Although among the Romanists there are very many who, with Thomas and all the Thomists, and very many others, think the contrary with us, and with Augustine, De Corruptione et Gratia, chapter 14, acknowledge that unto God willing to save, no will of man resisteth. And De Praedestinatione Sanctorum, chapter 8, “This grace is rejected by no hard heart; for therefore it is given that the hardness of the heart be first taken away.” But that the opinion of the Remonstrants and that established in the Synod of Dort of the Reformed Church may be rightly understood, first, the Remonstrants grant that the intellect of man is illuminated by irresistible power in the acknowledgment of Christ and of His benefits, likewise of our duty. Second, that the affections themselves are irresistibly affected through the Word and the Holy Spirit, that he feel the punishment and turpitude of sin, love his conversion, etc., that is, that God so inwardly knocketh upon the affections that the affections cannot but receive and feel this impulse. Third, whence necessarily followeth and the third, which is rightly observed and collected by some from the antecedent, that the will necessarily feeleth the persuasion of the intellect and the inclination of the affections; but yet they except that it is in the faculty of the will to obey that persuasion of the intellect or not to obey, likewise to follow the inclination of the affections or not to follow. Cameron, against Tilenus, that he may refute the Remonstrants from their own principles, inserteth a certain hypothesis, from which he judgeth that he will convince them, and that in this manner: The intellect is illuminated and affected by irresistible power in the conversion of man, from the hypothesis of the Remonstrants; but the will necessarily and irresistibly followeth the illumination and judgment of the intellect; therefore, the will also is necessarily and irresistibly converted or regenerated. The major they posit and grant, the Remonstrants; the minor he endeavoreth to prove by some arguments which he judgeth firm, both taken from nature and from Scripture. We say that man in conversion and first regeneration hath himself merely passively, and that the heart of man is so affected through the grace and efficacy of the Holy Spirit that man neither resisteth, nor willeth to resist, nor can so resist that conversion and regeneration do not actually follow; for we do not take away all power of resisting, because the remnants of the flesh are not all taken away, which are always apt and prone to resist; but the government and domineering power is taken away from it, so that those remnants of the flesh do not overcome in this deed the power and efficacy of grace, but are certainly overcome, and leave behind infallibly the work of regeneration. This is our assertion, which we prove by the following arguments of Holy Scripture:

  1. Because the operation of God in our regeneration floweth from the certain decree of election and eternal purpose, which cannot be hindered. This is proven from very many places of Holy Scripture. But the illustrious places are Romans 8:30, “Moreover whom He did predestinate, them He also called: and whom He called, them He also justified,” not some only, but all altogether. Romans 9:18, “Therefore hath He mercy on whom He will have mercy.” Likewise verse 16, “So then it is not of him that willeth, nor of him that runneth, but of God that showeth mercy.” Likewise Ephesians 1:4, “According as He hath chosen us in Him before the foundation of the world, that we should be holy.” And Romans 11:7, “The election hath obtained it, and the rest were blinded.”
  2. From the manners of speaking whereby this gift of conversion is expressed in Scripture; for it is called regeneration, John 3:5,circumcision of the heart, Deuteronomy 30:6, inscription and writing of the law of God in our hearts, Jeremiah 31:33; Hebrews 8:10. So Ezekiel 36:26, “A new heart also will I give you, and a new spirit will I put within you: and I will take away the stony heart out of your flesh, and I will give you an heart of flesh. And I will put My Spirit within you, and cause you to walk in My statutes.” It is called a new creation, Psalm 51:10. Likewise, resurrection from the dead, John 5:21, the first resurrection, Revelation 20:5, and quickening, Ephesians 2:1, renewing after the image of God, Ephesians 4:23.
  3. Regeneration, faith, hope, charity are said to be given unto us by God. John 6:44, 65, “No man can come to Me, except the Father which hath sent Me draw him.” Acts 5:31, “Him hath God exalted with His right hand to be a Prince and a Saviour, for to give repentance to Israel.” Acts 11:18, “Then hath God also to the Gentiles granted repentance unto life.” So 2 Timothy 2:25-26; Ephesians 2:1; Philippians 1:29; 2:13, “For it is God which worketh in you both to will and to do of His good pleasure,” and elsewhere throughout.
  4. From the divine promises, Jeremiah 31:33, “I will put My law in their inward parts, and write it in their hearts,” “They shall all know Me, from the least of them unto the greatest of them.” Ezekiel 36:26, “A new heart also will I give you, and a new spirit will I put within you: and I will take away the stony heart out of your flesh, and I will give you an heart of flesh. And I will put My Spirit within you, and cause you to walk in My statutes, and ye shall keep My judgments, and do them.” Joel 2:28; Isaiah 44:3, “I will pour water upon him that is thirsty, and floods upon the dry ground: I will pour My Spirit upon thy seed, and My blessing upon thine offspring.” Here He saith, “I am the LORD,” and “He shall call his name Jacob.” But these cannot lack the event.
  5. From manifest places where God testifieth that He so worketh in the elect that a certain effect followeth; as John 6:37, “All that the Father giveth Me shall come to Me.” Ephesians 1:18-19, Paul prayeth that the Ephesians may know “what is the exceeding greatness of His power to us-ward who believe, according to the working of His mighty power, which He wrought in Christ, when He raised Him from the dead.” 2 Corinthians 3:18. So Colossians 2:12, “Buried with Him in baptism, wherein also ye are risen with Him through the faith of the operation of God, who hath raised Him from the dead.” So Acts 16:14, “Whose heart the Lord opened, that she attended unto the things which were spoken of Paul.” That here not only the end is indicated, but also the event, is evident first from the construction; second from the comparison of those who heard and attended not, and in whom God, according to their opinion, had the same end proposed. Therefore, nothing peculiar is said here concerning Lydia. So 1 Corinthians 3:6, “I have planted, Apollos watered; but God gave the increase.” Certainly not God, but man, would give the increase to the Word, if the efficacy depended from himself. John 6:45, “They shall be all taught of God.” 1 John 2:20, “But ye have an unction from the Holy One, and ye know all things.”
  6. From absurdities. For man would have whereof to glory, and whereby to discern himself from himself, against the Apostle, Romans 3:27; 1 Corinthians 4:7.
  7. They themselves acknowledge:
    1. That some are miraculously converted, as the Apostle Paul, the thief on the cross.
    2. That the faithful are illuminated and the affections corrected irresistibly.
    3. That the powers of believing are given irresistibly, etc.

      4. But the powers are faith, or the very seed of faith, whatever they prate here; otherwise faith would be acquired by act alone, and act would be before every principle of habit, against the universal consent of the Church.

First Objection: It is taken from places in which Scripture testifieth that the Jews resisted the Holy Ghost, as Psalm 78:41; Isaiah 63:10; Proverbs 1:23; Matthew 23:37; Acts 7:51, where Stephen reproacheth them that they did always resist the Holy Ghost.

Response: It can be answered particularly that here it is treated of external means and the Word. For he who resisteth the edicts of the magistrates resisteth the magistrates, although he work nothing inwardly. So speaketh God, Zechariah 7:12, “They refused to hearken, and pulled away the shoulder, and stopped their ears, that they should not hear. Yea, they made their hearts as an adamant stone, lest they should hear the law, and the words which the LORD of hosts hath sent in His Spirit by the former prophets.” But if it be signified also the internal action of the Spirit, it is not proven what was controversial, namely, that the regenerating Spirit can be resisted; or whether grace is resistible in those who are converted. For this we deny, lest we attribute the praise of conversion at least in part unto man, which Scripture assigneth wholly unto God. But these places treat only of those who are not converted; but we confess that the grace which is in them, whether internal or external, is resistible, because they truly resist it.

Second Objection: From places where God testifieth that He seriously desireth the salvation of men, whom He reproveth as contumacious; as Psalm 81:13, “Oh that My people had hearkened unto Me!” Matthew 23:37, “O Jerusalem, Jerusalem, how often would I have gathered thy children together, even as a hen gathereth her chickens under her wings, and ye would not!”

Response: And these places treat of those who reject grace; but we confess that it is resistible in them, both external and internal; but they do not prove that that grace is the same as the grace of regeneration; for this is to krinomenon.

Third Objection: From places where sufficiency altogether is expressly contained, as Isaiah 5:4, “What could have been done more to My vineyard?” Ezekiel 12:2, “Son of man, thou dwellest in the midst of a rebellious house, which have eyes to see, and see not.”

Response: Nor do these places pertain to the matter; because in the first place God showeth not what He hath done inwardly, but what outwardly; but it remaineth one thing, namely, that He now chastise them, verse 5, “And now go to, I will tell you what I will do to My vineyard: I will take away the hedge thereof, and it shall be eaten up; and break down the wall thereof, and it shall be trodden down: and I will lay it waste.” In the latter, it is treated of eyes whereby they can behold the judgments of God, and tremble at them, and desist from those peculiar external sins whereby the wrath of God was provoked against the whole nation; therefore, here it is treated of the Israelitish people universally, and of the manifest judgments of the Lord, unto which they attended not, although they saw them with their eyes.

Maccovius saith: Isaiah 5:4, “What could have been done more to My vineyard?” Hence now they infer that the power of God willing to convert the Israelites was impeded. Response: In this place, the Lord, after the manner of men, reproacheth His benefits unto His people, that He brought them out of Egypt, that He gave them the law, and quiet seats in the land of Canaan, that He often admonished them through His prophets, finally, that He applied all those things which, if they had not been of iron, could plainly have moved them. Those who study to conciliate the friendship of men unto themselves indeed move the affections, but (as far as can be) by benefits, prayers, exhortations, signification of love, by which, if nothing is promoted, the fault is with those who would not be bent. Certainly, he who hath applied all these is without blame, and hath performed his parts, that he is thought to have omitted nothing. Therefore, God dealeth with men after the manner of men, and speaketh; but in vain, unless He give us eyes to see, a mind to understand, ears to hear, unless He pour upon our minds a singular light; which all are gifts of God, which they who will that they be common either to all or to certain wicked with the pious seem to have considered the nature of our regeneration too little attentively. Certainly, those who take the place of Isaiah otherwise, whatever they cavil, truly either do injury to the omnipotence of God, which could not bend the mind of His people, or accuse God of lying, exclaiming, “What could I have done?” It is therefore a saying anthrōpopathōs.

Luther saith: Those authorities, “Turn ye unto Me, and I will turn unto you.” Likewise, “Draw near to God, and He will draw near to you.” Likewise, “Seek, and ye shall find.” Likewise, “If ye seek Me, I shall be found of you,” and the like, if it be said that one is of nature, the other of grace, nothing else is affirmed than what the Pelagians said.

VII. Conversion: The Whole Man’s Change from Evil to Good

Conversion is the whole change of the whole man from evil into good, and unto God, in Christ.

Ames saith: The parts of calling are two: the offer of Christ, and the reception of the same, John 1:11-12, “He came unto His own, and His own received Him not. But as many as received Him, to them gave He power to become the sons of God.” By reason of reception, calling is called conversion, Acts 26:20, because all those who obey the divine calling are converted wholly from sin unto grace, from the world unto the following of God in Christ. It is also called regeneration, insofar as by that word the very beginning of the new life, of the new creation, or of the new creature, is often designated in the Scriptures, John 1:13; 3:6; 1 John 3:9. As with respect to the offer, it is properly called calling, insofar as God efficaciously inviteth and draweth men unto Christ, John 6:44.

Walaeus saith: The form of regeneration is the very restoration of the image of God in us, which consisteth in the abolition of the old man and the resurrection of the new man, Ephesians 4:22; Colossians 3:9-10, or conversion from evil unto good, Isaiah 1:16, and from darkness unto light, Acts 26:18. Which action indeed is of God alone, if we regard the first beginning; but if the increase and progress, it is sometimes also attributed unto man, as Romans 12:2, “Be ye transformed by the renewing of your mind.” Colossians 3:5, “Mortify therefore your members which are upon the earth; fornication, uncleanness, inordinate affection.” And Romans 8:13, “For if ye live after the flesh, ye shall die: but if ye through the Spirit do mortify the deeds of the body, ye shall live.” Deuteronomy 10:16, “Circumcidise therefore the foreskin of your heart.” John 15:2, “Every branch in Me that beareth fruit, He purgeth it, that it may bring forth more fruit.” Therefore, with respect to that, these two parts are also rightly attributed unto repentance in our Catechism. This is the general form; but particularly it consisteth in the illumination of the intellect, and the renewal of the will and affections. Concerning the intellect, see Ephesians 4:23, “And be renewed in the spirit of your mind.” Colossians 3:10, “And have put on the new man, which is renewed in knowledge after the image of Him that created him.” Hebrews 8:10, “I will put My laws into their mind, and write them in their hearts.” Ephesians 1:17-18, “That the God of our Lord Jesus Christ, the Father of glory, may give unto you the spirit of wisdom and revelation in the knowledge of Him: the eyes of your understanding being enlightened; that ye may know what is the hope of His calling, and what the riches of the glory of His inheritance in the saints.” Concerning the affections, see Ephesians 4:22, “That ye put off concerning the former conversation the old man, which is corrupt according to the deceitful lusts.” Concerning the will, let these arguments be seen:

  1. From the previous corruption in the will. For whatsoever is spiritually corrupted is renewed and corrected by this spiritual begetting. But that the will of man by nature is corrupted in itself and through itself, Scripture affirmeth in many places. Ephesians 2:3, “And were by nature the children of wrath, even as others.” 1 Peter 4:2-3, “That he no longer should live the rest of his time in the flesh to the lusts of men, but to the will of God. For the time past of our life may suffice us to have wrought the will of the Gentiles.” Whence also the Apostle, Romans 7:19, saith, “For the good that I would I do not: but the evil which I would not, that I do.” And verse 21, “I find then a law, that, when I would do good, evil is present with me.” Showing that he, by the will, which is regenerate, indeed desireth spiritual good; yet because of the remnants of the flesh inhering in him, he doth not so efficaciously and fully will that the effect which he desireth and willeth always follow.
  2. From the nature of the spiritual quality which is introduced through regeneration; for not only charity and confidence in God have their seat in the will, but also original holiness and righteousness. Ephesians 4:24, “And that ye put on the new man, which after God is created in righteousness and true holiness.” But that righteousness is the proper affection of the will, as all Ethicists acknowledge; whence the Jurisconsults define it as the constant will of giving to everyone his own.
  3. The manners of speaking whereby Scripture throughout useth in this matter efficaciously evince the same; for when God is said to circumcise the heart, to take away the hard and stony heart, Ezekiel 36:26, to take away the foreskin of the heart, Romans 2:29, and very many like, they are never explained by anyone of the inferior appetite alone, which man hath in common with beasts, nor can they be immediately understood of the intellect; because no place will be given in Scripture where the intellect is called hard or stony, or the intellect is said to be circumcised, or its foreskin to be taken away. But on the contrary, when Scripture treateth of the intellect, it is wont to call it blind and darkened, and its renewal illumination of the eyes of the mind, inscription of the law in the hearts, taking away of the veil from the hearts, donation of the mind, etc.
  4. From the vows and prayers of the saints, not only that God would illuminate them, but also that He would bend the will unto good. So David, 1 Chronicles 29:18, prayeth, “O LORD God of Abraham, Isaac, and of Israel, our fathers, keep this for ever in the imagination of the thoughts of the heart of Thy people, and prepare their heart unto Thee.” Psalm 119:36, “Incline my heart unto Thy testimonies.” Philippians 2:13, “For it is God which worketh in you both to will and to do of His good pleasure.” Especially since it cannot be that the will, instructed with the powers of nature only, should of itself follow the intellect already affected with a supernatural gift, or that the intellect should of itself impress upon the will some supernatural quality inhering in it; since Scripture everywhere affirmeth that this is of God alone, and that the intellect can no more of itself impress such power upon the will than produce the will itself from itself; but as God created both the faculty of the intellect and of the will in man, so He superaddeth supernatural gifts, both unto this and unto that, through that new creation.

We say that this regeneration is perfect with the perfection of parts, yet not of degrees in this life. But we call the perfection of parts, because it extendeth through all the faculties of the soul, as was seen above. Second, because it extendeth unto all the commandments of God, both with respect to the first and with respect to the second table, as is clear from the antecedents. 1 John 2:4, “He that saith, I know Him, and keepeth not His commandments, is a liar.” 1 John 4:8, “He that loveth not knoweth not God.” And verse 20, “If a man say, I love God, and hateth his brother, he is a liar.” Romans 8:1, “Who walk not after the flesh, but after the Spirit.” See also many other places which give testimony to the saints, that they walked in all the commandments and ordinances of the Lord, and that from the whole heart, as of Hezekiah, 2 Kings 18:3; Isaiah 38:3, of Zacharias and Elizabeth, Luke 1:6, etc. The same teach the prayers of the saints, as of David, Psalm 119:10, etc. Finally, also in this, that sin reign not, but the Spirit, that is, that the gift of regeneration be more robust than the remnants of the flesh, Romans 6:14; 8:13; 1 John 4:4; 5:4.

VIII. The End of Calling: Union with Christ

The end of calling is our union with Christ, and communion.

Ames saith: The parts of application are two: union with Christ, and communion of benefits which flow from that union, Philippians 3:9,“And be found in Him, not having mine own righteousness, which is of the law, but that which is through the faith of Christ, the righteousness which is of God by faith.” This union is that spiritual relation of men unto Christ, whereby they acquire right unto all those blessings which are prepared in Him, 1 John 5:12, “He that hath the Son hath life.” And 3:24, “He that keepeth His commandments dwelleth in Him, and He in him.” This union is made through calling. For calling is the aggregation of men unto Christ, that they may be united with Him, 1 Peter 2:4-5, “To whom coming, as unto a living stone, disallowed indeed of men, but chosen of God, and precious, ye also, as lively stones, are built up a spiritual house.” Ephesians 4:12, “For the perfecting of the saints, for the work of the ministry, for the edifying of the body of Christ.” From which union with Christ followeth union with God the Father, 1 Thessalonians 1:1; 2:1, “Unto the church of the Thessalonians which is in God the Father and in the Lord Jesus Christ.” This, therefore, is the first thing which pertaineth unto the application of redemption, Ephesians 1:7-9. And it is the first thing which constituteth a man actually elect in himself, that is, the first act of election which is exercised or exerted in man himself. Whence also it is that calling and election are sometimes taken in the Scriptures in plainly the same sense, 1 Corinthians 1:26-28. Hence the calling of men in no wise dependeth upon the dignity, probity, industry, or any endeavor of those to be called, but only upon the election and predestination of God, Acts 2:47, “And the Lord added to the church daily such as should be saved.” And 13:48, “And as many as were ordained to eternal life believed.” Romans 8:30, “Moreover whom He did predestinate, them He also called.” Titus 3:5, “Not by works of righteousness which we have done, but according to His mercy He saved us.” James 1:18, “Of His own will begat He us with the word of truth.”

IX. The Means: Spirit and Faith

Trelcatius saith: The internal means of this calling or application are two: the Spirit and faith. The Spirit, who calleth, is the efficacious cause, 1 John 2:27; Ezekiel 11:19. Faith, which answereth unto calling, is by the office of an instrument, Romans 10:14; 2 Corinthians 10:4. But as the donation of the Spirit, so also the bestowal of faith, is the singular gift of God.
